# Mittsu no Hoshi — 三つの星
## The Three Stars

## The Name

**Mittsu no hoshi** (三つの星) means simply "three stars." It is the formal name the boys give themselves — the name that appears in Shin's notebook, that gets invoked in moments of genuine gravity, and that serves as their private acknowledgment that what they are experiencing together is real and significant.

In casual narrative they are simply **the boys.**

---

## The Resonance of the Name

Stars are observed from a distance by something vast and old. Stars are what you navigate by. And Nushi, looking upward from within the earth rather than down from space, might see the boys above it the way we see stars above us — small, distant, bright, worth watching.

The name also connects to **Mitsu boshi** — the three stars of Orion's belt, one of the most familiar constellations to any Japanese child. Ancient, reliable, always found together.

## The Members

| Name | Age | Role |
|------|-----|------|
| Kenji 健二 | 11 | Leader, older brother to Taro |
| Shin 真 | 10 | Intellectual anchor, Kenji's best friend |
| Taro 太郎 | 9 | Chaotic element, younger brother to Kenji |

---

## Group Dynamic

Kenji provides momentum. Shin provides framework. Taro provides chaos that occasionally resolves into exactly what was needed.

The trio functions because each member supplies what the others lack. Kenji acts but sometimes acts without sufficient information. Shin knows but sometimes knows without sufficient courage to move. Taro neither knows nor plans but arrives at things sideways in ways nobody anticipated, including Taro.

---

## The Name in Practice

Taro loves the name unreservedly and uses it at every opportunity, including inappropriate ones.

Kenji accepts it with visible eye-rolling that gradually, over the course of the story, becomes less visible.

Shin uses it seriously and means it completely, which is simultaneously dignified and slightly funny.

The name is slightly too large for three ordinary boys in canvas sneakers. Then, gradually, it isn't.

---

## When They Named Themselves

The naming happens at a specific moment — not casually but deliberately, as a response to the growing awareness that something important is happening to them. Children reach for ceremony when they sense that ordinary life has become something else. The name is their way of making themselves feel equal to what they are experiencing.

The moment of naming is worth a scene. It would be Shin who suggests it, quietly. Taro who immediately and loudly approves. Kenji who says nothing for a moment, looks at the mountain, and then nods once.

---

## The Group's Shared History

- Kenji and Shin's fathers were graduate students together at Tokyo University (Todai) in the late 1950s to early 1960s, connected through an informal cross-departmental study group
- Both families were recruited to Tsukuba Science City within a year of each other — possibly not coincidentally
- Kenji and Shin's friendship felt inevitable rather than accidental from the beginning
- Taro came with Kenji, as Taro always does

---

## Their Territory

The boys range freely through the danchi complex and its surroundings. Their primary territories include:

- The danchi complex itself and its edges
- The drainage channel running along the remnant of an old farm boundary between two research institute buildings — overgrown, ignored by adults, leading toward the lower slopes of Mount Tsukuba
- The lower slopes of the mountain itself
- Construction sites and half-finished buildings in the still-developing science city

They have not yet followed the drainage channel all the way to the mountain. That changes.

---

## What They Are Becoming

Through their contact with Nushi, the boys begin developing extraordinary mental capacities — not dramatic superhero abilities but subtle and cerebral ones. Heightened intuition. The ability to sense each other's emotions. Unusual leaps of understanding. Fragmentary visions.

Each manifests differently. The differences in how their abilities develop will tell us something true about each of them.

---

## Open Questions

- Does Nushi select them deliberately, or does their friendship and complementary natures simply make them the most receptive cluster available?
- When their abilities develop enough that they sense Nushi's presence directly, who reaches out first — them or Nushi?
- Does the group dynamic shift as their abilities develop? Does Shin's intellectual primacy increase? Does Taro's apparently chaotic nature reveal a deeper pattern?
- Is there a moment when the adults notice that these three boys have become something more than childhood friends?
