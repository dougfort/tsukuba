# The Channel — Draft One

---

The gap in the fence was Kenji's find, which meant it was also Taro's, because anything Kenji found was also Taro's by a law that had never been stated and never needed to be.

The fence ran along the back of the second institute building — chain-link, adult-height, with a concrete retaining wall on the other side that came close enough to leave a triangular gap at the bottom. Kenji had been walking the fence line for no reason in particular, which was how he walked most places: systematically, in case something turned up. Something had turned up. He crouched and looked through and saw a concrete apron, a rusted grille, darkness that smelled of wet stone and something green and alive underneath.

He called back without turning around. "There's something here."

Behind him, Shin looked up from what he'd been doing, which was crouching over a beetle on the path and deciding whether it was worth a sketch. It was not.

"Where?"

Taro was already there. Taro was always already there — he moved toward Kenji's voice the way metal moved toward a magnet, a reflex so established that Taro himself no longer noticed it. He dropped to his stomach and stuck his head through the gap and came back up with his face showing the expression that meant he had decided something.

"We can get through," he said.

"We don't know what's in there," Shin said.

"There's something in there," Kenji said, which was different.

Shin considered this. He took off his shoulder bag and held it in front of him and followed Kenji through the gap.

---

The culvert was dark and smelled bad and they passed through it quickly. Beyond it the channel opened up between two runs of chain-link fence strung with signs that had faded past legibility — *something something no trespassing* or possibly *something something construction area*, the official world making its usual claims on places that had clearly been left to their own devices for years. The concrete lining was cracked and stained. A frog regarded them from a ledge of moss and then did not move, which in Taro's experience meant a frog that had decided they were not worth the effort of leaving.

Kenji was already moving ahead. He walked with his hands out from his sides, not quite touching the fence, taking in the dimensions of the place the way he always did when he arrived somewhere new — a physical accounting, almost unconscious, measuring what could be done here and how.

The channel went uphill. You could see that from here: the concrete floor slanting upward, the fences drawing closer on both sides as the vegetation thickened, until about fifty meters ahead it looked less like a path and more like a wall of green.

"Something lives here," Taro said. He said this with complete seriousness. He meant animals.

Shin was looking at the base of the fence, where the concrete met the ground. He crouched and looked more closely. "Horsetail," he said. "That's *tokusa.* It grows in wet places."

"Everything here is a wet place," Kenji said, which was accurate. The air in the channel was cooler and damper than the August air above them. It had a smell — not bad, just present, the smell of water on stone and something older underneath, a mineral quality that was new to all of them.

They moved uphill.

---

The concrete ended without announcing it. One section was cracked, the next half-buried in silt, and then there was no more concrete and the channel was earthen and entirely different: the banks higher and greener, the sky reduced to a strip, the vegetation on both sides gone from merely abundant to overwhelming. Tall grass pressed in from the west bank. On the east, bamboo had colonized heavily enough that the bank itself was no longer entirely visible — just bamboo, closely spaced, the stalks pale green and creaking very slightly in air that should not have had enough movement in it to creak anything.

Kenji stopped.

The feeling of the place was different here. Not wrong — not the feeling of somewhere you shouldn't be, which he had felt in other places and knew how to recognize. More like the feeling of somewhere that had not been organized by anyone's expectations about it. A place that was still entirely itself.

He moved forward.

The channel floor here was soft but not unpleasantly so — the foot sank in slightly and then found purchase, the soil dark and mineral-smelling, a different composition from anything he'd walked on before. Behind him he could hear Shin picking his way carefully and Taro moving with his usual confidence in the wrong footing, which always threatened disaster and never quite produced it.

"How far does it go?" Shin asked.

"Don't know," Kenji said. He kept walking.

---

The widening arrived the way good things did, which was without warning.

The channel expanded — not dramatically, just enough for the three of them to stand abreast rather than single file — and the bamboo on the east side drew back slightly, creating a platform of packed earth at bank height that received what was left of the afternoon light. The west bank had a section where thick miscanthus roots had consolidated into something that was almost a seat, a natural ledge of root and earth just at the right height to sit on with your feet in the channel. The floor here was noticeably firmer and drier than the section below. Above them, through the bamboo fronds, the sky was wider than it had been for the last hundred meters of channel.

They stood in it. All three, without deciding to, stopped moving.

The sound was a surprise. The channel was loud in a way it hadn't seemed from outside: water somewhere nearby, not much of it but cold and continuous, a sound that belonged to a place rather than just being present in it. Insects in the banks. The miscanthus making its papery dry rustling. A frog. These were ordinary sounds, but together, in the particular acoustic enclosure of the channel walls, they added up to something that was not ordinary — a sound that was specifically here, that you would not have found anywhere else.

Shin took off his glasses and cleaned them on his shirt and put them back on, a thing he did when he was looking at something carefully.

Kenji turned slowly, taking in the dimensions. The platform. The seat in the west bank. The firm dry floor. The way the bamboo filtered the light.

Taro sat down on the earth bank. He pressed both palms flat against the ground — not something he'd meant to do, it was just where his hands went — and looked up at the strip of sky and the mountain visible at its edge.

"This," he said, in the tone of a person announcing a discovery of great importance, "is ours."

Kenji looked at the mountain for a moment.

"Okay," he said.

Shin opened his notebook to a new page. He wrote the date in the upper right corner in small neat characters and then looked up at the bamboo profile against the sky and started to draw.

---

They stayed until the light started to change, which in August was later than it had any right to be. On the walk home Taro talked without stopping — plans, improvements, the question of whether you could dam the water and whether you should, the beetles that were certainly living in the bamboo roots if you knew how to look. Kenji half-listened, in the comfortable way of someone used to Taro's running commentary as a form of weather rather than communication.

Shin walked slightly behind them both. In his notebook, below the bamboo sketch, he had written the date, the word for channel (*suiro*, then *dobu* crossed out, then just the character for water-path), and below that: *cool. mineral smell. frog. taro's hands on earth.*

He had written the last item and then looked at it for a moment before closing the notebook. He wasn't sure why he'd written it. Taro put his hands on the ground all the time. It wasn't worth recording.

He kept it anyway.
