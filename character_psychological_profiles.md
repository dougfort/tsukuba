# Psychological Profiles — The Three Boys
## Kenji, Shin, and Taro

*Compiled from character documents, behavioral observations, and notes across all project files. These profiles are intended to go deeper than the character sheets — to describe not just personality traits but the interior logic of each boy, how he processes experience, and how he is likely to respond as the contact with Nushi develops.*

---

# Kenji — 健二
## Age 11 | Leader | Older Brother

---

## The Interior Life

Kenji is the boy who goes first. This is his defining characteristic and it is more complicated than it looks. Going first is not recklessness — it is a considered, if unconscious, stance toward the world. He has assessed, with the speed that comes from having done it constantly, that the unknown is better approached than avoided. The assessment happens below the level of articulation. He simply goes.

His father's profound quiet lives in him. When Kenji is thinking seriously — about something new, something strange, something that won't yet resolve into words — he goes silent in a way that Taro misreads as moodiness and Shin recognizes correctly as processing. His mouth goes slightly open. His whole body orients toward the thing he is thinking about. He is not withdrawn; he is attending.

This quality of attention is one of the things Nushi registers in him as distinct from mere animal boldness. Most creatures approach the unknown with their threat-detection system in the foreground. Kenji approaches it with something more like curiosity in the foreground and threat-detection running quietly in the background — a configuration that is rarer than it sounds.

He runs everywhere rather than walks. This is not excess energy in the hyperactive sense; it is the expression of a mind that is always slightly ahead of where the body currently is, pulling the body after it.

---

## Self-Concept and Social Role

Kenji is eleven and at peak childhood — fully present in it, with no sense yet of its ending. This is an important psychological fact. He is not performing leadership, not managing others' perceptions of him, not protecting a position. He leads because the situation requires someone to go first and he is the one who goes. The role is functional rather than status-oriented.

His relationship to Taro complicates this. He is responsible for Taro in a way he didn't choose and sometimes resents — but the resentment is clean and uncomplicated, the ordinary resentment of an older child who would prefer not to be responsible for a younger one, rather than anything deeper. He loves Taro without being able to say so. The love expresses itself in protective behavior he doesn't acknowledge and occasional flashes of real tenderness he thinks no one notices. Shin notices.

His relationship to Shin is the more intellectually interesting one. He relies on Shin's knowledge and framework without always admitting it — and there is something in this dynamic that is slightly asymmetrical in a way that doesn't create tension because both boys understand it implicitly. Kenji provides the momentum that makes Shin's knowledge useful; Shin provides the framework that makes Kenji's momentum purposeful. Each makes the other more effective. Neither would work as well alone.

The wristwatch from his father — slightly too adult for him, deeply prized — is worth attending to psychologically. It is not primarily a timepiece for Kenji. It is a connection to his father, worn on his body, a token of the kindred-spirit relationship that mostly expresses itself in shared silence and occasional intense conversation. He checks it when he is uncertain, not to know the time, but as a grounding gesture.

---

## How He Processes Difficult Experience

Kenji stores before he speaks. This is the most important thing to know about how he will respond to the contact with Nushi. He will be among the first to notice something unusual — his physical intuition is acute, and the proprioceptive sensations at the spot will register in him strongly. He will say nothing. He will go back to the channel partly to see if it happens again. He will build up a considerable internal record of anomalies before he names one aloud.

When he does speak, it will be sparse and precise. Not *it felt like the ground was paying attention* (that's Taro). Something more like: *the water sounds different here than it does further down.* A statement that is both accurate and deliberately limited, leaving room for the others to confirm or deny without his having committed to a claim he isn't sure about.

His developing abilities, as Nushi's contact deepens, are described in the project documents as likely to manifest as heightened physical intuition — knowing where to go, what to do, which way to move. This is consistent with his psychology: he doesn't receive information in the form of words or images. He receives it in the form of *direction*. A pull toward something, a resistance from something else. His body knows before his mind does. He is already somewhat like this before Nushi touches him.

---

## The Slope Node Incident

Kenji was the first of the boys to physically enter the Slope Node. This is the defining early fact of his relationship with Nushi. He went first, as he always does — and whatever he felt there was significant enough that he does not talk about it, which for Kenji is the highest register of significance. He came home and his dreams were vivid and he went back the next weekend.

His resonance with Nushi's cognitive activity is characterized in the project documents as bold forward motion — he moves toward the presence where others might retreat. What this means psychologically is that when Kenji encounters something vast and unknown, his instinct is not to classify it or explain it but to find out where it goes. He does not need to understand something before he approaches it. Understanding can come after.

---

## Relationships with Adults

His father, Hiroshi, is his primary adult relationship of significance. They communicate in the register that matters most to Kenji: quiet shared attention, occasional intense conversation, the understanding that some things don't need to be said. Hiroshi is inadvertently modeling for Kenji exactly the quality of attention that makes the boys receptive to Nushi — the deep, patient, outward-directed attention of someone who finds the world genuinely worth studying.

His mother watches him with quiet pride and careful attention. Kenji knows this and is comfortable with it. He does not find maternal attention intrusive; he finds it sustaining, the way sunlight is sustaining — something you don't think about but would notice the absence of.

---

## What He Does Not Know About Himself

Kenji does not know that his instinct to go first, which he has never examined because it has never failed him, is itself a cognitive style with specific properties — one of which is that it makes him unusually sensitive to the directional information that Nushi is beginning to provide. He experiences his developing intuition as an extension of something he has always had. He will be the last of the three to frame what is happening as contact with something external. For Kenji, it feels internal — a deepening of his own capacities — until very late in the process.

This will matter for the story. Kenji accepts the name Mittsu no Hoshi with a single nod toward the mountain — but he is processing something the others cannot fully see.

# Shin — 真
## Age 10 | Intellectual Anchor | Keeper of the Notebook

---

## The Interior Life

Shin is ten years old and is already living substantially in his mind. This is not dissociation or avoidance — it is genuine orientation. The world of ideas is, for Shin, as vivid and interesting as the physical world, and considerably more tractable. He finds indoor light perfectly satisfactory. He carries a notebook everywhere. His sneakers remain presentable.

His intelligence is the kind that makes adults pause mid-sentence and reconsider what they were about to say. This has been his experience since early childhood — adults revising their register, adjusting their assumptions, occasionally looking slightly uncomfortable. He has learned to modulate the expression of his intelligence in social situations without suppressing it, which is a more sophisticated skill than most adults have, and he has learned it early out of necessity.

The large round glasses, slightly too big for his face, are not merely corrective lenses. Without them he looks oddly unfinished and vulnerable, which he hates — the self-knowledge in that observation is characteristic. He knows what he looks like without the glasses. He knows what the glasses do for him. He would not say this aloud.

His hands are described in the project documents as slender and almost elegant — the hands of someone who handles books carefully and writes in small precise characters. This physical detail opens onto a psychological one: Shin is careful. Careful with objects, careful with words, careful with other people's feelings in ways that are not immediately obvious because the carefulness is quiet and doesn't draw attention to itself.

---

## Self-Concept and Social Role

Shin's self-concept is organized around knowledge and comprehension. He knows things. He understands things. He arrives at every strange situation with a framework already in place, however imperfect — and this capacity, which makes him invaluable in the trio, is also the thing most likely to be a limitation as the contact with Nushi develops. Frameworks that are almost right can be more obstructive than no framework at all.

He has read every science fiction novel available to a Japanese ten-year-old in 1979. He has strong opinions about what they are probably dealing with based on this reading and is occasionally right for entirely the wrong reasons. This is a perfect description of a specific intellectual style: pattern-matching to existing models, which works brilliantly until the situation is genuinely outside all existing models. Nushi is genuinely outside all existing models. Shin's science fiction reading will take him some of the way and then become a cage.

He knows what Taro's English t-shirt says and whether the grammar is correct. He has never told Taro what it says. This small withheld kindness is worth noting — it is not cruelty (Shin is not cruel) but a decision that some information is better not shared. He makes these decisions more often than his quiet exterior suggests.

---

## The Notebook

The notebook is the primary external expression of Shin's interior life. It is inseparable from him — described consistently as part of his daily equipment along with the glasses and the shoulder bag. What it contains, based on mentions across the project documents:

- Insect observations: sketches, dates, locations, behavioral notes
- Man'yoshu poems about Mount Tsukuba, copied for reasons he cannot fully explain
- A note about a spring mentioned in the Hitachi Fudoki
- A note about the cap stirring — dated, underlined, marked with a question mark — after the third time. By the fifth time he has stopped writing about it.
- A note that says only: *the smell, again.*
- A question: *what is the frequency of attention?*
- An unidentified ground beetle drawn three times from different angles

The notebook is a document of careful attention to the living things of the channel — but it is also, increasingly, a document of the channel's anomalies. The insect records and the anomaly records exist in the same notebook without Shin having decided that they belong to different categories. He has not yet organized what he is noticing into a coherent account. But the material is there.

Shin's notebook will become important. This is stated in the project documents without elaboration. Its importance is probably this: it is a record of the contact in real time, made by the most observant and systematic of the three boys, preserved in a document that can be read by others — including adults.

---

## How He Processes Difficult Experience

Shin processes by writing around things. Direct description of what he cannot yet categorize produces nothing — the pen stops. So he approaches obliquely: a poem that is adjacent to the feeling, a question that circles the phenomenon, a physical detail (the smell, the sound, the specific temperature) that anchors the experience without claiming to explain it.

His hypnagogic experiences, as described in the contact document, have a cognitive quality — the feeling of understanding something completely and clearly that dissolves before he can identify what it was. He wakes from these experiences with a specific frustration: the sense that something was known to him a moment ago and is no longer.

This frustration is new to him. Shin is accustomed to understanding things. The experience of understanding slipping away before it can be grasped is one he has no prior model for, and it bothers him in a way he does not express to the others. He tries harder. He concentrates more deliberately at the spot. He attempts to hold the hypnagogic experience rather than letting it dissolve.

This is exactly the wrong approach, and Nushi is patient. The lineage practitioners spent years learning not to grasp. Shin will have to learn the same thing faster and without instruction.

---

## The Name

Shin says *Nushi* quietly, almost to himself, not even necessarily directing it at the others. It is simply right in a way that stops the conversation.

The psychological mechanism behind this moment is worth examining. Shin has been reading about the Tsukuba mountain's folklore, about nushi as a concept, about the shrine tradition. He has copied Man'yoshu poems into his notebook without fully knowing why. He has been, in a diffuse and unorganized way, gathering the components of a framework that he hasn't yet assembled.

When the word arrives, it does not feel like a thought he is having. It feels like a recognition — the experience of finding the right word for something you already knew but couldn't name. This is also, as the contact history document notes, possibly Nushi's first deliberate act of communication: placing the right word in the most receptive mind.

Shin cannot distinguish between these two possibilities. He does not know whether he found the word or the word found him. This uncertainty, which he holds carefully and does not share, may be the beginning of a more fundamental uncertainty about the boundary between his own thoughts and what Nushi is beginning, very carefully, to introduce into them.

---

## Developing Abilities

Shin's abilities, as Nushi's contact deepens, are described as likely to manifest as deepened perception and comprehension — understanding things whole rather than piece by piece. This is the amplification of what he already does, taken to a qualitative extreme: not faster pattern-matching but a different mode entirely, in which the pattern is present all at once rather than assembled sequentially.

This will terrify him briefly before he accepts it. Shin's sense of himself is organized around the sequential, careful, notebook-mediated process of understanding. Understanding that arrives whole and complete, without the process, will feel like something is being done to him rather than something he is doing. He will resist it. He will eventually stop resisting.

---

## What He Does Not Know About Himself

Shin does not know that his intellectual carefulness — the quality that makes his mind briefly legible to Nushi in a way adult minds are not — is precisely what makes him the most powerful conduit of the three. Adult minds are cluttered with frameworks, with certainty, with knowledge that forecloses rather than opens. Shin's frameworks are extensive but held lightly, because he is ten and the world is still genuinely surprising to him. His coherence during reading is not despite his youth but because of it: the combination of systematic attention and genuine openness that is very rare in any mind and nearly impossible to maintain past a certain age.

He will understand what is happening before the others do. He will name it correctly. He will also be the last to fully trust it.

# Taro — 太郎
## Age 9 | Chaotic Element | Younger Brother

---

## The Interior Life

Taro is nine and is entirely, completely, without reservation, himself.

This is rarer than it sounds and more significant than it appears. Most children moderate their self-expression in response to social feedback — they learn what is welcome and what is not, they develop a social self that differs from the private one. Taro has received the same social feedback as everyone else and has not particularly modified his behavior in response. Not because he is impervious to feeling — he is not — but because the gap between what he thinks and what he says is so small as to be functionally nonexistent.

This is simultaneously his most social liability and his most important perceptual asset. He says what he notices immediately, in the wrong words, with complete confidence, and is surprisingly close to accurate. *It feels like the ground is paying attention. It's like when you think someone is looking at you and you turn around and they are.* These descriptions are not sophisticated. They are not filtered through any framework. They are direct transcriptions of sensation, and they are often the most accurate descriptions available.

Nushi finds Taro's apparent randomness mathematically interesting — like a fractal that reveals deeper order the longer you look. What this means psychologically is that Taro's cognitive style, which appears chaotic from outside, has its own internal coherence that operates below conscious awareness. He notices patterns without knowing he is noticing them. His stubbornness — which appears to be a flaw until it suddenly becomes exactly what is needed — is often the stubbornness of a pattern-recognizer who has registered something that he cannot articulate but is not willing to abandon.

---

## Physical Self-Concept

Taro is small for his age and deeply aware of this. Everything about him is slightly asymmetrical in an endearing way — the gap-toothed smile higher on the left, one ear fractionally higher than the other, the scar through his left eyebrow from the incident the family doesn't discuss without sighing. His hair never lies flat. His randoseru is slightly too large for him, giving the impression of a backpack wearing a small boy.

He is deeply aware of the four-inch height difference between himself and Kenji and considers it a personal injustice. This is not an obsessive grievance — it is more like an ongoing minor complaint that he registers and moves on from, the way you move on from weather. He cannot keep up physically with the older boys. He absolutely refuses to accept this fact. This stubbornness is what keeps him in the territory and present for the contact — his unwillingness to be left behind means he is there for everything.

The red baseball cap is absolutely filthy and present in roughly half his waking hours. Its stirring without wind is the first anomaly the boys all notice. Taro's response to the stirring is to reach up and feel the cap move, look around for the source of the wind, fail to find it, and then continue whatever he was doing. He has done this enough times now that the motion is automatic: cap stirs, hand goes up, look around, nothing, continue. He has not stopped to examine why he does not find this more disturbing. He will, eventually.

---

## Self-Concept and Social Role

Taro's self-concept is organized around presence and belonging. He is here. He is part of this. He chose this spot — he takes personal credit for the channel, for the territory, for the specific pleasantness of the spot where the ground stays dry. The fact that the pleasantness of the spot has an external cause he doesn't know about does not undermine this; from his perspective, his good judgment led them to a good place, which is accurate even if the causal chain is not what he imagines.

He idolizes Kenji completely while driving him crazy with equal completeness. This is a precise description of a specific sibling dynamic: the younger child who has organized his sense of direction around an older sibling, who follows that sibling everywhere, and whose intrusions are simultaneously unwelcome and essential. The trio would not be the trio without Taro — he provides the chaos that occasionally resolves into exactly what was needed — and Kenji, who sometimes resents Taro's presence, would be less effective without him.

His relationship with Shin is mutually tolerant and more affectionate than either of them acknowledges. Taro finds Shin funny in ways he doesn't fully understand; Shin finds Taro funnier than he lets on. There is a specific comedic structure to many of their interactions — Taro says something wrong that is somehow right; Shin notes the accuracy without confirming it — that both of them play to without having arranged it.

---

## How He Processes Difficult Experience

Taro processes out loud, immediately, with the wrong words.

He says *it feels like the ground is paying attention* and means it completely and does not follow up with analysis or qualification. The statement is made, it is accurate, and he moves on. When the others don't engage with it, he lets it go — not because he is uncertain about what he felt, but because his uncertainty threshold for remarkable experiences is simply different from theirs. Strange things happen. He notices them. He says something. He continues.

His hypnagogic experiences are the most vivid and the least analyzable. Intense physical sensations, a feeling of enormous friendly weight below him, the sound of water much louder than the channel actually is. He sleeps through them rather than waking, and in the morning he remembers them as good dreams about the channel.

The good-dream quality is important. Taro does not experience the early contact as unsettling. He experiences it as pleasant, as confirmation that the channel is a good place, as consistent with his view of the world as a place that contains interesting things that he is well-positioned to encounter. His lack of a sophisticated framework for what is happening turns out to be an advantage: he has no model that the experience contradicts, so the experience requires no defensive processing.

---

## The Mountain Card

Taro chose the mountain card from the secondhand shop himself, from a box of mixed old menko. His mother doesn't remember him asking for it specifically. He cannot explain why he wanted it. He just did.

The card is in his tin with the bent corner, which lives in his randoseru, which comes to the channel because the channel is where menko is played. When the mountain card begins to behave oddly — winning more than it should, appearing faceup when it should appear facedown — Taro will be the first to notice and the first to speak.

What he will say will not be a hypothesis. It will be an observation, stated as fact, about what the card is doing. The others will test it. Taro will be right. He will take considerable satisfaction in this.

---

## The Scar

The scar through his left eyebrow has a story. The family sighs when it comes up.

This detail has never been elaborated in the project documents, and possibly should not be. Its function is to establish that Taro has a history of events that produce sighing in adults — a history consistent with his character. The scar is the physical record of a nine-year-old who goes places and tries things without sufficient consideration of structural integrity. It is also, perhaps, the record of a specific incident that Kenji was present for and that Taro does not hold against him, which is one of the small facts that reveals the depth of his love for his brother without either of them needing to say so.

---

## Developing Abilities

Taro's developing abilities are described as potentially the most surprising of the three. What looks like chaos may be a form of pattern recognition operating below conscious awareness. This is almost certainly what it is: his fractal quality, the mathematical interest Nushi takes in his apparent randomness, suggests that Taro's intuitive pattern recognition is going to be amplified rather than replaced. He will not suddenly become systematic. He will become *more Taro* — more accurate in his immediate unfiltered responses, more reliably right about things he cannot explain being right about.

The specific manifestation may be something like: Taro knows things are about to happen before they do. Not in a dramatic precognitive sense but in the ordinary sense of pattern completion — he has registered enough of the pattern that his nervous system fills in the next beat before it arrives. He will experience this as the world simply cooperating with him more than it usually does, which he will accept without much examination because the world has always cooperated with him more than seems reasonable.

---

## What He Does Not Know About Himself

Taro does not know that his immediate, unfiltered, wrong-worded descriptions of sensation are the most accurate reports generated by any of the three boys. He speaks before his mind has processed what he felt, which means he speaks before his mind has had the chance to substitute a framework for the raw experience. *It feels like the ground is paying attention* is a more accurate description of what is happening than anything Shin's more sophisticated processing has yet produced.

He does not know that Nushi finds him the most mathematically interesting of the three boys. He would be delighted by this if he knew it, without fully understanding what it meant. He would take personal credit for being interesting.

He would not be entirely wrong.

# The Three Together

The gestalt quality of the trio is more than the sum of its parts, and the psychological profiles illuminate why.

Kenji moves toward experience before he understands it. Shin understands experience before he can trust it. Taro experiences experience without processing it at all, and reports it accurately for exactly that reason.

In the contact with Nushi, these three modes are complementary in a way that no single mode would be. Kenji's forward motion means they keep going back. Shin's framework-building means the accumulating experience eventually becomes coherent. Taro's unfiltered reporting means nothing significant is lost in the gap between experience and articulation.

Nushi, which has spent a thousand years working with minds that were extensively trained and carefully prepared, is encountering for the first time three minds that approach it with no preparation at all — but whose combination constitutes, accidentally, something very like the optimal receiving apparatus. The courage to approach. The capacity to understand. The inability to filter.

The name Mittsu no Hoshi fits them precisely. Three stars: distinct, distant from each other in their natures, navigated by something vast that looks up at them from below.
