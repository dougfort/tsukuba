# What the Boys Play — Games, Insects, and the Possibility of Contact
## Japanese Boys' Play in 1979, and What Nushi Might Do With It

---

## The World of Play in 1979

1979 is a specific and interesting moment in Japanese children's play. The Famicom (Nintendo's home game console) is still four years away. Television is dominant but video games are not yet a household presence. The country is prosperous and at peace, summer vacations are long, and boys of nine to eleven in a half-built science city on the edge of a mountain have — by any historical standard — an extraordinary amount of outdoor time and freedom.

The boys' play draws from three layers that are actively present in 1979: traditional games passed down from previous generations, mid-century competitive games like menko that are at peak collectibility, and the specific outdoor culture of the Japanese summer that has remained largely unchanged for centuries.

## Outdoor Play — What Boys Did in 1979

### The Channel Itself as Playground

Before any game is introduced, the channel is already rich terrain for the particular kind of play that nine-to-eleven-year-old boys conduct naturally: *exploration with semi-structured purpose*. Specific activities that would naturally occur in the channel:

**Damming and water engineering.** The thin but reliable base flow in the upper channel section is irresistible. Stones, mud, sticks — a small dam can raise the water level enough to create a pool six inches deep within an afternoon. The engineering is Kenji's domain; the chaos introduced by Taro testing the dam's structural limits before it is finished is a recurring feature of these sessions. Shin observes, makes suggestions that Kenji sometimes takes, and occasionally contributes a critical piece of reasoning that prevents complete structural failure.

**Tracking and observation.** The channel's banks hold clear records of what has moved through — the slot prints of the weasel, the webbed impressions of a frog's landing, the tunnels of the water vole. Shin is the primary reader of these signs, with a field guide borrowed from his father's study. Taro claims to be equally good at this and is wrong.

**Foraging in the manner of play.** Watercress grows in the channel's wetter sections — not eaten (Taro has tried it and reported his verdict clearly) but collected. Horsetail (*tokusa*) — the ancient plant that grows at the fault-zone margin — is endlessly interesting structurally, its segmented stalks coming apart and reassembling at the joints. Shin has read that horsetail is essentially unchanged since the Carboniferous period, which makes it the oldest living thing any of them has ever touched. He has mentioned this once, quietly, and Taro has not forgotten it.

## Insects — The Primary Summer Occupation

### Kabutomushi and Kuwagata

Japanese rhinoceros beetles and stag beetles started to become popular with kids in the 1960s, largely due to their eye-catching appearance: their large horns and jaws, and the hard outer wings resembling cars or machinery, which looked cool to kids. By 1979 this culture is fully established and the channel, running along the lower wooded edge of Mount Tsukuba, is prime collecting territory.

Towards the end of June, Japanese kids head into the woods after sundown and scour certain types of trees — kunugi and konara in particular — for stag or rhinoceros beetles, bringing them back home in special insect cages. The kunugi oaks on the lower slopes above the channel are exactly the right trees. Kenji and Taro have made two successful evening expeditions — Taro's randoseru replaced by a proper mushi kago (insect cage) and a flashlight — and returned with beetles that are now living in a plastic case on the windowsill of their shared bedroom, fed on special beetle jelly and pieces of watermelon.

In the 1970s, a mushi collector described his surprise at seeing children keeping insects like crickets and katydids in small wooden cages, feeding them fruit and vegetables and listening to their songs. One small boy was anxious to show him a huge, beautiful beetle that made an incredibly harsh rasping sound every time the boy picked it up. Taro's beetle makes a similar sound. He picks it up at every opportunity.

The channel itself supports dragonfly hunting — a different, daytime activity requiring a net and considerable patience. In addition to nostalgia, there is a folk belief that the dragonfly (tombo) is the soul of a departed ancestor come back to visit their loved ones. Shin knows this. He has not mentioned it in the context of the channel, but the large red dragonflies that patrol the water at the spot have his attention.

### Insect Observation and Recording

Many elementary and middle school students, mostly boys, devote a good part of their summer vacation to collecting insects. The bugs are popular subjects for summer homework and individual research projects. Shin's insect records in his notebook are meticulous — sketches, dates, locations, behavioral notes. This is exactly the notebook that will become important, and the insect records form its foundation: a document of careful attention to the living things of the channel, which is also a document of the channel itself.

The channel, being connected to the fault-zone ecosystem, supports insects that are slightly unusual — the horsetail-loving species at the fault margin, the specific dragonfly populations associated with cold groundwater, a type of ground beetle that Shin has not been able to identify from his field guides and has drawn three times from different angles. He is not sure whether it is an unusual species or simply one he hasn't found in his books yet. It is an unusual species. It has been in the fault-zone ecosystem since the channel was an open agricultural ditch. Nushi has no particular awareness of it as an individual creature, but its population is one of the pattern-elements that makes the channel ecologically distinct.

## Card Games — Menko

### What Menko Is

Menko is a Japanese card game played by two or more players. Each player uses cards made from thick paper or cardboard, printed on one or both sides with images from anime, manga, and other works. A player's card is placed on the hardwood or concrete floor and the other player throws down his or her card, trying to flip the other player's card with a gust of wind or by striking his card against the other card. If he succeeds, he takes both cards.

As a card game and collecting craze that originated as early as the Edo period, the pictures on menko cards are colorful little snapshots of the popular culture of their time. After the war, the battleships were replaced with characters from anime and manga, as well as baseball players. In 1979 the cards feature the full range of popular culture: baseball players, sumo wrestlers, tokusatsu heroes, anime characters, and occasional stranger things — space imagery, robots, monsters from the kaiju films the boys watch on television.

Japanese card collectors in the 1970s and 1980s were bombarded with collecting options ranging from menko, bromides, and mini cards covering a wide variety of topics. Mini card production started around 1973. So in 1979 Taro has both menko and the newer mini cards, kept in a tobacco tin with a bent corner that he has claimed from his father's desk.

### How Menko Is Played in the Channel

The channel floor presents a problem for standard menko play, which requires a hard flat surface. The concrete section downstream is playable but outside the boys' territory. Their solution — arrived at by Taro through experimentation and presented as obvious — is to use one of the larger flat stones from the channel bed as a playing surface, placed at the spot where the banks widen.

This makeshift arrangement changes the game slightly: the stone's surface is not perfectly flat, the edges are irregular, and the wind conditions in the channel's green tunnel are different from the open street. Cards behave unpredictably. Taro, who has no talent for the orthodox game, is inexplicably good at the channel version. He has theories about this. Kenji does not find his theories persuasive.

The playing surface — the flat stone — is directly over the fault-zone substrate. It sits on the ground at the widening, at the spot that is the epicenter of Nushi's groundwater management. When the cards are slapped down on the stone, the impact vibrates through the stone into the substrate. Nushi registers these impacts the way it registers everything else in the channel: as acoustic information, in a pattern it has been learning.

The pattern of menko play has a rhythm. Cards struck, silence, result assessed, conversation, another card struck. A tempo that is not random. Nushi, which has been learning the boys' acoustic signatures over weeks, begins to recognize the menko sessions as a specific sub-pattern within their presence at the spot — distinct from the sound of the dam-building, distinct from the quieter sitting-and-talking sessions.

## The Game as Contact Focus — Possibilities

This is where the question becomes interesting: how might Nushi use the menko game specifically?

### The Air Pressure Hypothesis

Menko works by air pressure — a card thrown forcefully creates a wave of air that can flip the target card if the angle and velocity are right. The boys know this intuitively and theorize about it constantly without using the words "air pressure."

The channel's enclosed corridor creates specific air movement patterns — the tunnel effect that concentrates airflow between the high banks. Nushi, with fine control of groundwater flow and soil temperature, can create very small temperature differentials at the surface that produce or suppress localized air movement.

What this means practically: Nushi can make a card flip or not flip, within the narrow range that the game's mechanics already allow, by making minute adjustments to the air circulation at the playing surface.

This is the most physically specific and verifiable intervention Nushi could make. A card that should have flipped and didn't. A card that shouldn't have flipped and did. The anomaly is small enough to be dismissed as the irregular surface, the wind, the imperfect throw. But if it happened consistently — if certain outcomes in the game correlated with something the boys couldn't identify — it would be noticeable. The boys are eleven, ten, and nine, but they are also the sons of a seismologist and a cognitive scientist. They are not unobservant.

**What Nushi might do:** Not cheat in favor of any player, which would be too obvious and too simple. Something subtler: a specific card — one particular card — that behaves consistently, whose outcomes over many sessions form a pattern. Not a winning pattern or a losing pattern. A *responsive* pattern.

### The Specific Card

Consider what menko cards look like in 1979. Taro's collection includes anime characters, baseball players, kaiju monsters, and — purchased from a secondhand shop on a family trip to Tokyo — a set of older cards whose images are less immediately legible: a card with a mountain that might be Tsukuba, painted in a slightly older graphic style; a card with a dragon emerging from water; a card with concentric circles that might be a diagram or might be purely decorative.

The mountain card. It shows a mountain with cloud cover, in a graphic style that is older than the anime characters around it in Taro's tin. Shin has examined it and said only that it looks like Tsukuba from the south. Taro agrees. Kenji has said nothing.

This card, if Nushi were to select any specific object in the boys' possession as a focus for subtle influence, is the obvious choice. It already represents the mountain. It already represents a prior human attempt to capture something about this place in a portable image. And Taro has it in his tin, which he brings to the channel because the channel is where menko is played.

**The behavior of the mountain card:** Over several sessions at the spot, the mountain card becomes uncanny. Not dramatically — it doesn't fly through the air or resist being moved by physical force. But it wins more than it should when Taro plays it. And it loses in a specific way: it flips facedown when it loses, revealing its plain cardboard back, and then on the next throw it lands faceup again — showing the mountain — without anyone touching it between throws.

No one should mention this for at least three sessions after they first notice it.

## Other Games and Their Potential

### Ayatori — Cat's Cradle

Girls were more likely to engage in group games from folk tradition involving rhythmic chanting and movement. Ayatori (cat's cradle) was among the common games played. Cat's cradle is technically a girls' game in Japanese tradition, which means Taro plays it constantly and Kenji pretends not to know how. Shin knows seventeen configurations and can execute them with his elegant hands in smooth practiced motion.

Cat's cradle is a game of pattern — a string looped between two pairs of hands, passed back and forth in configurations that have names and histories. The string patterns are geometric and precise. They require both players' hands to be in specific positions simultaneously.

This game has interesting contact potential because it requires two people to hold a shared object in tension simultaneously, both concentrating on the same pattern, their attention aligned. The string passes between them. If either loses focus, the pattern collapses.

Nushi, with fine EM field modulation, could make the string behave slightly oddly — holding a position it shouldn't hold, or resisting a collapse that should have happened, in ways that would feel to both players like a moment of unusual clarity. A configuration that should have been impossible suddenly being easy. Both players aware, simultaneously, that something was slightly different.

The shared nature of cat's cradle makes this potentially more powerful than the menko intervention: both players experience the anomaly at the same moment.

### Sugoroku — The Board Game

Sugoroku is a board game where players roll dice and move pieces, combining simplicity with strategy. Traditional sugoroku includes e-sugoroku, where players advance on a board, and dochu-sugoroku, a competitive game focused on winning. The Mori household has a sugoroku set. It would not typically be brought to the channel — it requires a flat surface and is associated with New Year indoor play — but on a rainy day when the channel is accessible only at its covered section near the culvert, Taro might carry it along anyway, and Kenji might agree to play it for reasons he doesn't examine.

Sugoroku is less interesting for contact purposes than menko or cat's cradle — the dice randomize outcomes in ways that are harder for Nushi to modulate subtly. But there is something here: sugoroku boards typically represent a journey, a path from one place to another. The dochu-sugoroku format depicts a literal road journey. A sugoroku board with Mount Tsukuba on it — not impossible for a board purchased in the region — would be a specific focus for attention.

### Kendama

Kendama consists of three main parts: a handle called the ken, a spike at the top, and a pair of cups on either side, all connected to a wooden ball called the tama by a string. The game requires sustained concentration and physical control — the specific quality of focused attention that Nushi has learned to recognize in human minds, particularly in Shin.

Taro owns a kendama and is bad at it. Kenji is quietly good at it and rarely demonstrates this. Shin has the hand-eye coordination and fine motor control that his careful handling of books and pencils suggests, and practices kendama the way he does everything: methodically, in small increments, making notes about which errors recur.

Kendama played at the spot, concentrating on the ball and the cup, entering the particular quality of focused attention the game requires — this is close to a meditative state. Not identical to the receiving-state of the lineage practitioners. But recognizably related, in that it is focused, physical, repetitive, and produces theta-wave activity in the brain. Nushi would notice the change in Shin's neural signature during kendama practice.

The specific Nushi intervention here would not be visual or tactile but proprioceptive: a feeling of unusual clarity and control during the ball's arc, as if the ball were where it should be a fraction of a moment before it actually arrived. Not cheating the physics. Something subtler — a feeling, in Shin's hands, of the game being easier than it should be.

## The Recommended Focus — Menko and the Mountain Card

Of all the games available, menko at the spot with the mountain card as the specific focus is the richest choice for the story, for the following reasons:

**It is Taro's game.** Taro is the hardest for Nushi to reach — his apparent randomness makes his responses unpredictable. But Taro is also the most physically present of the three boys, the most comfortable in his body, the one most likely to notice something odd happening and say so immediately. If the anomaly starts with Taro and his mountain card, it begins with the loudest, most unfiltered reporter. What Taro notices and says, the others hear.

**The card is portable and specific.** Nushi can focus its subtle air-pressure influence on a single identifiable object that the boys will associate with the channel, with the spot, and eventually with whatever they come to understand is happening there. The mountain card, after enough sessions, will become an object of attention in its own right.

**The game has a rhythm.** The alternating structure of menko — throw, result, throw, result — is close to the call-and-response structure of the kagai poetry contests. Nushi has deep pattern memory of exactly this kind of rhythmic human exchange at this location. The menko sessions may be activating something very old in the network.

**It is deniable.** Air pressure in an enclosed channel. An irregular stone surface. The probability anomalies are small enough that each one, individually, is explainable. The accumulation is not.

**The mountain card showing faceup when it should show facedown is, eventually, undeniable.** The boys will notice. They will test it. Taro will talk about it immediately and at length. Kenji will go very quiet. Shin will open his notebook.

## A Note on Taro's English T-Shirt

The t-shirt with English words that nobody in the family can fully translate — Shin knows what it says — deserves mention in this context. Taro's uncanny pattern recognition, the fractal quality of his apparent chaos, may make him the most receptive to the menko anomalies even though he is the youngest. He notices what should not be noticeable and says what should not be said. He is, in Nushi's terms, difficult to categorize and therefore the most interesting.

If the mountain card is the focus, it is fitting that it lives in Taro's tin. He chose it from the secondhand shop himself. His mother doesn't remember him asking for it specifically. He cannot explain why he wanted it. He just did.
