# Part One, Chapter Eight — Draft One

---

The first time they went past the usual point, Kenji didn't announce it. He simply didn't stop.

The usual point was a section of the upper channel where the bank on the east side had partially slumped toward the floor, creating a narrow passage that required some care and that Taro reliably misjudged, requiring Kenji to reach back a hand. They had always stopped somewhere near here on the earlier summer's visits, the slump creating a natural limit that nobody had formally agreed to observe. Today Kenji went through the passage without pausing and kept walking.

Taro went through behind him, didn't need the hand, and didn't mention this.

Shin followed, picking his way. He noted in his notebook that evening: *upper section — went further. Bank slump passage. Channel continues.*

---

What continued was different.

The channel above the slump had a character distinct from the section they knew. Not dramatically different — it was still the same channel, still the same earthen banks and overgrown margins — but the difference was perceptible in the way that the difference between shallow and deep water is perceptible before you can see the bottom. The bamboo on the east bank had given way to a denser mixture of brush and small trees that Shin identified as typical of fault-zone margins. The west bank was higher here, the earth darker and damper, and the horsetail grew not in stands but in continuous dense masses along the base of both banks, filling the wet margins and pressing in from the sides.

The smell was different. Shin had been noting the mineral quality of the channel's smell since the first visit, the thing he had written as *something else — older* in the August 3 entry and had not improved upon in any subsequent entry. Here it was the same smell but stronger — not intensified exactly, more like: concentrated. As if the smell had been diffusing downhill through the whole length of the channel and this was where it came from.

The air was cold in a way that the lower channel was not. Not the relative coolness of shade in summer, which was what the bamboo section provided. This was a different cold — deeper, arriving from below, the temperature of the groundwater itself expressing through the surface of the earth. Kenji pushed his sleeve back and felt the temperature on his forearm and found it measurably different from the air ten meters below.

He kept walking.

---

On the second session Taro walked ahead of Kenji for the first time in anyone's memory.

This was not something he decided. He arrived at the slump passage first because he had been moving faster than usual through the lower sections, with a quality of purpose that neither Kenji nor Shin had witnessed in him before at the channel. Not urgency — Taro never moved with urgency, which was a source of occasional frustration — but something more like intention. He went through the slump passage and was already in the upper section when Kenji arrived.

He had stopped about thirty meters above the slump, at a place where the channel floor widened slightly and a large stone had been exposed in the east bank — grey and old, its surface worn smooth in a way that suggested great age, tilted at an angle that suggested the fault's long history of movement. He was crouching in front of it with both palms on its surface, which was the gesture he had been making at the widening for months, but here his face had a different expression — more focused, less habitual.

Kenji came up behind him and looked at the stone. It was clearly old — older than the channel, older than the farm boundary the channel had followed, the kind of old that was geological rather than historical. The fault had brought it up from somewhere below. It wore its age plainly.

"Good stone," Taro said, not looking up.

"Yes," Kenji said.

He crouched beside Taro and put his own palms on the stone. The cold came through immediately — not the ambient cold of the air but a specific cold, precise, the temperature of something that had been insulated from the seasons by depth and had only recently been brought to the surface. The stone felt, under his hands, more present than stones usually felt. He couldn't account for this. He held his palms against it for a moment and then stood up.

Shin had arrived and was examining the stone from the other side, notebook out, sketching its approximate dimensions and the angle of tilt. He noted the rock type — something in the grey-green range, heavily foliated, unfamiliar to him, definitely not the local sedimentary surface rock. He wrote: *fault zone uplift — different geological layer. Much older. How much?*

He didn't know how much. He wrote a question mark.

---

On the third session they went further still.

The channel above the old stone continued for another hundred meters before the banks finally lowered enough that the channel was no longer a corridor but a shallow depression in the hillside, the distinction between channel and slope becoming unclear. The vegetation changed again — the horsetail thinning where the slope began to dominate, replaced by the rough grasses of the mountain's lower approach.

They stopped where the channel effectively ended, at the point where the ground ahead was unambiguously slope rather than channel. The mountain was visible above them from here in a way it wasn't visible from the lower sections — not the distant shape above the rooftops that Shin had been watching all year, but the actual mountain, close, its lower body right there, the treeline ascending toward its peak. The kunugi oaks of the beetle expedition were off to their left. Above the oaks the mountain continued, densely wooded, rising toward whatever was above the treeline.

Kenji looked up at it. He had spent two summers approaching this view incrementally — first the danchi rooftops, then the channel's bamboo frame, then the open slope from the beetle trees — and now he was close enough to see the individual tree shapes rather than the mass of them. He stood and looked for a long time.

Taro sat down on the earth at the channel's end where the slope began. He crossed his legs and put his palms on the ground in the by-now automatic gesture. He looked at the mountain.

After a while he said, not to anyone in particular: "How old is this place?"

Shin was crouching at the channel's end where the horsetail grew thickest, examining the transition from channel floor to slope substrate. He looked up.

"The channel was made for farming," he said. "Maybe two hundred years, maybe more. The farmers dug it along the field boundary."

"Not the channel," Taro said. "The place. The whole thing."

Shin looked at the horsetail. He looked at the old stone behind them, visible at the edge of his vision. He looked at the mountain above.

"The fault has been here for millions of years," he said. "Longer. The rock in the east bank — the tilted grey stone — that's much older than the surface rock. The fault brought it up from deep down." He paused. "Before there were people. Before there were mammals. Before there were most of the animals that exist now."

Taro looked at the ground under his palms.

"How much older?" he said.

"I don't know exactly," Shin said. "Hundreds of millions of years."

Taro was quiet for a moment. He looked at his hands on the ground, as he often looked at his hands — with the mild curiosity of someone checking on something that might have changed while he wasn't paying attention.

"Huh," he said.

He said it in the tone he used for things that settled at whatever level they settled at and didn't require further processing right now. But Shin watched him and saw that it wasn't dismissal. Taro was sitting with hundreds of millions of years the same way he sat with the good stone and the cold air and the channel's warmth in the dry afternoons — letting it be real without insisting on explaining it.

Kenji had not moved. He was still looking at the mountain.

---

On the walk home that day the three of them were quieter than usual. Not the silence of something unresolved — the silence of something settling. Taro's question had done something to the scale of the afternoon that all three of them had felt. Not frightening. More like: the world was larger than usual, and the usual size felt slightly cramped by comparison.

Shin walked with the notebook under his arm, not open. He had written what he could. The fault's age. The rock type. The question mark. There were other things he might have written — the quality of the cold, the way Taro had looked at his hands, the mountain at that distance — but he had written enough. Some things wanted the notebook and some things wanted to settle on their own terms, and he had learned, this second summer, to know the difference.

Behind him Taro was still thinking about it. He walked with his hands in his pockets and his head slightly down and the expression that meant he was not done with something. Hundreds of millions of years. He was doing something with the number — not calculating, not imagining, something more like testing the weight of it. Seeing how much it changed.

It changed quite a lot.

Kenji walked ahead of both of them. He had checked his watch on the slope and checked it again now and both times it had said what it said, which was the time, which was less surprising than it had felt to be there. He put it back on his wrist. He thought about the mountain at that distance. He thought about the channel running down from the slope through the science city through the culvert and out the other side, the whole length of it connecting the danchi to the mountain, the mountain to the danchi. He had known this in one sense since the August they had first found the gap in the fence. He knew it in a different sense now.

He said nothing. He walked home.
