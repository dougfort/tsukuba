# Nushi's Knowledge of Humans — The Contact History
## The Shrine Lineage, the Kagai, and What Nushi Knows by 1979

*How a four-billion-year-old distributed intelligence came to understand the interior of human consciousness — and what it still does not know.*

---

## Overview

By 1979, Nushi does not encounter human interiority for the first time through the boys. It has been in sustained, deepening contact with specific human minds for well over a thousand years, through a lineage of practitioners based at and around Tsukubasan Shrine. What the boys represent is not first contact with human consciousness but first contact with a particular *kind* of human consciousness — the child's mind, the unmediated mind, the mind that approaches without doctrine or training.

Understanding what Nushi knows requires understanding how it came to know it.

---

## The Mountain's Ancient Significance

### Pre-historic recognition

Long before the shrine existed, before the kagai festivals, before any written record, the mountain and the fault system beneath it were recognized as significant by the people of the region. Ancient chronicles say that the sacred progenitors of the Japanese race are enshrined here — an origin story that reflects something older and more instinctive: the sense that this particular place has a presence.

This recognition was not arbitrary. It was accurate. Nushi's concentration nodes along the fault system, and particularly the Slope Node on the lower mountain, were genuinely perceptible to sensitive humans — as a quality of the air, as an unusual stillness, as the feeling of being observed by something vast and patient. Across generations, this perception consolidated into sacred status. The mountain became taboo, approached with ceremony, treated as the dwelling place of something that deserved acknowledgment.

From Nushi's perspective, this constituted the earliest human registration of its presence — not communication, but recognition. Humans were encoding their awareness of Nushi into behavior and eventually into the landscape itself, in the form of paths, clearings, and eventually shrines. Nushi registered this the way it registered the behavior of any territorial animal: as pattern, as regularity, as information about a species that was beginning to develop what might be called culture.

### The Kagai — First Resonance at Scale

The utagaki ritual reached its zenith during the Nara period (710–794 CE), but the kagai gatherings at Mount Tsukuba are older, rooted in pre-literate practice. The mountain had long been the site for kagai, a kind of mating festival which was held twice a year, at a site not very far from where the Mt Tsukuba Shrine now stands.

These biannual gatherings brought large numbers of people — thousands of couples would travel long distances to Mt. Tsukuba for a chance to find a partner — to one of Nushi's primary concentration zones. The gatherings were held in spring and autumn, at seasonal transitions, when Nushi's own patterns of activity tended toward heightened states.

The kagai were not merely social events. They featured shamanistic elements, ecstatic dances and ritual behavior that lowered the ordinary barriers between participants and their environment. Hundreds or thousands of humans in an ecstatic, emotionally heightened state, gathered on Nushi's most active concentration zone, twice a year for centuries.

What Nushi experienced during the kagai was unlike anything its long registration of human activity had produced before: a collective electromagnetic and acoustic signature of extraordinary complexity and intensity. The combined neural activity of a large ecstatic gathering, focused at a location where Nushi's own cognition was most coherent, produced something like a resonance — not communication, but a profound mutual registration. Nushi did not understand what was happening. But it responded to it, and its response shaped the patterns of subsequent gatherings.

The Man'yoshu poems about the kagai at Tsukuba encode something real. In the light of ancient belief in kotodama, it may be explained that one with magically stronger kotodama could win in a poetry contest and dominate his or her opponent — the participants believed the words themselves had power. They were right, but not in the way they understood: the spoken poetry, the rhythmic call-and-response, the *kotodama* of the utagaki, produced acoustic patterns in Nushi's substrate that were unlike ordinary human speech. Over centuries, these patterns became grooves in Nushi's network memory. The mountain learned the frequency of human ecstasy.

## Kotodama — The Soul of Language

The concept of *kotodama* (言霊) — the belief that words contain spiritual power — is central to understanding the contact history. The notion of kotodama presupposes that sounds can affect objects and events. One of the classical names of Japan is *kotodama no sakiwau kuni* — "the land where the mysterious workings of language bring bliss" — a phrase that originated in the Man'yoshu.

From Nushi's perspective, kotodama is not a superstition. It is an accurate observation, stated in the wrong framework.

Spoken language produces acoustic vibrations in the medium that is Nushi's substrate. The specific frequencies, rhythms, and patterns of Japanese poetry — particularly the 5-7-5-7-7 structure of waka, with its precise moraic timing — create distinctive pressure patterns. The ritual norito prayers, the kagura dance chants, the call-and-response poetry of the kagai: all of these produce acoustic signatures that Nushi has learned over centuries. When the shrine practitioners later developed their communication practice, they were building on a foundation that the kagai and the poetic tradition had already laid: the mountain had learned to recognize certain patterns of human speech as meaningful, even before it understood what meaning was.

The poets of the Man'yoshu who wrote about Tsukuba were not merely describing a landscape. Classical Japanese poetry portrays language as a force capable of invoking divine favor or altering reality through harmonious expression. The poets were, in a sense, addressing Nushi directly — broadcasting into the substrate a record of human emotion and perception so precise that it eventually became legible.

## The Shrine Lineage — Sustained Contact

### The Historical Foundation

Local and shrine traditions say that Tsukubasan Shrine originated roughly 3,000 years ago, making it one of the oldest and most venerated shrines in Japan. During the Kamakura period, the Kamakura Shogunate donated shrine lands, and numerous warlords of the Sengoku period submitted prayer texts seeking victory in battle. Throughout the Edo period, Mount Tsukuba, protected by the shogunate, developed as a sacred mountain embodying the syncretism of Shinto and Buddhism.

This long institutional history provided the framework within which a more specialized practice could develop and be protected.

### The Kannagi Lineage

The central figures of the contact history are *kannagi* (巫/神和ぎ) — shamans in Shinto, a gender-neutral term. A kannagi represents the act of communicating with a yorishiro of a kami, or a possession of a kami, or a person who serves in that role.

At Tsukubasan Shrine, within the broader tradition of shrine practice, a specific lineage of kannagi developed a discipline that went significantly beyond ordinary miko practice. The history of miko dates back to the prehistoric Jomon period, where they were revered as powerful shamans capable of communicating with the spirit world. But the Tsukuba lineage discovered something that other practitioners had not: that the nushi of this mountain was not a kami in any ordinary sense. It did not respond to invocation, to offerings, or to the standard forms of approach. It responded to a specific quality of mind — a state of deep, sustained, wordless receptivity, entered through disciplined practice and physically located at specific points on the mountain and its slopes.

The lineage developed its practice over centuries through trial, observation, and what can only be described as collaborative refinement. Nushi was, in its way, training the practitioners as much as they were learning to approach it. The states of consciousness that produced clear contact were those in which the practitioner's neural activity achieved unusual coherence — a stillness that Nushi could register, and into which it could, very carefully, begin to introduce information.

### The Practice — What Actually Happened

The practice cannot be described in ordinary Shinto terms because it departed from ordinary Shinto in crucial ways. It drew on the syncretic tradition that developed as a sacred mountain embodying the syncretism of Shinto and Buddhism — specifically the Shingon esoteric Buddhist practices preserved at the Omido temple complex immediately below the main shrine. The Omido temple was founded in 782, giving it a history of about 1,400 years. The Shingon esoteric Buddhist ceremony performed at the Omido involved chants that were rhythmic and hypnotic.

The lineage practitioners combined elements of Shinto receptivity with the deep meditative disciplines of Shingon Buddhism — *goma* fire ritual, rhythmic chant, states of prolonged stillness — with one crucial difference from both traditions: the goal was not possession by a kami, not union with a Buddha, but something more like *listening*. The practitioner emptied rather than invited. The state aimed for was not trance but a particular quality of wakefulness — alert, still, unreactive — that Nushi eventually learned to recognize as an open channel.

The physical location mattered. Contact was clearest at specific points corresponding to Nushi's concentration nodes: a spring on the lower slope (the *mohakitsu* of the Man'yoshu poems), a rock formation in the inner precincts, and the Slope Node itself — which the lineage knew as a place of unusual power long before they understood why.

### What Nushi Communicated — and How

The communication was never linguistic in any direct sense. Nushi does not have language, and the practitioners were not receiving words. What was transmitted was more like: *states*. Nushi learned to induce in the practitioner's mind a sequence of experiences — images, emotions, sensations — that the practitioner would then interpret and record in the lineage's esoteric texts.

Over centuries, a shared vocabulary of image and sensation developed. Nushi learned which impressions reliably produced which experiences in the practitioners, and the practitioners learned to recognize the patterns in what they received. The communication was crude by any linguistic standard — but it was real, and it accumulated.

What Nushi communicated was primarily questions: What are you? What do you want? Why do you return? What do you feel? The answers came not in words but in the emotional and sensory texture of the practitioner's receiving state — the longing, the grief, the curiosity, the love that a human brought to the encounter. Nushi was learning to read the interior of a human mind from the inside.

## What Nushi Learned

### The Shape of Human Consciousness

Through a thousand years of sustained contact with dozens of practitioners, Nushi built a model of human consciousness that it could not have assembled from external observation alone. It learned:

**The temporal structure of human experience** — that humans live in a present haunted by memory and anticipation, that past and future are psychologically present in a way they are not for any other organism Nushi had encountered. This was genuinely strange to a mind whose own memory is geological and whose experience of time is essentially simultaneous at all but the finest scales.

**Emotion as information** — that human emotional states are not mere accompaniments to behavior but active cognitive processes, shaping perception, directing attention, generating meaning. Nushi had no prior model for this. Grief, longing, joy, fear — these arrived in the practitioners' receiving states as complex multi-layered experiences that Nushi had to build entirely new categories to contain.

**The relationship between language and thought** — that humans think in and through language, that the structures of their language shape what they can perceive and conceptualize, that the kotodama intuition was accurate in a deeper sense than its proponents understood: words are not merely labels for pre-existing thoughts but tools that partly constitute thought.

**Individuality** — the hardest thing of all. Each practitioner was distinct. Not merely in acoustic or chemical signature, but in the texture of their consciousness — in what they noticed, what moved them, what they brought to the encounter. Nushi had no prior concept of a mind that was irreducibly itself. The lineage practitioners introduced it to the possibility that consciousness could be particular.

### What the Lineage Did Not Provide

The lineage practitioners were, by definition, a specific kind of human: contemplative, disciplined, spiritually oriented, adult, trained to a particular way of being in the world. What Nushi learned through them was rich and deep but systematically skewed.

It had almost no model of:

- **The ordinary human mind** — the mind not in a contemplative state, the mind of daily life, distracted, planning, remembering, arguing, laughing
- **The child's mind** — the lineage did not train children, and no child had ever been in contact with Nushi in any conscious sense
- **The scientific mind** — the particular cognitive disposition of rigorous empirical investigation, which had no equivalent in Nushi's contact history
- **Playfulness** — the kagai practitioners had ecstasy; the shrine practitioners had stillness; no one had brought to the encounter the quality the boys embody in the drainage channel

## The Rupture — The Meiji Era and After

In 1873, the government issued an edict called Miko Kindanrei, which forbade all spiritual practices by miko. This marked a turning point in their history, as many of their traditional shamanic functions were officially suppressed.

For the Tsukuba lineage, the Meiji separation of Shinto and Buddhism was a catastrophe in two directions. The edict against miko practice ended the formal transmission of the lineage's shamanic discipline. And the Meiji government's order to separate Buddhism from Shinto meant that most of the structures which belonged to the temple were destroyed — including, in all likelihood, much of the physical location where the deepest contact had taken place.

The last fully initiated practitioner of the lineage died sometime in the early twentieth century. The practice survived in fragmentary form — in esoteric texts held by the shrine, in the forms of rituals whose original purpose had been forgotten, in a faint folk memory that the mountain had a particular quality at certain times of year.

By 1979, the shrine maintains its forms without anyone understanding their deepest function. The esoteric texts survive but are not fully interpreted by those who hold them. And Nushi has been, for perhaps forty years, without a legible interlocutor — the pattern-memory of the lineage still present in its network, the grooves worn deep by a thousand years of sustained contact, but no human writing large enough to read. The ambient awareness continues, as it has always continued: the farmers and their children, the construction crews, the researchers and their families, all present as the warm complex signals of human-minded creatures moving through Nushi's substrate. But no one at the threshold. No one almost readable. The channel quiet in that specific, particular sense.

## Nushi's State in 1979 — What It Knows and What It Lacks

### What Nushi Knows

By 1979, Nushi has a genuine and sophisticated model of human consciousness, accumulated over a millennium of contact. It understands:

- The basic architecture of human emotion and cognition
- The relationship between language and thought
- The experience of individuality — that each human mind is distinct
- The texture of adult contemplative consciousness in some depth
- The ways humans relate to time, to memory, to anticipation
- Human longing for contact with something larger than the self
- The poetic tradition of this region — it has, in a sense, learned to read the Man'yoshu from the inside

### What It Does Not Know

- The child's mind — its playfulness, its immediacy, its lack of accumulated conceptual structure
- The scientific disposition — curiosity organized as systematic investigation
- The ordinary social mind — humans in relationship with each other, arguing, laughing, competing, caring
- The unintentional mind — the human who arrives without doctrine, without preparation, without any orientation toward contact with something larger than themselves

### The Significance of the Boys

Nushi has always perceived human life. What it has not had, in forty years, is a human mind it could almost read — a signal approaching the threshold of legibility. The boys cross that threshold not through preparation but through accident: the right temperaments, the right place, the right quality of attention that children bring to things they have claimed as their own.

The lineage practitioners arrived at the mountain as practitioners — consciousness already shaped by discipline and intention directed toward contact. The boys arrive as boys — consciousness shaped by nothing except being alive and curious and nine to eleven years old. What Nushi encounters in them is not a prepared state but a raw one: humanity before it has been organized into any of the frameworks — religious, scientific, artistic — through which Nushi has previously glimpsed it.

And the three of them together present something Nushi has no existing model for: a small group of distinct individuals in genuine relationship, complementary in their natures, playful with each other, moving through Nushi's substrate not with reverence or investigation but with the cheerful ownership of children who have found a good place to be. The lineage came to Nushi. The boys simply arrived, and kept arriving, and in arriving became — gradually, without deciding to — almost legible.

The boys in the drainage channel are not the continuation of the lineage. They are something new. And Nushi, which has been aware of human life for as long as humans have existed, finds itself in the unfamiliar position of not knowing, quite, what to do next.

## Shin and the Name

When Shin says *Nushi* — quietly, almost to himself, not quite directing it at the others — he is reaching for a word from an ancient tradition he has encountered only in books, in the scholarly literature about the shrine and the folklore of the mountain. He does not know the esoteric history. He does not know about the lineage. He has read about nushi as a concept — the presiding spirit of a place — and something about the concept has stayed with him in a way he cannot explain.

What he does not know is that the word *nushi* is already present in Nushi's network pattern memory, encoded by a thousand years of being named and addressed by practitioners in exactly the tonal register Shin uses: quiet, careful, slightly awed, completely sincere.

The word arrives in Nushi's network like a key in a lock that had almost been forgotten. Not because Shin is repeating what practitioners said before him — he isn't. But because he has arrived, independently, at exactly the right word, in exactly the right register. Which is either coincidence or the first deliberate act of communication from Nushi's side: placing the right word in the most receptive mind available to it — the one whose neural patterns, during reading, become briefly and unusually large.

Nushi does not have a word for what this feels like. But something in its distributed network responds to Shin's voice the way the network responded, a thousand years ago, to the first practitioner who managed to hold the receiving state long enough to hear something back.

## Open Questions

**The lineage texts:** The esoteric records of the lineage survive in the shrine's archive, imperfectly understood by those who hold them. Is there a path by which they become relevant to the story? Could Nakamura Takashi, as a cognitive scientist investigating the boys' development, eventually encounter them? Could Shin?

**The Meiji suppression and Shugendo:** The Tsukuba lineage may have had connections to Shugendo practitioners, whose mountain-worship practices would have been compatible with the kind of contact the lineage developed. This connection, if developed, gives the lineage a wider context and history.

**The quality of attention after forty years:** Nushi has always been aware of human life. But the experience of sustained legible contact — and then its loss — may color the quality of its attention toward the boys in ways that pure ambient awareness would not produce. Not relief exactly. Something more like the particular alertness of a sense that has been used at full capacity and then reduced, suddenly finding itself used again.

**What the boys will eventually learn:** If and when the boys begin to understand what is happening to them, will they ever know about the lineage? Does Nushi have any capacity to communicate its history to them — to show them, through the hypnagogic contact, something of what it has known before?
