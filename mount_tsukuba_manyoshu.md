# Mount Tsukuba in the Man'yoshu

## The Basic Fact

Mount Tsukuba is mentioned 25 times in the Man'yoshu — more than the far more famous and majestic Mount Fuji, which appears only 13 times. This is a point of considerable pride among the people who live near the mountain. For a relatively modest peak (877 meters) located at what was then considered the remote edge of civilization, this frequency of mention is remarkable and meaningful.

The Man'yoshu itself is the oldest extant collection of Japanese poetry, compiled in the late Nara period (710–794 AD), containing poems written between the 4th and 8th centuries. It is widely considered the greatest of Japan's poetic collections — fresh, direct, and free of the complex rules that dominated later waka poetry.

---

## Why Tsukuba Was Mentioned So Often

### The Twin Peaks
The key is Mount Tsukuba's distinctive shape. Its twin peaks were seen by ancient inhabitants as representing the male and female forces — the fundamental interacting principles of existence. Simply mentioning the mountain in a poem carried automatic connotations of union, fertility, and the meeting of opposites. Poets used Tsukuba symbolically even when — perhaps especially when — they had never visited it.

The river flowing down from the valley between the two peaks is called the **Minanogawa** — 男女川 — literally *the Man-Woman River*. This name alone tells you everything about how the mountain was understood.

### The Sakimori Poems
Many of the Tsukuba references appear in poems of the *sakimori* — peasants conscripted from the eastern provinces (including what is now Ibaraki Prefecture, where Tsukuba stands) to serve as border guards on distant Kyushu Island. These poems express deep longing for home and family, and Mount Tsukuba — visible from great distances across the Kanto Plain — became the symbol of everything left behind.

## Key Poems

### Poem 1759 — Takahashi no Mushimaro
From Volume 9, describing attendance at the Kagai festival on the mountain's peak:

*"On Mt. Tsukuba, where the eagle dwells... the Gods who have ruled this mountain since time immemorial do not forbid this practice — on that special day."*

This poem explicitly names the gods who have ruled the mountain *since time immemorial* — an ancient presiding presence that predates human civilization in the area. For our story, this is a remarkable piece of authentic folkloric grounding. The ancient poets sensed something.

### A Poem of Secret Meeting
From the Man'yoshu, describing souls meeting across distance:

筑波嶺の 彼面此面に 守部据ゑ 母い守れども 魂ぞ会ひにける

*(On Mount Tsukuba, here and there, guardsmen are set — my mother stands vigilant over me — yet our souls have met.)*

The poem turns on an ambiguity — have the lovers met in person, or only in dreams? In the Nara period, a meeting of souls in dreams carried as much significance as a physical meeting. The mountain is the place where such crossings happen.

### A Folk Poem Variation
筑波山は山しげ山茂きをぞや誰が子も通ふな下に通へわがつまは下に

*(Tsukuba Mountain has peaks both high and low — so many pass along their ways. Go secretly! For my wife is secret, too...)*

The mountain is a place of hidden passages, secret meetings, things moving beneath the surface.

## The Kagai Festival

Twice yearly — in spring and autumn — Mount Tsukuba was the site of the *Kagai*, an ancient fertility festival in which young men and women gathered to exchange improvised poems and choose partners. The festival had the explicit sanction of the mountain's presiding gods. It was understood as something the mountain itself authorized and blessed.

The Kagai represents one of the oldest documented instances of the mountain being understood as a place where normal social rules are suspended — where something larger than human custom presides.

## Implications for the Story

### Nushi Has Been Sensed Before
The ancient poets who wrote about Mount Tsukuba's presiding gods were not merely being conventionally religious. They were recording, in the only framework available to them, a genuine perception — that this mountain has something inhabiting it that predates human presence. They were right. They simply lacked the conceptual vocabulary to understand what they were sensing.

### Shin's Notebook
Shin almost certainly has copied some of these poems into his notebook, drawn to them not yet knowing why. When the boys begin experiencing Nushi's presence, Shin will find these old poems newly significant in a way that disturbs and fascinates him.

### The Dream Dimension
The Man'yoshu poem about souls meeting in dreams connects directly to the story's use of the hypnagogic state as Nushi's primary mode of contact. Ancient Japanese poetic culture already understood that Mount Tsukuba is a place where the boundary between waking and dreaming is permeable. Nushi has perhaps always used this quality of the mountain's atmosphere — and the ancient poets noticed.

### The Male-Female Peak Symbolism
The twin peaks representing the interaction of fundamental opposing forces resonates with the story's broader themes — the meeting of the ancient and the modern, the physical and the mental, the human and the incomprehensibly other. The mountain has always been understood as a place of meeting between opposites.

### The Sakimori Connection
The homesick border guards who looked back toward Tsukuba from far away were sensing the mountain's presence across distance. This suggests Nushi's distributed nature extends well beyond the mountain itself — it is the Kanto Plain, not just the peak. The sakimori felt something real when they longed for Tsukuba. They were feeling the pull of the substrate they had grown up within.

## A Note on Using This Material

These are authentic historical poems and folkloric details that Shin could plausibly have encountered in books available to a Japanese ten year old in 1979. The Man'yoshu is taught in Japanese schools and widely published. The Kagai traditions and the significance of the twin peaks are part of local Tsukuba culture that any curious child living near the mountain might discover.

This gives Shin's eventual naming of Nushi its full weight — he reaches for an ancient word because ancient people were reaching toward the same thing, and found the best words available to them.
