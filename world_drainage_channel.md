# The Drainage Channel — 排水路
## A Physical Description, 1979

---

## What It Is and Where It Came From

The channel is old in the way that useful things are old — not preserved, not significant to anyone, simply still there because there was never a reason to remove it.

Before the science city existed, this part of the Tsukuba area was agricultural land: rice paddies, field boundaries, the slow careful hydraulics of Kanto farming that had been developed over centuries. Traditional rice paddy irrigation in the Kanto region used shallow earthen ditches — hand-dug, flowing, ecologically alive with the snails and frogs and fireflies that made those channels their habitat. The channel that the boys know began as one of these: a farm drainage ditch running along the boundary between two fields, following the line of least resistance across the slight slope, which happened to trace the old geological fault beneath.

In the 1960s, when the science city development began in earnest, the agricultural landscape was progressively consumed. Paddy fields were drained and graded. Earthen ditches were replaced with concrete. The standard drainage solution of the period was the U-shaped concrete channel — cheap, efficient, easy to construct in sections, requiring no maintenance. Most of the old field drainage was replaced. This channel survived because it ran along what would become the property boundary between two research institute parcels, and neither institution wanted to take responsibility for it. It was marked on planning documents, concreted along its lower section, and then administratively forgotten.

The result, by 1979, is a channel that is half-modernized and half-abandoned: concrete at its lower end where it passes through a culvert under a service road and discharges into the storm drainage system, but increasingly informal as you follow it uphill toward the mountain. The concrete lining ends at an unmarked point somewhere past the back fence of the second research institute. Above that point, the channel reverts to its original character — earthen banks, the old farm boundary alignment, the fault-line topography expressing itself in the slight irregularity of the gradient.

## The Physical Character — Section by Section

### The Entry Point — The Culvert

The boys approach the channel from the danchi side, squeezing through a gap between the rear fence of one of the institute buildings and a concrete retaining wall. The entry is unpromising: a concrete apron, a rusted grille over the culvert mouth (one corner bent back, creating a gap sufficient for a determined nine-year-old with a small randoseru), the smell of stagnant water and wet concrete.

This is the least interesting part of the channel and the boys move through it quickly. The culvert itself is not entered — even Taro, who has tested most physical limits, has not gone into the culvert. It is dark, it smells, and the grille bent back just enough to suggest it was meant to be passed, not explored.

### The Lower Concrete Section

Past the culvert the channel opens up. The concrete lining here is standard 1960s civil engineering: U-shaped cross-section, approximately sixty centimeters wide and fifty deep, the interior surface rough and stained with decades of water flow and algae. The channel runs between the rear fences of the two institutes — chain-link on both sides, strung with the occasional faded warning sign that no one reads.

Despite the concrete, life has moved in. The channel floor carries a permanent thin film of water except in the driest August weeks — enough to sustain a long streak of bright green algae, some patches of moss in the shadowed sections, and the occasional frog that has found its way in and cannot find its way out. The walls carry lines of mineral staining that record the high-water marks of different seasons like geological strata. In the seams between concrete sections, plants have established themselves: first the opportunists — dandelion, plantain, shepherd's purse — and then, in the older cracks, things with roots deep enough to matter: a persistent clump of miscanthus grass, some broadleaf dock.

The chain-link fences on both sides are similarly colonized at their bases — tall grasses, bramble, the inevitable gourd vine that appears in any neglected corner of the Kanto countryside. In summer this creates a corridor of green that is taller than the boys and substantially obscures the fences and the buildings beyond them. The channel is already, here, a different country from the institute grounds above it.

### The Transition Zone

Where the concrete ends, there is no clear marker. The concrete sections simply become less regular — one cracked and tilted, another half-buried by accumulated silt and root growth — until the channel is more earth than concrete. This transition happens over perhaps ten meters and the boys have never thought about it precisely. They are simply aware, at some point, that the channel has become different.

The transition zone collects things. Silt from upstream, leaves, the occasional piece of construction debris that has found its way over the fences. In wet seasons there is standing water here that goes nowhere — held by the accumulated silt, slowly drinking down through the fault-zone substrate rather than draining forward. This is one of the spots Nushi has made slightly more hospitable: the water table here is managed, invisibly, so that the standing water recedes faster than it should, leaving the ground damp but passable within a day of rain.

### The Upper Earth Section — The Boys' Territory

This is where the channel becomes the boys' territory, and where it becomes genuinely interesting.

Above the transition, the channel is earthen and entirely overgrown. The banks are high here — nearly a meter on each side — because the fault-line topography creates a slight depression that the old farm drainage followed and deepened over decades of water flow. The channel floor is perhaps eighty centimeters wide, though in summer the vegetation reduces this to a passable strip of perhaps forty. The banks are held by the roots of the vegetation growing on them: tall grasses, bramble, a stand of young bamboo that has colonized the east bank and throws useful shade in late afternoon.

The feeling here is of a green tunnel. Standing in the channel, the banks are above head height for Taro and at nose level for Kenji. The sky is a strip directly overhead and through the bamboo fronds and miscanthus plumes. Adults who stood in the channel would see over the banks easily — but adults do not stand in the channel.

The floor of the channel in this section is remarkable. It is not muddy — or rather, it is not as muddy as it should be. The silt here has a composition unusual for an urban drainage channel: mineral-rich, dark, the color of good farmland rather than construction runoff, firmer underfoot than it has any right to be. The boys' feet sink in a little but not unpleasantly. Taro has taken personal credit for this. He is not entirely wrong that it is an unusually good place.

### The Spot

The boys have a specific spot in the upper section, perhaps thirty meters above the transition zone, where the east bank widens into a shallow platform — a remnant of the old field boundary berm, perhaps, or simply an irregularity in the fault line that has expressed itself in the topography. The bamboo on this side overhangs the channel slightly, providing shade. The west bank at this point has a section where the roots of a large miscanthus clump have created a kind of solid seat at bank height — a place to sit with your feet in the channel, your back against the grassed earth.

The space here is perhaps two meters wide at its widest — an enlargement of the channel that would barely register on any survey plan but constitutes, for three boys, a room. The floor of this section is the driest in the whole upper channel. Nushi's first deliberate act has been focused here specifically: the groundwater management that keeps this spot usable has its epicenter in the fault zone directly beneath this widening.

There is always water in the channel below this spot and above it. At the spot itself, the channel floor is reliably passable within hours of rain, for reasons that defy the hydrology of the surrounding terrain.

## The Ecology of the Channel in Summer

The channel supports a small but specific community of living things, most of which are not visible to casual inspection.

**Water**: The channel carries a thin flow from the fault-zone groundwater discharge that is largely independent of rainfall. In dry weather it is barely a trickle; after rain it becomes a proper small stream for a day or two before returning to its base flow. The water is notably cold — groundwater temperature — even in August. It carries no significant pollution, having no residential catchment, and is considerably cleaner than the concrete section downstream.

**Plants**: The dominant species on the banks are *Miscanthus sinensis* (susuki, the tall silver grass of Japanese autumn paintings — Taro calls it the porcupine grass because of its stiff leaves) and common bamboo on the east bank. Lower down, *dokudami* (houttuynia) spreads in wet patches, its heart-shaped leaves and white four-petaled flowers familiar to every Japanese child as a medicinal plant that smells medicinal. The fault-zone silt supports plants that prefer mineral-rich substrate: horsetail (*tokusa*) at the wetter margins, some wild garlic.

**Animals**: Frogs — brown, unremarkable, abundant — are the most conspicuous animal residents. They emerge at dusk and the channel sounds like a different place entirely on summer evenings. Water skaters on the slow stretches. An occasional grass snake. A family of weasels has denned somewhere in the east bank; the boys have seen the adults but not the den. Birds use the channel margin for foraging but do not nest there — too little cover, too many boys.

**Insects**: In summer the channel hums. Dragonflies patrol the water. Beetles of several kinds work the rotting vegetation at the base of the miscanthus. Crickets in the bank grass produce the sound that defines Japanese summer evenings. Taro has, at various points, attempted to catch nearly all of these with varying success.

## The Channel in the Different Seasons

**Spring**: The channel emerges from winter brown, the banks bare except for the first new growth of miscanthus from the cut stubs. The frogs return loudly. Snowmelt from the mountain raises the base flow for several weeks. This is when the channel is most alive and least comfortable — the ground is consistently wet, the banks are slippery, and the lack of vegetation cover means less protection from the wind. The boys visit but do not linger.

**Early summer**: The vegetation explodes. Within three weeks of the first warm rain, the banks are green to above the boys' heads and the tunnel quality of the channel is fully established. This is the beginning of the channel's best season. The frogs are at peak volume. The weasel kits emerge.

**Midsummer**: Peak use. The shade of the bamboo and miscanthus makes the channel noticeably cooler than the open ground — perhaps two or three degrees, enough to matter in August humidity. The groundwater flow is at its minimum but is still present. This is when the boys are there most often, most afternoons, sometimes until the light starts to change.

**Autumn**: The miscanthus produces its silver-white plumes, which Taro persists in using as improvised weapons and Shin has twice pressed into his notebook. The light in the channel changes as the vegetation begins to thin — more sky visible, longer shadows earlier. The boys' visits become less frequent as the school year intensifies. The channel takes on a quality of ending-of-something that Kenji notices and does not mention.

**Winter**: The channel is cold and mostly bare and the boys do not go there. The bamboo holds its color. The fault-zone groundwater keeps a thin flow even in the coldest weeks. The channel exists without them.

## What the Channel Means — For the Story

The channel is the boys' territory in the deepest sense: a place that belongs to them because they found it, because they return to it, because they have made it theirs through use and attention. It is invisible to the adults around them — not hidden from them exactly, just below the threshold of adult concern. A drainage channel between two institute buildings, overgrown, irrelevant. The gap in the fence is not large enough for a person who is not specifically trying to get through.

What it is, for the story, is the place where two things meet: the children's ordinary world and something vastly older. The boys experience the channel as refuge and territory and good place. Nushi experiences it as the location of its deepest local concentration — the fault line, the mixed hydrology, the mineral substrate — and as the place where the new contact is developing.

The spot where the channel widens, where the ground stays dry, where Taro sits with his back against the earth and makes pronouncements about the excellence of their territory — that spot is, from Nushi's side of things, the location of the most focused attention it has directed at anything in forty years.

Taro is not wrong that it is a very good place. He simply has the causality reversed.

## Notes for Scenes Set in the Channel

- Sound: the channel is surprisingly loud. Water, frogs, insects, wind in the miscanthus producing a continuous papery rustle. Conversation happens at a slightly raised volume. This creates a useful acoustic privacy — voices in the channel don't carry far beyond the banks.
- Smell: distinctive and good — the smell of cool water, green growth, mineral earth. The dokudami has a sharp medicinal quality that the boys have stopped noticing. In autumn there is a smell of decay that is not unpleasant, the smell of things turning into soil.
- Light: in midsummer the channel is a green-lit space — the sun filtered through bamboo and miscanthus creates a moving dappled light that changes constantly with any breeze. Direct sunlight reaches the floor only at midday when the sun is directly overhead. Otherwise the channel is in a kind of continuous shade that is never dark.
- Physical movement: the boys move through the channel in single file, with Kenji typically first and Taro typically requiring assistance over the one tricky section where the east bank has slumped slightly toward the channel floor. Getting back out at the boys' end requires climbing a root-stepped section of bank that has become automatic after many crossings.
- Taro's cap: when Taro's cap stirs without wind in the channel, it is most likely to happen at the spot — the widening with its groundwater epicenter — and on days when Nushi's cognitive activity is elevated. The boys have all noticed it. None of them, yet, has said anything about it.
