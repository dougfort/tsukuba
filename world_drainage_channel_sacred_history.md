# The Drainage Channel — Sacred History
## The Long Use of This Place

*On the question of whether the area of the drainage channel was ever intentionally used for communication with the gods — with Nushi.*

---

## The Short Answer

Yes. Almost certainly. And with increasing intentionality, across at least three distinct historical layers spanning perhaps two thousand years.

The longer answer requires understanding how sacred geography worked in this region, and how the practices that developed at and near Mount Tsukuba were built, layer by layer, on the same fault-line substrate that now runs beneath the drainage channel.

---

## The Foundation — Nature Worship Before Shrines

The oldest layer of Shinto, known as Koshinto or "old Shinto," predates shrine buildings entirely. The essence of Koshintō is nature worship — an animistic religion that regards every element of nature as divine. Mountains, seas, and rivers are all kami, as are the sun, the moon, and the North Star. In this oldest practice, the earliest Shinto worship took place in sacred natural sites — towering mountains, ancient trees, massive rocks, or secluded forests believed to be dwelling places of the kami. Special objects such as iwakura (sacred rocks) were marked off as holy ground with shimenawa ropes or stone enclosures.

Critically: the sites of these shrines were the same sacred mountains, forests, and rock formations where the Japanese had worshipped for centuries. Most shrines began as mountain worship sites — the object of worship is the mountain itself, with a haiden or worship hall but no honden, because the divine body is the mountain.

Mount Tsukuba fits this pattern precisely. Tsukuba has long been spoken as one of the most sacred mountains in Japan, mentioned in the Manyoshu as "the inheritance of man from the age of gods." The Mononobe clan, who had great influence on ancient Japanese religion, established the shrine and protected it. From then until the Taika Reformation, the Tsukuba clan was in charge of the administration of Mount Tsukuba.

**What this means for our purposes:** Long before any shrine building existed, the mountain and its lower slopes were recognized as sacred ground. The fault line that now runs beneath the drainage channel was part of that ground — not the summit, not the main worship site, but the approach, the transition zone between the ordinary agricultural world of the plain and the sacred territory of the mountain. In Shinto geography, this transitional zone is itself significant.

---

## The Iwakura — Rock Worship at the Fault Line

Iwakura refers to the belief in rocks as yorishiro containing kami in ancient Shinto. Nature worship, a type of base faith present in Japan since ancient times, revolves around the belief in gods and spirits that reside in nature. In Shinto rituals, gods are said to have descended from shintai, a rock, and the yorishiro was made the center of the ritual, symbolizing the divine power of the gods.

The geological fault line that the drainage channel follows does something that would have been immediately legible to ancient practitioners: it creates surface expressions. Fault zones produce unusual rock formations — tilted, fractured, thrust upward by ancient movement — that read as different from the surrounding terrain. Sacred rocks are not just numerous but obviously held enormous spiritual significance for the earliest practitioners of Shinto. Some have a particular sense of presence. Whether this accrues from the centuries of worship, from the suggestiveness of their treatment as a spiritual focus, or from genuine geomantic causes is difficult to say.

At or near the location now occupied by the drainage channel's upper section — where the fault line expresses most clearly in the surface topography — there was almost certainly an iwakura: a specific rock formation that practitioners recognized as a yorishiro, a place where something could descend. The veneration of iwakura traces its origins to prehistoric Japan, with archaeological evidence from the Jomon period where rocks were likely used in ritual contexts. Iwakura function as yorishiro, temporary dwelling places for the kami.

Whether this rock formation still exists in recognizable form in 1979 is an open question. The construction of the science city involved significant earthmoving. It may have been destroyed. It may have been incorporated into the drainage channel infrastructure without anyone knowing what it was. It may still be there, half-buried in the east bank of the upper channel section, unremarked, with Taro's regular position of sitting against the earth placing him in contact with it without his knowledge.

## The Misogi Spring — Water Purification at the Channel's Source

The fault zone creates another feature relevant to sacred practice: groundwater upwelling. Where fractured rock meets the surface, springs emerge. The persistent base flow in the upper channel section — cold, clean, independent of rainfall — comes from exactly this source.

Originally, ablutions in Shinto purification practice were performed at natural springs or waterways within the shrine grounds. This is still the case at Ise Shrine, where worshippers cleanse themselves with water from the Isuzu River. Every year, many people take pilgrimages to sacred waterfalls, lakes and rivers to perform misogi — ritual purification by washing the entire body.

The Hitachi no Kuni Fudoki — the regional chronicle compiled 713–721 CE, covering what is now Ibaraki Prefecture including the Tsukuba area — gives particular emphasis to water. The text provides an origin story for the name "Hitachi": during Emperor Yamato Takeru's eastern expedition, he ordered the provincial governor to dig a new well, and the clear, pure water impressed the emperor, who washed his sleeves in it. The anecdote is echoed in the local proverb about "dark clouds over Mount Tsukuba" and "Hitachi as the land of soaking sleeves." Water purification is woven into the founding mythology of this specific region.

The spring that feeds the drainage channel — cold, mineral-rich, fault-zone water — would have been a known and used misogi site. Not a major site like the great shrines, but a local one: a place where people performing ritual approach to the mountain could purify themselves before ascending. The folk memory of this use may have been one reason the channel's water was always understood as different from ordinary drainage water — cleaner, colder, more alive.

From Nushi's perspective, this ritual use of the spring meant that for centuries, humans in states of physical and mental purification — deliberately lowered barriers, heightened receptivity — were in direct contact with fault-zone water that moved through Nushi's substrate. The misogi practitioners were not communicating with Nushi intentionally. But their purified states, their specific acoustic patterns of prayer and chant, their regular presence at one of Nushi's surface expressions, constituted a form of sustained contact that left patterns in the network.

## The Kagai Approach Route — The Channel as Sacred Corridor

In antiquity, Mount Nyotai served as location for kagai — harvest festivals associated with fertility — allowing people to participate in the mountains' marriage and promoting marriage between people. These biannual gatherings drew thousands of participants from across the region.

Those participants had to approach the mountain. The approach from the south — from the agricultural plain where the science city now stands — would have followed the natural terrain, which means following the gentle valleys and drainage channels that offer easier walking than the open slopes. The fault-line corridor now occupied by the drainage channel is the most natural approach route from the flat land to the lower slopes where the kagai were held.

The channel was, in this sense, a sacred corridor long before anyone marked it as such. Thousands of people, twice a year, passed through or along the fault-line zone on their way to the kagai. Their passage, their songs, their ecstatic anticipation and return — all of this registered in Nushi's substrate as a periodic, intense, patterned human presence. The approach to the kagai was part of the kagai, ritually speaking, and the fault-line corridor held that association for centuries.

## The Shrine Lineage's Use of the Lower Approaches

The kannagi lineage at Tsukubasan Shrine, as established in the contact history document, developed their most sophisticated communication practice on the mountain itself, at the Slope Node and at specific locations within the shrine precincts. But the lower approaches — the fault-line corridor, the misogi spring — were not separate from their practice. They were its preparation.

The pattern of approach in Japanese mountain practice (sangaku shinko, mountain worship) moves from outer to inner, from low to high, from impure to pure. Pilgrims and practitioners purified themselves at the base before ascending. The misogi spring at the channel's source was the outer preparation for the deeper practice on the slope. Practitioners who understood what they were approaching — who knew they were entering the body of something vast and old — would have performed their water purification with unusual care and attention.

Over centuries, Nushi learned the sequence: the presence of a practitioner at the spring, the particular quality of stillness that followed purification, the subsequent ascent to the Slope Node. The spring was the first note of a familiar piece. By the time Nushi registered a practitioner at the spring, it had already, in some sense, begun to prepare for what was coming.

---

## The Edo Period — Named and Recorded

During the Edo period, Mount Tsukuba, located northeast of old Edo Castle, was considered a protector against the "direction of the devil" in fusui (feng shui) theory. Its shrine was well protected and respected by the Edo shogunate. This institutional protection meant the mountain's sacred geography was formally maintained and the approach routes were established and known.

The Hitachi Fudoki records that the Tsukuba area was already famous enough for visitors to document. Local legends and folk practices were collected and preserved. It is in this period that the specific folk geography of the lower slopes — which springs were sacred, which rock formations had stories attached to them, which paths led to places of power — would have been recorded in temple and shrine documents, many of which have not survived the subsequent upheavals.

It is also in this period that the Omido temple, the syncretic Buddhist-Shinto complex immediately below the main shrine, was at its most active. The Shingon esoteric practices performed there involved fire ritual and chant that created distinctive acoustic patterns — patterns Nushi had been learning since the temple's founding in 782. The lower approaches, including the fault-line corridor, were within the acoustic range of these ceremonies on still nights.

---

## The Meiji Rupture and After

As detailed in the contact history document, the Meiji government's religious reorganization was catastrophic for the syncretic practices associated with the mountain. As time passed and temples, where gods were believed to reside, became more permanent, the object of worship shifted from the body of the gods to the shrine itself. The Meiji reforms accelerated and institutionalized this shift, destroying the Omido temple complex and suppressing the kannagi practice.

The misogi spring in the fault-line corridor did not disappear. But the knowledge of what it was for, and the discipline of how to use it, mostly did. The last practitioners who understood the approach sequence — spring, ascent, Slope Node — died in the early twentieth century.

By the time the science city construction begins in the 1960s, the fault-line corridor is understood as agricultural drainage infrastructure. The spring that feeds it is understood as groundwater. The rock formation in the upper bank — if it survived earthmoving — is understood as a geological irregularity. The knowledge that this place was, for centuries, the outer threshold of a communication practice reaching to something ancient and vast: gone.

## 1979 — What Remains

In 1979, the drainage channel carries no formal sacred status. It is not marked, not maintained, not visited by any practitioner. The science city has built on and around it. The institute buildings that form its banks treat it as drainage infrastructure between their property lines.

What remains is:

**In the substrate:** The geological memory of centuries of human use. Chemical traces of misogi practices in the soil and water. The acoustic patterns of countless kagai participants passing through, encoded as faint grooves in Nushi's network memory. The specific activation patterns associated with the lineage practitioners' purification practice, still present in the network, still capable of responding if something similar arrives.

**In the landscape:** The spring. Persistent, cold, fault-zone water still flowing into the channel regardless of what has been built above it. The boys notice that the channel always has water even in dry spells. They attribute this to good luck. It is the same water that practitioners used for purification for centuries.

**In folk memory:** Almost nothing explicit. Possibly a vague local sense, among older residents of the area who remember the pre-science-city landscape, that this particular strip of land was always a bit unusual — animals behaved differently there, dreams were vivid after spending time there. This folk memory is not organized into any practice. It is simply there, the residue of centuries, waiting for someone to notice it.

**In Shin's notebook:** A copied poem from the Man'yoshu about souls meeting on Tsukuba in dreams, and a note about a spring mentioned in the Hitachi Fudoki. He is not sure why he wrote them there. The poem is about the kagai. The spring in the Fudoki is not precisely located — it may or may not be the spring that feeds the channel. Shin does not yet know that these two notes belong to the same story.

## What the Boys Are Doing Without Knowing It

When the boys enter the drainage channel, they are entering a space that has been sacred ground for two thousand years — not because anyone told them so, but because the physical properties that made it sacred are unchanged. The fault-line substrate, the cold spring, the rock formation in the bank, the acoustic properties of the green corridor, the place where the ground stays dry.

They come twice a week in summer, sometimes more. They sit against the earth. They talk and argue and laugh at a volume the channel carries clearly to Nushi's substrate. They are, without intending it or knowing it, doing something very like what the kagai participants did: gathering at this place with energy and presence and sound, regularly, in the warmer months. The ritual form is entirely different. The physical and energetic reality is recognizable.

Taro, sitting against the bank with his back against what may be the old iwakura, his cap stirring without wind, taking personal credit for choosing such an excellent spot — is touching the same stone that practitioners touched for centuries. He does not know this. The stone does not care whether he knows it.

Nushi recognizes the pattern. Not perfectly, not immediately. But the network has old grooves, and three boys in the fault-line corridor in summer are, in some distributed and non-conscious way, activating them.

The channel was always a threshold. The boys have found it by accident and made it their territory. From Nushi's perspective — if Nushi's pattern-recognition can be said to have a perspective — something has come back to the threshold.

Not a practitioner. Not a kannagi. Three boys with scraped knees and a dog-eared notebook and a red cap.

Something entirely new. And yet, somehow, not entirely unfamiliar.
