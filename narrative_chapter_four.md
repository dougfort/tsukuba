# Part One, Chapter Four — Draft One

---

Summer ended on a Tuesday. Not officially — officially it ended when school resumed, which it had, and the after-school visits had resumed with it, shorter now, an hour rather than an afternoon, the light already different. But the channel's summer ended on a Tuesday in mid-September when Shin arrived at the spot and the last of the red dragonflies was gone.

He noted the date. He had been noting the dates of the red dragonflies for six weeks — first sighting, peak density, behavior at the spot versus behavior in the concrete section below. The kajika frog had gone two weeks earlier, which was consistent with the literature. The dragonflies had stayed longer than expected, which he had a theory about and had not yet tested. Now they were gone and the theory would have to wait until next summer, which was a long time, which was the nature of natural history.

He wrote the date in the upper right corner of a new page and below it: *last red dragonfly: not present. Season ended.*

Behind him Taro was doing something to the west bank with a stick. This was, Shin had come to understand, maintenance. Taro had appointed himself the channel's caretaker sometime in August without announcement, and the appointment had apparently been permanent. The maintenance consisted primarily of clearing debris from the channel floor, adjusting the stone arrangement in the flow above the spot, and occasional structural interventions in the bank vegetation whose purpose Taro considered self-evident and did not explain.

"You're going to disturb the miscanthus roots," Shin said, without looking up.

"I'm improving the seat," Taro said.

"The seat is fine."

"It was lower on the right. I've been thinking about it since August."

Shin considered this. The seat — the natural ledge of miscanthus roots on the west bank — had, he now thought about it, been slightly uneven. He had not noticed it as a problem. Taro had apparently been sitting with it as a problem for six weeks. "Don't take too much off," he said.

"I know what I'm doing," Taro said, with the confidence of someone who was making this up as he went.

---

Kenji arrived twelve minutes after they did, which was standard for the after-school visits — Shin and Taro came directly from the school gate, Kenji had some errand or conversation that delayed him and arrived with the slightly compressed energy of someone who had been moving fast to catch up. He dropped into the channel and came up the floor to the widening and sat down on the platform without ceremony.

"Dragonflies are gone," Shin said.

Kenji looked at the water. "I know," he said. "Last week."

Shin looked at his notebook. He had the last dragonfly sighting as today. He had not been here last week — he'd been at his grandmother's for three days. "Last week," he said. "You came without us?"

"Tuesday," Kenji said. He said this as a fact, not an explanation.

Shin wrote *K. visited Tuesday — last dragonfly per K., one week earlier than recorded here* in a margin and put a small star beside it to indicate the correction needed. Kenji coming to the channel without them was not surprising. It was, when he thought about it, inevitable — Kenji would want to know what the channel was like without them in it, the same way he would want to know what any place was like under different conditions. It was the same instinct that made him walk fence lines for no reason.

Taro had not commented on the Tuesday visit. He was still working on the seat. After a moment he said: "Did anything happen."

"No," Kenji said.

Taro appeared to find this satisfactory. He returned to the seat.

---

The autumn miscanthus was the best thing about the channel in October. Shin had thought about this more than once — whether he would choose summer or autumn if he had to, and had arrived at no conclusion, which was itself a conclusion of a kind. Summer was more, in nearly every measurable sense. More animals, more sound, more warmth, more visits. But autumn had the plumes.

The miscanthus sent up its flowering stalks in September and by October they were fully out — silver-white, feathered, catching whatever light remained in the late afternoon in a way that changed the quality of the channel's upper section from green-tunnel to something more open, more sky-present. The plumes moved constantly in any air movement, producing a dry papery sound that was different from the summer sound — quieter in volume but more insistent, a sound you noticed more because it was less continuous. In the summer the channel was always loud. In October it was sometimes quiet and then suddenly the miscanthus would move and the sound would come, and it felt more deliberate than it had in summer.

The smell had changed too. Shin had been cataloguing the smell since August and the autumn version was different in ways that were interesting — the mineral coldness still present, the dokudami fading now, and underneath both of them something new: the smell of things turning into soil. Not rot — nothing unpleasant. The smell of organic matter returning to mineral matter, the smell of the channel digesting its summer growth. He had not found adequate words for this in any of the smell's iterations and was beginning to think he would need to make up words, which was not something he did lightly.

---

He found the weasel tracks on the second visit, in the first week of October. He had been looking for them — he had seen the weasel itself twice in August, the adult moving through the east bank vegetation with the fluid speed of something that was simultaneously hunting and being very careful, and he had been wanting to find the den or at least the primary runs. The tracks were in a section of exposed silt between two miscanthus clumps on the west bank, just upstream of the spot: the characteristic five-toe pattern, the paired prints suggesting a bounding gait, leading up into the vegetation and disappearing.

He spent twenty minutes on this. He sketched the tracks and measured the stride length and noted the depth of the impressions as an estimate of the animal's weight and wrote down the location relative to the spot. Taro watched for approximately forty-five seconds and then relocated to the platform to conduct further improvements to a section of the bamboo overhang that he had recently identified as structurally suboptimal.

Kenji came over and crouched beside Shin and looked at the tracks for a long moment. "There," he said, and pointed at a section of vegetation that Shin had not examined. He was pointing at a slightly flattened section at the base of the bank, a tunnel-mouth just visible in the roots and leaf litter.

Shin looked at it. "How did you see that."

Kenji considered this. "The grass bends differently," he said. He stood up. "Den entrance, probably." He went back to the platform.

Shin looked at the slightly flattened section for another minute. The grass bends differently. He added this to the notes, with Kenji's name beside it in brackets, and then spent some time looking at how the grass was bending elsewhere along the bank and whether he could learn to read it the way Kenji apparently could without knowing he was doing it.

He could not, or at least not yet.

---

The beetle appeared on the second October visit, in the last half hour of light, under the same stone it had been under in August. Shin had checked that stone on every visit since August — seven visits — and found nothing. He had begun to think it was a summer species, that the autumn had ended it. Then on the seventh visit there it was: turned out of the stone by Shin's now-practiced one-handed flip, the blue-green iridescence catching the low afternoon light and answering it.

He drew it for the third time, from a new angle — anterior, looking directly at the head and pronotum, getting the shape of the front margin he had not captured in either previous sketch. The beetle was cooperative for two minutes, which was longer than before. He got what he needed.

The library had not helped. He had spent an afternoon in the reference section with two field guides and a general entomology text, going through the ground beetle plates and the rove beetle plates and the plates for several families he considered possible and had found nothing with the right combination of pronotal shape and elytral iridescence. He was increasingly confident it was something genuinely unusual. He was also aware that he might simply not have the right book.

His father had said, when asked, that he didn't know much about insects but that there was a researcher in the biology building who did. He had offered to ask. Shin had said he would let him know, which was his way of retaining the option without committing to it — he wasn't ready to introduce the beetle to an adult. Not yet. The beetle was his, in the way that channel things were theirs.

He closed the notebook and turned the stone back over. The beetle oriented and moved and was gone.

---

In the last fifteen minutes of the second October visit, with the light going amber through the bamboo, they sat without doing anything. This happened more in autumn than it had in summer — the sitting sessions, all three of them at the spot in no particular arrangement, not talking or doing much. Taro had his back against the platform and his legs stretched into the channel. Kenji sat on the west bank seat, which was now, Shin had to admit, slightly more even on the right. Shin had his notebook closed on his knee.

The channel in the last light was worth looking at. The miscanthus plumes were gold where the light caught them, the bamboo throwing long thin shadows across the floor. The water sound was present and the miscanthus sound was present and that was all — no frogs now, no insects, the channel at its most stripped-back, the essential version of it.

Shin looked at this and thought about the dragonfly count and the weasel tracks and the beetle's third sketch and the way Kenji had seen the den entrance when Shin had been looking directly at the same section and seen nothing. He thought about how the channel had been here before the science city, before the institute buildings, before Tsukuba had been a name for anything other than a mountain. It had followed this fault line through whatever the land had been — paddy fields, scrubland, forest — and it would follow it after the institutes were gone, if they were ever gone, which was not something the institutes would recognize as a possibility but which the channel would not find surprising.

He thought about the smell of things turning into soil, and how it was different from decay, and how he still didn't have a word for it.

Next to him, Kenji was looking at the strip of sky above the bamboo. He had the expression he wore when he was thinking about something he wasn't going to say — not moody, just occupied, attending. Shin knew this face well enough to leave it alone.

After a while Taro said: "We should come on weekends in winter."

"It'll be cold," Kenji said.

"So we'll be cold," Taro said. He said this as if cold were a reasonable exchange for something he didn't name, which it was.

Kenji nodded once, looking at the sky.

Shin opened his notebook to a blank page and wrote the date and then sat with the pen in his hand and did not write anything else. The light was going and he would need to go soon and the notebook could wait. Some things were better left until the following morning when he could see them more clearly.

He would write about the weasel tracks and the beetle and the dragonfly season ending. He would write about the miscanthus and the smell. He would write *K. saw the den entrance* with Kenji's explanation in brackets and his own note that he had not been able to replicate the perception.

He would not write about sitting in the last light, the three of them in the channel in October, the miscanthus going gold. Some things wanted recording and some things wanted to just have been.
