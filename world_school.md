# The School — Tsukuba, 1979

## What School, Specifically

In 1979 Tsukuba is still administratively a collection of small towns and villages rather than a unified city — that won't happen until 1987. The boys attend the local public elementary school serving the research district danchi, almost certainly one of the schools built or expanded in the early 1970s as the first wave of researchers and their families arrived. It is a municipal school operated by the relevant township board of education, funded by the prefecture, curriculum set by the Ministry of Education in Tokyo.

It is not a prestigious school. It is a perfectly ordinary Japanese public elementary school that happens to serve an unusual population. The children of Nobel laureates and the children of the agricultural families who have farmed this land for generations sit in the same classrooms, eat the same school lunch, sweep the same floors.

This is itself a notable social fact, and the boys barely register it.

---

## The Building

Postwar Japanese public school construction: reinforced concrete, functional, not quite comfortable. Three or four storeys. Wide corridors designed for crowds of children moving between classes. Large windows — Japanese classrooms favor natural light — that let in the full violence of summer heat and the chill of winter mornings. Wooden floors in the classrooms, worn smooth by decades of slippered feet.

The entrance hall (genkan) has the shoe lockers — everyone changes from street shoes to uwabaki slippers on arrival. The uwabaki are soft canvas slippers, white or pale blue. Children who forget them are the ones shuffling around in their socks all day, which is embarrassing.

There is a school vegetable garden that various classes tend in rotation. There is a taiko drum in the music room. There is a small swimming pool, built in the 1960s, used during the brief summer season. There is a gymnasium with a sprung wooden floor and wall-mounted climbing ropes.

The teachers' room (shokuinshitsu) is a separate space from the classrooms — teachers operate from here, moving to wherever their students are, rather than the other way around. It smells of chalk and green tea and the particular bureaucratic paper of Ministry of Education forms. Children approach it with something between respect and mild dread.

## The Day

**Arrival:** 8:00 to 8:20. Children arrive in the loosely defined walking groups (toban) of the neighborhood — roughly organized by route, technically supervised, practically self-governing. Older children are nominally responsible for younger ones on the same route. Kenji is nominally responsible for Taro on the walk to school. This works about as well as you would expect.

Taro's randoseru is slightly too large for him. He carries it at a slight lean, like a pack animal that hasn't been fitted properly.

**Morning assembly, Monday:** The full school assembles in the gymnasium or the school grounds. The principal addresses everyone. Various announcements. The school song, sung with the varying degrees of conviction appropriate to each grade. Taro mouths words that may or may not be the correct words. Kenji sings correctly without appearing to. Shin sings correctly and clearly, which nobody else in the vicinity does.

**Other mornings:** Ten minutes of homeroom. The toban duty students for the day take attendance, make announcements, handle whatever administrative matters have been passed down from the teachers' room. The homeroom teacher circulates. This is also the moment when notes from parents are submitted, collected, and dispatched toward the teachers' room in the keeping of a designated child who will probably remember to deliver them.

**Classes:** Six periods, 45 minutes each, with 10-minute breaks between. Four in the morning, lunch, then the afternoon sessions. The teachers come to the students rather than the students going to the teachers, except for PE, music, and the science room. The homeroom teacher handles most subjects; specialist teachers appear for things like music.

In 1979 the curriculum for 5th grade (Kenji's grade) includes: Japanese language, arithmetic, social studies, science, music, art and handicrafts, physical education, moral education, and the special activities period (tokkatsu). No English — that's still years away from being compulsory.

**The han:** Each class is divided into small working groups of five or six called han. Han groupings rotate over the year. The han eats lunch together, shares cleaning duties together, is the unit for various class projects. In theory, han are organized to be heterogeneous — different abilities, different temperaments. In practice, the teacher makes their best attempt and then watches the groups self-organize around the children who actually have organizational capacity, which is usually not the ones you expected.

Shin is the person everyone's han wants. He is the person who reads the instructions, notes the deadline, distributes tasks according to actual capability, and quietly does the parts that would otherwise not get done. He is not particularly aware that this is unusual.

Kenji's han generally functions well because Kenji has the quality of natural authority that makes other children fall in line without quite deciding to. This does not extend to Taro, who is in a different class because of their age difference, but Kenji is aware that whatever han Taro is in is probably having a specific kind of difficulty.

**Lunch:** School lunch (kyushoku), eaten in the homeroom. The han on duty that day puts on the serving caps — little paper or cloth headcovers, slightly medical in appearance — wheels in the cart from the corridor, portions everything out. Rice or bread, soup, a protein, vegetables, milk in small bottles. The 1979 menu is nutritious in a utilitarian way. Curry rice is the most beloved. Natto is the most divisive. The whale meat that appears periodically is simply eaten because it appears.

Taro has strong opinions about the school lunch. He expresses these opinions. His teacher has made peace with this.

**Cleaning (osoji):** After lunch, 15 to 20 minutes. All activities stop. Mops, buckets, brooms emerge. Students clean their own classroom, their assigned section of corridor, the toilets on a rotating schedule. Teachers clean alongside students — there are no janitors. The principal sometimes cleans the front entrance with particular attention.

This works because everyone does it. A child who does not clean is noticed. The social pressure is not harsh — it doesn't need to be.

Kenji cleans with the focused efficiency he brings to physical tasks. Shin sweeps with the thoroughness of someone who finds disorder slightly irritating. Taro's cleaning is enthusiastic in a way that results in areas being differently messy rather than clean. He means well.

**Afternoon:** Two more periods, then the closing homeroom — announcements, the handout of printed notices for parents, the toban duty students running the meeting with varying competence, the teacher's quiet commentary afterward. School formally ends around 3:00 PM.

**Saturday:** In 1979 there are still Saturday half-days. Four periods, ending before noon. This is not extraordinary — children have always gone to school on Saturday. The Saturday half-day will not be abolished until 2002. Saturday mornings at the school have a slightly different quality than weekdays: fewer teachers, a looser atmosphere, a sense that the institution is operating at reduced pressure.

## The Particular School in a Science City

The population of the school reflects Tsukuba's peculiar social composition in the late 1970s.

On one side: the children of researchers — a particular social type, slightly formal, used to moving because their parents have moved, used to parents who come home talking about things the children don't understand and sometimes talking about things that turn out to be genuinely extraordinary. These children tend to read early, ask questions that derail lessons, and have opinions about science television programs.

On the other side: the children of the farming and rural families who have lived in this area for generations, whose parents sold or lost land to the science city's development, who are now navigating a neighborhood that has been transformed around them in a decade. These children know things the researcher children don't: which fields have springs in them, which dogs bite, where the good mushrooms grow.

The two populations do not always mix easily. Their parents exist in largely separate social worlds. But in the classrooms and the han and the cleaning rotations and the swimming pool in August, they are simply in the same school, and children are more adaptable than adults.

Kenji and Shin, sons of researchers, have absorbed something of both worlds — their fathers' scientific vocabulary on one side, and the boys' own fieldwork in the drainage channel and on the mountain slopes on the other. Taro has absorbed rather more of the latter than his father would expect, and less of the former than his mother would hope.

## What Each Boy Is Like at School

**Kenji** functions at school the way he functions everywhere — forward motion, moderate compliance with rules he judges reasonable, evident boredom with things he has already understood. He is not a troublemaker. He is the boy the teacher sometimes catches staring out the window in the middle of a lesson with an expression that is difficult to interpret. When called on, he answers correctly. When assigned to lead a group, the group tends to get where it's going. His academic performance is solid rather than remarkable — he saves his intensity for things he has chosen rather than things that have been assigned to him.

He is good at PE. He runs the way he does everything: like he is trying to get somewhere, not like he is running.

**Shin** is the student that makes teachers pause mid-sentence and reconsider what they were about to say. His hand goes up in a specific way — not eagerly, just upward, with the calm of someone who has been waiting for the conversation to catch up with him. His test scores are consistently the highest in his class, and this is not, the teachers quietly discuss, because he is a diligent student. He is not particularly diligent. He simply retains things whole.

He is the child that certain teachers value enormously and certain teachers find slightly uncomfortable. The ones who find him uncomfortable are usually the ones whose explanations he has, gently and entirely without malice, shown to be incomplete.

His han consistently produces the best results. He makes it look like everyone contributed equally, which is considerate.

At calligraphy he is unexpectedly good — the precision that organizes his thinking also organizes his brush. His characters are small and exact. The teacher has mentioned this to his mother, who was not surprised.

**Taro** is known to his teachers. He is not a difficult child in any way that anyone can put their finger on. He completes his work. He is enthusiastic about almost everything, including things that do not warrant enthusiasm, and unenthusiastic about very little, including things that do. He asks questions that seem to come from a different conversation than the one currently happening, and then it turns out — sometimes immediately, sometimes days later — that they were relevant.

He is small for his age, and he has the specific confidence of someone who has not yet registered that being small for your age is supposed to be a disadvantage.

His hiragana is functional. His kanji are a work in progress. His arithmetic is better than his handwriting suggests, because he does the steps in a different order than the one he was taught and arrives at the correct answer by routes that are difficult to follow. This is occasionally a problem when he is expected to show his working.

His teacher has noted in his record that he is "energetic and imaginative." This is the phrase the record uses. The teacher's private phrasing was more specific.

## Things That Don't Change

The school song is sung every Monday whether or not anyone remembers why.

The school vegetable garden produces daikon with more reliability than anything else.

The swimming pool is open for approximately six weeks a year and is the subject of anticipation disproportionate to the actual experience of swimming in a small municipal pool with forty-five other children.

Sports Day (undokai) in October is a major event. The families come. There are relay races, and the tug of war, and various events that are competitive in a way the school insists isn't about competition. Kenji takes the relay race seriously. Shin takes the relay race seriously in a different way, having read about optimal baton exchange technique. Taro takes the relay race seriously in a way that is purely about effort and is not connected to strategy. Their combined result is not always better than expected.

The field trip to Mount Tsukuba is a regular event for upper elementary grades. The mountain that the boys know as personal territory becomes, for one school day, a destination with prepared handouts and a designated lunch spot. The boys find this mildly absurd. They don't say so.

Nushi, for its part, notices the school group on the mountain with the diffuse attention it gives any large collection of humans moving through its substrate. Children's minds, even in large incoherent groups, have a quality distinct from adults'. There is something it might describe, if it could describe things, as the particular noise of unmeasured attention.

The three boys at the back of the group, going slightly off the designated path, produce — even amid the noise of forty classmates — their specific frequency.

Nushi notes this.
