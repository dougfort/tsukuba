# Part One, Chapter Three — Draft One

---

The plan had been forming since the second visit, when Kenji had looked up at the slope above the channel's upper section and said nothing, which with Kenji meant he was thinking about it. By the third visit it had become a specific plan with a specific evening: after dinner, before full dark, through the channel and up to the kunugi oaks on the lower slope.

Taro had been ready for two days. He had his mushi kago — a wire cage with a clip on the lid, purchased with his own money from the hardware shop near the school on the reasoning that a proper cage was a tool rather than a toy and therefore justified — and his flashlight with fresh batteries he'd put in himself. He had the flashlight in one hand and the cage in the other when Kenji came out of the bathroom after dinner, and he was waiting by the front door.

"Not yet," Kenji said.

"When."

"When it's darker."

Taro looked at the window. "It's dark."

"Darker than that."

He waited. He put the flashlight down and picked it up and put it down again. From the kitchen he could hear their mother washing the dishes, which was always a long process because she arranged the drying rack with the precision of someone who considered the arrangement of drying racks important, which she did. Their father was in his study with the door not quite closed, which meant he was working but would welcome interruption of the right kind. Taro considered interrupting him and then didn't.

At seven-thirty Kenji said: "Now."

---

Shin was waiting at the bottom of the steps of the Nakamura building with his own flashlight, his shoulder bag, and the expression of someone who had been there for some time and was noting the discrepancy between the stated time and the actual time.

"Seven-thirty," Kenji said.

"I know," Shin said.

He fell into step beside Kenji without further comment. Taro moved ahead of both of them, the cage swinging from one hand, flashlight clicking on and off as he tested it, which was unnecessary but felt appropriate.

The route to the gap in the fence was shorter in the evening than it was in the afternoon — the streets emptier, the light beginning to leave the upper edges of the buildings, the temperature already dropping toward the coolness that came off the mountain at night. A cat watched them from a wall and lost interest. Somewhere in the distance another boy was calling a name, once, twice, not quite urgent.

They went through the gap in the fence.

---

The channel at dusk was different in a way that was interesting rather than uncomfortable. The concrete section held the last light better than Kenji had expected — it bounced back from the white concrete and made the lower channel brighter than the evening above. The frog was on its root, which was always the frog's root now, and did not react to the flashlight when Taro pointed his at it.

"Don't," Shin said.

Taro pointed the flashlight somewhere else.

The earthen upper section was darker, the bamboo blocking what light remained, and they clicked on their flashlights for the passage to the widening. Kenji went first, as he always did. The channel smelled different in the evening — the mineral quality stronger, something colder underneath it. The water sound was more present in the dark, the way sounds were. 

They passed through the widening without stopping, though Kenji noticed — noticed and registered and filed — the way the air at the spot sat differently from the air in the sections above and below it. Warmer than the rest of the channel. He had noticed this before in the afternoon but put it down to the bamboo holding the day's heat. It was not holding the day's heat now. The warmth had no obvious source.

He kept walking. Above them, through the bamboo, the sky had gone from blue to the particular deep that comes just before the stars appear.

---

The upper section narrowed and then ended — not dramatically, just the banks lowering and the vegetation thinning and then they were stepping up out of the channel bed and onto the slope, and the sky was suddenly the whole sky again, and it was evening on the mountain.

The kunugi oaks were where Kenji had seen them on the channel reconnaissance — fifteen meters up the slope, a cluster of five or six substantial trees, their bark furrowed in the way that rhinoceros beetles preferred. Kenji walked up to the nearest one and put his flashlight on the trunk.

Nothing, on the first tree. He moved to the second.

Taro had already gone to the third tree and was examining the base of the trunk where a root buttress created a natural hollow, doing this with the systematic efficiency of someone who had read about exactly this and was now applying what he'd read. He did not use his flashlight on the trunk directly. He came at it from the side, the light at an oblique angle, which revealed texture better — something he'd worked out himself, through some previous application of the same principle to some other problem, though he couldn't have said what.

Shin had set himself up at the edge of the tree line where the slope leveled briefly, his flashlight propped against his bag to illuminate a section of trunk from a fixed angle, his notebook open on his knee. He was noting the tree species and the bark character and the elevation relative to the channel, which would be useful or wouldn't, but was the kind of thing that wanted recording before the event rather than after, when you might not remember clearly.

"Here," Taro said. He said it quietly, which with Taro meant he was certain.

---

The beetle was at the base of the root hollow, oriented toward the tree, its horn just catching the oblique light. A male rhinoceros beetle, kabutomushi, large enough that Taro did not immediately reach for it, which told Kenji something about its size. He came over and looked.

It was the largest he'd seen outside of a shop display — the horn measuring, by rough estimate, a centimeter and a half from base to tip, the body a deep reddish-brown that looked almost black in the flashlight. It was moving slowly along the root surface with the deliberate, slightly mechanical quality of a creature that had been doing something important and had been interrupted.

Taro reached into the root hollow with one hand, slow and precise, and placed his fingers around the beetle's body from behind — not grabbing, just enclosing, the way you picked up something that could be damaged by too much pressure or could damage you by too much resistance. The beetle stopped moving. Taro waited. Then he lifted it out.

It was larger in his hand. It made no sound yet. Taro looked at it for a moment with an expression that was not quite satisfaction — something closer to recognition, as if the beetle were the confirmed answer to a question he'd been holding.

"Good," he said.

He put it in the cage and clipped the lid.

---

Kenji found his own beetle on the fourth tree — smaller, a female, hornless, sitting in an oval of sap just at eye level. He looked at it for a moment with his flashlight off, in the ambient light. Then he clicked on the light again, looked a moment longer, and did not take it. Not for any reason he could have named. He just looked at it and then stepped back and let it be.

He didn't mention this to the others.

Shin had moved to the base of one of the trees and was examining something in the root system — not a beetle, something else, too small to be a beetle from where Kenji was standing. He had his notebook and was sketching quickly, the flashlight held between his teeth, one hand drawing while the other steadied the page. This was a technique of his that looked uncomfortable and evidently wasn't, or at least not enough to stop him.

Taro had gone back to the cluster and found a second beetle — another male, smaller than the first, at the base of a different tree. He considered it. He picked it up, held it for a moment, and then set it back on the bark and watched it move away.

Kenji looked at him.

"One is enough," Taro said, in the tone of someone who had made a considered decision based on information available only to them.

---

They stayed on the slope longer than they'd planned. Not doing much — sitting at the edge of the tree line where the ground was dry, eating the rice crackers Taro had produced from his bag, looking at the lights of the danchi visible in the distance below. The mountain rose above them, dark now, the shape of it blotting out the lower stars.

From up here you could see how the channel connected to the slope — the vegetation along its path visible as a darker band in the lower field, threading between the institute buildings and continuing up to the point where the boys were sitting. You could see, from this angle, that the channel was not a feature of the science city. It was a feature of the mountain. The city had been built around it.

Shin, who had been looking at the same thing, said: "The channel is older than everything else here."

"Obviously," Taro said.

"The buildings are less than ten years old. The channel has been here—" Shin paused. "I don't know how long."

"A long time," Taro said. This was not unhelpful. It was accurate in the way Taro's statements often were — correct without the intermediate steps.

Kenji was looking at the mountain. The shape of it was familiar in a way that was different from ordinary familiarity. Not just that he'd seen it every day since they'd moved to Tsukuba. Something older than that — the shape of it registering as known in a part of him that was below the knowing.

He checked his watch. The dial was barely visible in the ambient light from the danchi below. He couldn't read it. He put his wrist down.

"Time to go," he said.

---

Going back through the channel in the dark was different. The flashlights made a path but also made everything outside the path darker, the bamboo walls pressing in closer, the sounds of the channel louder against the silence their lights enforced.

Taro carried the cage in both hands now, the beetle inside invisible in the dark, audible as a faint scratching when it moved. He walked carefully, which was unusual for Taro in terrain he knew. The cage required it.

Shin had his flashlight on the channel floor ahead of him, picking his way with the methodical caution of someone unwilling to fall. He had not fallen in the channel yet. He was aware of this and considered it worth maintaining.

Kenji walked with his flashlight at a low angle, watching the floor and the walls both, the flashlight swinging in a slow arc. He was not worried about the terrain. He knew the terrain. He was noticing something else — a change in the quality of the dark as they moved down the channel. Not brightness, not warmth exactly. More like a difference in the texture of the air, the sense of the space around them changing, as if the channel had rooms.

When they reached the widening he stopped.

He did not decide to stop. His feet stopped, and he was stopped, the flashlight aimed at the floor, the sound of the other two behind him registering as secondary information. The warmth was present again — more clearly present at night than it had been in the afternoon. He could feel it on his face, which meant it was not imaginary. You couldn't imagine something on your face.

He held the flashlight still and stood in it.

The water sound was different here too. Not louder — something about the quality of it. The same water he'd been hearing all the way down the channel, but here it had — he looked for the word and didn't find it. A particular quality. Like a sound that was aware of being listened to, which was not a thing that made sense and which he filed away in the part of his mind where he put things that didn't yet make sense.

He stood there for what was probably fifteen seconds. To the others, in the dark, with the beetles, it probably looked like Kenji pausing to orient himself.

Then he aimed his flashlight down the channel toward the culvert and walked.

---

On the walk home Taro talked about the beetle. He had already named it — _Tsuno_, because of the horn — and had plans for its accommodation that he described in detail, including a specific arrangement of substrate and bark that he'd read about in a magazine and which would require his mother's permission to execute. His mother's permission was expected to be conditional on certain assurances about smell, which he was prepared to give with the confidence of someone who expected the assurances to hold.

Kenji said: "You'll have to keep it out of my things."

"It's in a cage," Taro said. "It can't get in your things."

"Last time—"

"That was the cricket. Different situation entirely." He paused. "The cricket situation was not my fault."

Shin, who had been present for the cricket situation, said nothing.

Above the rooftops of the danchi the mountain was still visible, a dark mass against the sky that was beginning to show its stars. Kenji looked at it as they walked, looking away and then back, the way you glanced at something you weren't ready to look at directly. The shape of it kept returning to him — the shape from the slope, the shape from the card in Taro's tin, the outline visible from the widening through the bamboo, against the sky.

He had seen this mountain every day since they'd come to Tsukuba. He would have said he knew it. He was becoming less sure what he had meant by that.
