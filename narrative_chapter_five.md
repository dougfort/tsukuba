# Part One, Chapter Five — Draft One

---

The channel in November was a different country from the channel in August, and Shin had been thinking about how to describe the difference since the first cold weekend visit in late October when he'd stood at the spot and felt it shift in his understanding from one kind of place to another.

It wasn't that the channel became less. It was that it became more clearly itself — stripped of the summer abundance that had, he now saw, been a kind of decoration, however beautiful. The miscanthus had dropped its plumes. The bamboo had kept its color but lost the fronds' summer weight, and the light came through differently, finding the floor in shafts rather than the filtered general glow of the green months. The smell had changed again, from the soil-turnover smell of October to something cleaner, older: stone and cold water and the mineral quality that had been underneath everything since August, now present without competition.

The base flow ran thin and continuous.

"It's still dry," Taro said, about the floor. He said this with the satisfaction of someone who had predicted a thing and been proved correct, though he had not, in fact, made this prediction. He crouched and pressed his palm to the ground at the widening, which was something he did now without seeming to notice he was doing it.

Kenji was standing at the upper end of the spot, looking at the channel where it narrowed and continued into the section above. He was doing the same thing he'd been doing on the last few visits: looking at it. Not moving toward it. Just looking.

Shin wrote the date, wrote *first winter visit — base flow present, floor condition unchanged, bamboo green,* and then looked up at the mountain visible above the bamboo in the grey November sky.

He had looked at this mountain every day for more than a year. He knew its silhouette the way he knew the shape of his own hands. But lately — since sometime in October, he couldn't say exactly when — the looking had acquired a quality it hadn't had before. Not different information. A different kind of attention, on both sides.

He wrote nothing further about this. He closed the notebook.

"Cold," Taro said, not as a complaint but as an observation, pressing his other palm to the ground and then standing up and putting both hands in his pockets. "Not as cold as it should be, though. Here."

Shin looked at him.

"I mean it's warmer at the spot than it is in the section below," Taro said. "Has been every time."

"Yes," Shin said.

Taro nodded, apparently satisfied with the confirmation. He didn't follow it further. That was the thing about Taro — he noticed, stated, and moved on, as if the noticing were its own sufficient thing.

*Warmer than it should be, as usual,* Shin wrote, when Taro had moved off toward the menko stone to assess its winter condition. *Taro notes the differential unprompted. Third time.*

He capped his pen and looked at the mountain again. Whatever he wanted to write about the quality of looking, he still didn't have the words. He let it be.

---

The dreams began in December.

He couldn't say when exactly because the first ones arrived so gently that he didn't recognize them as different until they had already established themselves as a pattern. He had always been a vivid dreamer. The distinction between ordinary vivid dreaming and what was happening now was not immediately obvious — only in retrospect, looking back from January, did the December ones acquire their particular character.

What distinguished them was not their content. The content dissolved, every time, with the swiftness and completeness of a soap bubble disappearing — not gradually, not in fragments, but all at once, gone before he was fully awake. What remained was not images or narrative or even impressions in any ordinary sense. What remained was a quality. A residue.

Warmth, was the closest word. Not the warmth of the futon or the room but something that had nothing to do with temperature — a warmth that belonged to scale rather than heat, the warmth of something vast being, in some sense, hospitable. Depth. The specific sensation of having been, for the duration of the dream, at the edge of something very large, looking in rather than away from it.

And something else, harder to name: the sense of having been attended to.

He turned this over carefully in those early morning minutes while the dream was still fresh and the household still quiet. *Attended to.* Not observed, exactly, and not watched. Something more precise than both. The quality of attention you felt from someone who was genuinely interested in you, who had been waiting for you to arrive, who received your presence as something that mattered.

He was nine years old and he had no framework for this and it did not frighten him. He noted this fact about himself with some interest. It did not frighten him. The opposite, if anything. He lay in the dark and held the residue and was aware that the most significant thing that had ever happened to him was, apparently, happening to him.

He did not reach for the notebook. The field-notes register would make it smaller. He knew this with the same certainty that had told him, in October, that the sitting in the last light wanted to just have been. Some things required the notebook and some things required him to simply be equal to them, without recording.

He was not sure yet whether he was equal to this one. But the effort seemed correct.

---

His father's study smelled of books and old paper and the ghost of a pipe his father rarely smoked inside the house. It had other smells underneath: the specific mineral smell of the geological samples on the windowsill, the slight dustiness of the older volumes on the top shelves, the clean oil of the desk lamp that had been running since before Shin was born. The room was a record of his father's intellectual life in the same way that Shin's notebook was a record of the channel, and he navigated it with the particular care he gave to things that mattered.

He was looking for the entomology guide — specifically the Kanto-region volume that he believed was on the lower shelves, the one that might have ground beetles from unusual microhabitats. He worked along the bottom shelf systematically, reading spines.

He found the entomology guide. He also found, wedged beside it, a thin volume he hadn't noticed before: a regional folklore study from the 1950s, its spine faded to illegibility, the title restored only when he tilted it toward the lamplight. *Ibaraki no Minzoku to Shizen* — something about the folk traditions and natural features of Ibaraki Prefecture.

He looked at it for a moment. He put the entomology guide on the floor beside the chair and took the folklore volume to the corner.

The chapter on the Tsukubasan Shrine was near the back. It was dry in the way of regional folklore scholarship from that period — careful, slightly defensive, as if the author expected to be questioned about why this subject merited academic attention. But within it, quoted in part and referenced in full, were several poems from the Man'yoshu that mentioned the mountain.

He read them.

He was a methodical reader and this was not, at first, different from any other reading — he went through the first poem carefully, noted the vocabulary, moved to the second. But by the second poem something had changed in the quality of his attention, a settling and sharpening that he recognized as the reading-state Nushi was, without Shin's knowledge, finding increasingly legible.

The poems were about the mountain. He had understood this before beginning them. But the mountain in the poems was not a backdrop or a landmark or even a subject in the ordinary sense. The mountain in the poems was a threshold.

*筑波嶺の 彼面此面に 守部据ゑ 母い守れども 魂ぞ会ひにける*

He worked through the classical Japanese slowly. His classical Japanese was imperfect but serviceable — he had been teaching himself from a grammar in the school library since the beginning of the year, for reasons he hadn't examined closely. *On every face of Tsukuba-yama, though guardians are posted, though the mother keeps her watch — still, in dreams, our souls have met.*

He read this and was very still.

He did not think: *this is my dream.* He was too careful for that. He did not leap to connections that hadn't been established to his satisfaction. But the poem's logic — the mountain as a place where the waking world's barriers became permeable, where meeting happened that couldn't happen otherwise, where souls went in sleep to the place that sleep made possible — sat alongside what he knew without yet resolving into anything he could write down.

It was enough to have found it. It was enough that someone, twelve hundred years ago, had stood near this mountain and known that it did this.

He found a second poem. *筑波嶺に 雪かも降らる 否をかも 愛しき子ろが 布乾さるかも* — this one more oblique, working through images of snow on the peak, uncertainty, longing. The mountain again at the center of something that couldn't be named directly.

He sat with the book closed on his knee for a long time after, in the corner chair, in the lamplight. His father was working at the desk. They were both in the room and not talking, which was a mode of conversation they had — comfortable, each in his own thinking, the room large enough for both. Outside the window the mountain was in the dark. He couldn't see it but he knew the direction of it precisely, the way he knew the direction of north.

After a while he opened his notebook to a new page and copied both poems, slowly and carefully, in his small characters. He left no note explaining why they were there. They went in after the October beetle observation and before a blank page and no note was necessary — or rather, no note he could write would be adequate, and inadequate notes were worse than none.

He went to bed later than usual. His mother noted this without making an issue of it. She brought him tea.

---

The dreams continued through December and into January.

He was learning their texture. Each one was distinct in the way that days in a place are distinct — recognizably the same place, each time differently inflected by weather or angle of approach or the particular quality of attention he brought to the threshold. The warmth. The depth. The attending presence that was interested in him specifically, in a way he could feel even through the dissolution of waking.

He tried, in early January, to approach the threshold differently. Not grasping — he had established quickly that grasping produced nothing but a tense wakefulness that was the opposite of what was needed. Something more like: presenting himself at the edge of sleep with a quality of open attention, the way you stood at the spot in the channel and let its particular character come to you rather than going after it.

He didn't know if this was working. He didn't know what working would mean. But the effort felt right in the same way that careful observation always felt right — not forcing the outcome, just attending to what was there.

One morning in late January, four days after his birthday, he woke with a word in his mind. Clear and specific, held for the three seconds it took his eyes to open, and then gone with everything else. He lay still for a long moment afterward, trying to recover it, knowing already that it was gone.

He couldn't remember the word. He knew it had been the right word for something. This was, he admitted to himself, the most frustrating experience of the entire winter — not the dissolution of the dreams themselves, which he had largely made peace with, but this: having been given something complete and then having it taken, the specific cruelty of a key that fits a lock you can feel but cannot see.

He got up. He wrote in his notebook: *January 30. Something given and taken. Will not grasp. Will try again tomorrow.*

He capped the pen. He thought about the poems.

*Still, in dreams, our souls have met.*

He closed the notebook and went to breakfast.

---

He went back to the study in late January, specifically, which was different from the November visit in the way that all deliberate returns to a place are different from finding it by accident. He was looking for the Hitachi Fudoki, an eighth-century regional chronicle he had found referenced in the folklore study's footnotes, which apparently contained a description of a spring on the lower slopes of the mountain near what might, from the geography, be the fault line.

His father found the Fudoki on the upper shelves — he knew his shelves the way Shin knew the channel, by use and association rather than system — and brought it down and they looked at the passage together. The spring was there: a source noted for its unusual coldness and the clarity of the water, used in antiquity for purification. The location was consistent with the fault system. Shin copied the reference with the precision he brought to all field evidence and thanked his father.

They stayed in the study for a while after. His father made tea. Shin sat in the corner chair and looked at his notes and then looked at the window, beyond which the mountain was just visible against the winter dusk, and thought about how the spring in the Fudoki was probably still there, was probably what fed the base flow he had been measuring since August, was the cold that made the water at the widening different from any other water he'd encountered.

"Otousan," he said.

His father looked up.

"Is there anything that gets more true the less you think about it directly?"

His father was quiet for a moment. He took the question seriously, which Shin had expected — his father always took questions seriously, it was one of the things about him that Shin had understood and relied on since early childhood. He took off his glasses and looked at them and put them back on.

"In seismology," his father said, "there are features of the substrate that don't reveal themselves to direct measurement. You probe them and they look one way. You look at the pattern of everything around them over a long period and they look entirely different. The direct approach sees the surface of the thing. The indirect approach sometimes sees through it." He paused. "I don't know if that's what you're asking about."

"I think it might be," Shin said.

His father nodded once, in the way that meant he had registered something and was keeping it. He went back to his work.

Shin opened his notebook and sat with it closed in his lap for a while. He was thinking about the spring and the fault and the cold water and the warmth at the spot that should not be there and the dreams that would not be held and the poems that twelve hundred years ago knew the mountain did what it was doing. He was thinking about the unidentified beetle that was still unidentified, and about the way Kenji had seen the weasel den entrance when Shin, looking directly at it, had seen only a bank.

Some things revealed themselves to direct attention. Some things required you to look at everything around them for a long time.

He thought he was beginning to understand which kind of thing this was.

---

The December channel visit was brief and cold and good.

Taro had brought the tin. He took out the mountain card and looked at it for a moment — the older graphic style, the familiar outline of the peak — and put it back without comment. Kenji stood at the upper end of the spot and looked up the channel in the direction of the slope. Shin stood at the base flow and listened to the water, which had its almost-rhythm today, the one he had written about in August that was not the stone arrangement, that was becoming — he had come to accept this word — more distinct.

He did not take out his notebook. He stood and listened.

On the walk home he thought about the poems. He thought about the dreams. He thought about his father's description of the substrate that didn't reveal itself to direct measurement. He thought about Taro pressing his palms to the cold ground of the widening without seeming to know he was doing it, and about the way Kenji looked at the upper channel with the expression he wore when he was attending to something he wasn't going to say.

He thought: *we are all doing it. In our different ways. We have all been doing it since August.*

He did not write this down. He had been writing around things all winter. But this one he wanted to sit with until he could see it clearly, and when he could see it clearly he would find the right words, and when he found the right words he would write them, and until then the notebook could wait.

That night he opened the notebook to a fresh page and wrote:

---

December 14

Base flow present. Bamboo green. Frost on the east bank stones, gone by noon. Floor: dry. Warmer at the widening than in the sections above and below.

*筑波嶺の 彼面此面に 守部据ゑ*
*母い守れども 魂ぞ会ひにける*

*筑波嶺に 雪かも降らる 否をかも*
*愛しき子ろが 布乾さるかも*

*In dreams our souls have met.*

What is the frequency of attention?

---

He looked at what he had written for a moment. Then he closed the notebook.
