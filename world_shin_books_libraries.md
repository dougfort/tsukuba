# Where Shin Gets His Books

## The Problem of Books in Tsukuba, 1979

In 1979 Tsukuba is not yet a city. It is a collection of small towns and villages in the process of being transformed by the arrival of forty-six national research institutes and a new university. The administrative infrastructure of an actual city — including a proper municipal library — is still catching up to the population that has been dropped into the Ibaraki farmland.

The municipal public library situation is, in the language of the research on the period, "developing." In 1970 only 70% of Japanese cities had public libraries at all. By 1980 that number would reach 91%, but many of those newly established libraries were modest — a single room, a limited collection, hours that assumed adults rather than children. The Tsukuba area in 1979 has something — probably a branch of the Ibaraki prefectural library system, almost certainly inadequate for a family that reads the way the Nakamuras read.

What Tsukuba does have, in extraordinary abundance, is institutional libraries. The forty-six research institutes each maintain specialist collections. The University of Tsukuba, founded in 1973, has a central library with open stacks — deliberately open, as a design philosophy of the new university — and explicitly welcomes members of the public.

Shin has discovered this. It is one of the more significant facts about his daily life.

## The School Library

The school has a library. It is in a single room on the second floor, maintained by a rotating selection of teachers with the help of students assigned to library duty (figure duty). The collection runs to approximately three thousand volumes — picture books and easy readers for the lower grades at one end, the collected works of approved authors of Japanese children's literature in the middle, and a reference section at the other end with encyclopedias, atlases, and a set of science textbooks one edition behind current.

There is also a modest science fiction shelf. This is somewhat unusual for an elementary school library of the period. It exists because the school serves an unusual population, and the PTA — containing a higher density of scientists than any PTA in Ibaraki Prefecture — made gentle representations to the principal about the importance of speculative literature in the formation of scientific thinking. The principal agreed, or at least did not disagree.

The science fiction shelf contains: Hoshi Shin'ichi's Bokko-chan and other story collections. Komatsu Sakyō's Japan Sinks, which is technically too old for this library and present because someone donated it. Tsutsui Yasutaka, which is also arguably too old for this library. An anthology of translated Western science fiction including Bradbury's Something Wicked This Way Comes, which is absolutely too old for this library and which Shin has read twice. A shelf of the SF Magazine's annual best-of collections, donated by a parent who subscribed and passed them along. A copy of Lem's Solaris in Japanese translation, which has been here since 1975 and which no one has ever checked out except Shin, twice.

Shin has read everything on this shelf. He has opinions about the relative merits of everything on this shelf.

**Shin's relationship with the school library:** Unrestricted access. He serves library duty voluntarily beyond his assigned rotation because this gives him first notice of new acquisitions and proximity to the reference encyclopedias, which he uses for research the other students use for homework. The teacher who manages the library, a woman in her early forties who takes her role seriously, recognized his quality in the first week of school and has since treated him as something between a student assistant and a colleague. She tells him when new science books arrive. He tells her, carefully, when something in the reference section is out of date.

## The Home Library

The Nakamura household contains books the way other households contain furniture — as the primary material of daily life, accumulated over decades, filling the shelves that Takashi built along the study wall and overflowing in organized stacks. The sections are not formally organized but have a logic visible to anyone who understands how a cognitive scientist's mind works: perception here, consciousness there, memory and its disorders, philosophy of mind, anthropology. A separate shelf for things that don't fit the main categories but seemed important. Several boxes of academic journals that have never been unpacked since the move from Tokyo.

The children's books and the books Yuriko bought and the books that arrived as gifts have colonized the living room shelves. These include a complete set of the Kodansha manga encyclopedias — science, world history, Japanese history, nature — which are reference material presented in a format Taro can read and Shin finds useful for quick facts. They include a collection of Japanese folklore and folk story anthologies that were Yuriko's, from her teaching years.

**Shin's relationship with the home library:** Total. He has read everything in the house that was not too technically dense for him, including things he could only partially understand. He started reading his father's professional books around age eight and by ten has a working knowledge of perceptual psychology that occasionally alarms his father's colleagues when it surfaces in conversation. He has read all of Yuriko's folklore collection, which is where he first encountered material about place spirits and shrine traditions in the context of Japanese folk belief.

The Hitachi Fudoki note in his notebook came from a volume in this collection — a scholarly anthology of Kanto regional folklore that contains a translated excerpt describing a spring near Tsukuba used for ritual purification. He copied it into the notebook without being entirely sure why.

## The University of Tsukuba Library

This is the important one.

The University of Tsukuba opened in 1973 with a deliberate policy of being a different kind of Japanese university — more open, more interdisciplinary, more connected to the community around it. The central library was built with open stacks from the start, meaning any visitor can walk directly into the collection and handle the books rather than requesting them through a desk. Members of the public are welcome.

The library building is large, modern, slightly institutional in the way of 1970s Japanese public architecture — concrete, functional, full of natural light. The stacks occupy multiple floors. The children's section is small but present, and the general humanities and science collections are extensive. The reference room has a complete run of scholarly journals in the sciences, a strong folklore and ethnography section (reflecting the university's interdisciplinary ambitions), and the best collection of Man'yoshu scholarship in Ibaraki Prefecture.

**How Shin got access:** Nakamura Takashi holds an institutional affiliation. He can borrow books and uses the library regularly for his research. He brought Shin once — Shin was eight — because Shin had exhausted the home library on a specific topic and Takashi thought, without quite deciding to think it, that this seemed like the right thing to do.

Shin's response to the university library was the response of someone who had been living in a small house and was shown, for the first time, that houses could be very large. He stood in the stacks for several minutes not touching anything. Then he began reading the spine labels.

Takashi now has a semi-official arrangement with the reference librarian, a woman named Watanabe-san, who understands that a faculty member's ten-year-old son will appear on Saturday mornings and use the reading room. She has an unofficial policy of guiding him toward things he might not have found on his own. She has never spoken to Takashi about this arrangement and Takashi has never asked.

**What Shin uses it for:** He comes on Saturday mornings after the school half-day, if his mother agrees and the weather is not interesting enough to go to the channel instead. He sits in the reading room — the good table by the window, second from the left — and reads for two or three hours. He does not check books out, because that would require more explanation of the arrangement than currently exists. He reads in the library and takes notes.

His notes from these sessions are written in the notebook in a compressed academic style that is slightly different from his other entries — smaller characters, more abbreviations, the shorthand of someone who has noticed that his notebooks fill up.

**Specific things he has found here that matter to the story:**
- A scholarly edition of the Man'yoshu with commentary, which is where the Tsukuba poems became not just text but annotated text — he could read what scholars said the poems meant, and compare this to what he thought they meant, and note where these diverged
- A volume on Kanto regional shrine traditions, which contained material on the kannagi lineage at Tsukubasan Shrine that he has not been able to find summarized anywhere else
- A folklore and ethnography journal that ran a 1971 article on iwakura and geological sacred sites, which he read twice and from which he copied a passage about the relationship between underground water and sacred ground before he understood why this seemed important
- Yanagita Kunio's collected works — he has not read all of these, but he has read the sections on place spirits and water spirits, which is where Nushi fully crystallized as a concept before he had the word

**Watanabe-san's role:** She is in her late thirties, careful, precise in the way of someone who has organized knowledge professionally for years. She has a policy, developed with Shin specifically in mind, of leaving things on the reading room table — placing a journal open to a relevant article when she has stacked the returns, leaving a book slightly out of alignment on the shelf when she notices he is working in that section. She does not do this obviously. She is not entirely sure why she does it. It feels like the correct thing. Shin has never thanked her for it directly, because doing so would require acknowledging it, and acknowledgment would change the nature of the thing.

## The Institute Libraries

The forty-six national research institutes in Tsukuba each maintain specialist collections. These are, in the first instance, closed — for researchers and affiliated personnel. But the science city was designed with collaboration in mind, and the social reality of a community in which everyone is connected to everyone by one or two relationships means that a child with the right parent can occasionally gain access.

Nakamura Takashi's institute library is the one Shin knows best. It has nothing Shin wants for the Nushi project — it is a cognitive science collection, strong on perception research, weak on folklore. But it is where he has become comfortable with the idea of institutional libraries, with the smell of professional collections, with the experience of walking into a building full of things humans have learned and written down.

Hiroshi's earth sciences institute library is more interesting, and Shin has been there once. It has a geology section that includes historical surveys of the Kanto plain — some quite old, some recent, some including maps. He did not take notes from this visit. He has thought about going back.

Whether Shin ever finds, in the earth sciences collection, survey materials that touch on the underground anomaly's geological history is an open story question.

## The Bookmobile and the Kodomo Bunko

In areas underserved by public libraries, 1970s Japan developed two informal solutions. Bookmobiles — small trucks fitted with shelves — ran circuits through suburban and rural neighborhoods on fixed schedules. And kodomo bunko (home libraries for children) emerged from neighborhood mothers who collected books and opened their living rooms on Saturday afternoons.

There is probably a kodomo bunko somewhere in the danchi, run by a mother who was a librarian in her previous life. Yuriko knows about it. She has contributed books to it. She has, in the gentle way she has with these things, suggested to Shin that he might visit. He went once, found the collection limited by the university library's standards, and has not gone back — not dismissively, but with the pragmatic calculation of a reader who has three hours on a Saturday and knows where the best books are.

Taro has been to the kodomo bunko twice. He took out a book about rhinoceros beetles both times. It may have been the same book.

## A Note on What This Means for the Story

Shin's book sources are not just logistical facts. They map his intellectual world and the adults who have, without quite deciding to, given him access to it.

The school library teacher recognized him and treated him accordingly. Watanabe-san at the university library has quietly guided him toward things he needed without his asking. His father brought him to the library at eight years old and has since left the arrangement alone, which is its own form of trust. His mother's collection of folklore books was waiting in the living room when he needed it.

The word *Nushi*, when Shin says it, does not come from nowhere. It comes from Yanagita Kunio in the university reading room on a Saturday morning, from his mother's folklore anthology in the living room, from the Tsukuba poems in the annotated Man'yoshu, from the shrine history journal article that Watanabe-san may or may not have positioned slightly out of alignment on the reference shelf. It comes from years of reading with attention in places that were made quietly available to him.

This is what libraries do. Shin is an excellent argument for them.
