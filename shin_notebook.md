# Shin's Notebook

_A composition notebook, dark blue cover, 18cm x 25cm. Name inside front cover: Nakamura Shin. Below it: vol. 3._

_Conventions: dates flush right. Sketches noted in brackets. Italics for entries where thought has outrun the notation._

***

## Summer 1979

***

August 3

\[sketch: bamboo profile, east bank — culms in single strokes, fronds indicated, strip of sky above, mountain outline at upper right]

New location. Drainage channel behind Institute #2 — access via gap in retaining wall, south end. Approx. 150m total. Lower 60m: concrete lining. Upper section: earthen, overgrown, banks approx. 90cm. Bamboo on east bank. Susuki and dokudami on west. Floor: dark silt, mineral, firmer than expected. Base flow present and cold — groundwater, not rainfall runoff.

Widening at approx. 30m into upper section. East bank platform, possibly old field boundary remnant. West bank susuki roots form natural seat. Floor here is the driest section. Air cooler than above. Smell: water, mineral, dokudami underneath, and something else — older, I don't have a word for it yet.

Sound: continuous. Water, insects, bamboo in slight air movement — makes a sound like paper. Frogs heard, not seen. Acoustically enclosed.

\[sketch: unidentified beetle, west bank miscanthus base — black, approx. 18mm, distinctive pronotal shape — three views with question mark]

_the beetle was gone before I could get a proper look. will check again._

Taro put his hands flat on the ground at the widening. Not sure why I wrote that.

***

August 6

\[sketch: tokusa — three stems, nodes indicated, one stem disassembled at joints beside it for scale]

Third visit. Menko stone established — flat granite, approx. 40 x 30cm, levelled by K. over two sessions with smaller stones as shims. Stable. Taro has already lost four cards and won six. Playing surface is slightly irregular along the north edge; Taro claims this is an advantage and may be right.

Tokusa (*Equisetum*) growing in dense stands at the fault margin, upper channel. Unchanged since the Carboniferous — approx. 300 million years. The oldest living thing here by a significant margin. Taro stepped on some. It comes apart at the nodes and reassembles. He did this several times.

\[sketch: unidentified beetle, second observation — dorsal view, improved — pronotal angles clearer, elytral striations faint but present, approx. 19mm, legs now included]

Beetle again. Same individual or same species — west bank, this time under a stone rather than at the miscanthus base. Better light. Better look. Elytra dark with a faint blue-green iridescence not visible in low light — this is why the first sketch missed it. Pronotal shape still doesn't match anything in the guide. Not a ground beetle I recognise. Not a rove beetle. Possibly something I don't have a book for.

_will try the library._

Base flow: still cold. Measured approx. temp with hand — significantly colder than ambient. Consistent with deep groundwater source rather than surface drainage. The smell at the widening is present again. Still no word for it.

Sound of water at the widening has a rhythm. Probably the stone arrangement upstream. Probably.

***

August 9

\[sketch: view from lower slope, looking south — channel visible as darker vegetation band crossing the institute grounds, buildings to either side, danchi rooflines at lower left, plain of Tsukuba beyond; mountain outline omitted, present above the frame]

First time on the slope above the channel. Elevation gain approx. 25m from channel bed to kunugi stand. Slope angle moderate, footing good — dry leaf litter over mineral soil, different composition from channel floor. Cooler than the danchi by several degrees even at this elevation.

Vegetation transition: channel margin to slope — susuki and dokudami give way to mixed woodland. Oak dominant: kunugi (*Quercus acutissima*) and konara (*Q. serrata*) from approx. 10m above channel exit. Bark furrowed, sap runs at several points on older specimens. Correct habitat for kabutomushi and kuwagata.

From the kunugi stand the channel is visible below as a band of denser vegetation crossing the institute grounds — follows its own line independent of the buildings, older than them. Not a feature of the science city. A feature of the mountain.

\[sketch: *Allomyrina dichotoma* male — Taro's specimen — dorsal and lateral views, horn length indicated, approx. 68mm body, reddish-brown notation in margin]

Kabutomushi, male, large specimen. Located by T. at base of root hollow, third kunugi from south. Method: oblique flashlight angle along root surface — effective. Horn approx. 15mm. Now resident on bedroom windowsill in wire cage. Named *Tsuno* by T., which is accurate if not technical.

One smaller male noted and released by T. at adjacent tree. No comment given.

K. examined female specimen on fourth tree. Did not take it. Also no comment.

_something about tonight that I am going to write around._

Return through channel in dark. Passed through widening approx. 21:10. Warmer than the channel sections above and below — measurable difference, not marginal. This has been true on every visit but is more apparent in the dark when you cannot attribute it to sun on the bamboo.

The water sound at the widening: the rhythm again. I stood still for a moment to listen. It is not the stone arrangement. The stones have not moved and the sound is not the same as on the sixth. It is not getting louder. It is becoming more — I don't have the right word. Distinct, possibly. Like it is trying to be heard.

_I notice I wrote 'trying'. I don't think water tries._

K. stopped at the widening for approximately fifteen seconds. I don't know what he was doing. He didn't say anything. Then he walked.
