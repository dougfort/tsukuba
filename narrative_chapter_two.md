# Part One, Chapter Two — Draft One

---

They went back the next day, and the day after that.

This was not unusual. Kenji had a method with new places: he returned until the place was ordinary, until he had walked it enough times that it gave up its surprises and settled into known territory. The channel, after three visits, had not yet become ordinary. He kept expecting it to and it kept not doing it, which was its own kind of interesting.

The second visit was reconnaissance. Kenji walked the length of the accessible channel twice while Taro examined the playing stone he'd selected and Shin crouched at the upper margin taking notes on something. On the walk back down Kenji stopped at a section of bank where a weasel had pushed through the vegetation and left a clean oval gap at ground level. He crouched and looked through it without touching anything and then stood up and continued. He didn't mention it. There would be a right time to mention it and this wasn't it.

The third visit was the day Taro brought the tin.

---

The tin was his father's, or had been — a flat tobacco tin with a dent in one corner from a fall Taro had no memory of causing. It was painted dark green and had a picture of mountains on the lid that had mostly rubbed away. Taro carried it in the front pocket of his shorts with the particular care he gave objects he considered important, which was the same as the care he gave all objects, which was not much, but the intent was there.

"What's in it," Kenji said. This was not a question.

"Cards," Taro said. "And other things."

He would not be more specific about the other things until they reached the spot, which Kenji had learned was how Taro operated with objects he considered significant. The significance required the right location.

Shin, who was walking behind them reading a page he'd torn from a field guide and folded into his shirt pocket, said: "If it has the dragonfly cards in it I want to look at them."

"It has better things than dragonfly cards," Taro said.

Shin folded the field guide page and put it back in his pocket.

---

At the widening Taro sat cross-legged on the channel floor and opened the tin with the ceremony of someone presenting evidence. He spread the cards in a fan on his knee: anime characters, a baseball player whose name Kenji didn't know, a sumo wrestler, two tokusatsu heroes Taro would defend loudly if anyone questioned their inclusion, and at the bottom, slightly apart from the others, a card that was older than the rest. Not damaged — just different. The image was a mountain, rendered in a graphic style from some earlier decade, painted rather than printed, and the mountain was familiar in the way that things you've always seen are familiar: you know the shape without having learned it.

Shin reached over and picked it up.

He looked at it for a moment. "That's Tsukuba," he said. "From the south."

Taro, who had been waiting for this: "I know."

"Where did you get it."

"A shop." Taro's tone indicated that the shop's location was irrelevant to the card's quality. "In Tokyo. With Okasan."

Shin turned it over. The back was plain. He turned it face-forward again and looked at the mountain on it and then set it down carefully on the edge of the tin.

Kenji, who had not looked at the card, was examining the flat playing stone — a piece of granite that sat, naturally or near enough, at the base of the east bank platform, angled with one end slightly elevated. He crouched and pressed it with both hands to test the stability, found it satisfactory in some places and not in others, and began looking for shims.

"This needs levelling," he said.

"It's fine," Taro said.

"The north edge is high."

"That's just how granite is."

Kenji picked up a smaller stone, approximately the right thickness, and wedged it under the low end. He pressed the playing surface again. Better. He looked around for another candidate.

"You'll change the physics," Taro said. This was not a technical objection. Taro had a theory about the channel's air movement that he had explained twice and that neither of the others had accepted.

"The physics will survive," Kenji said.

---

They played three rounds on the levelled stone. The surface was not perfect — Kenji had found two shims but the granite had a slight crown that no shim would correct — but it was better, and Taro won two of the three rounds in ways that suggested the irregularity might genuinely be working in his favor, which Kenji declined to investigate further.

On the second round Shin played instead of watching. He lost badly and without obvious concern. His throw was too careful, the card released too deliberately, so that it reached the target with no residual air movement behind it. He noted this, adjusted on the third throw, and lost less badly. He could see the physics of it. He was not sure the physics could be made to reliably produce the outcome, which seemed to be the whole point of menko, but he was willing to consider the question.

While they played, the channel maintained its sound — water cold and continuous somewhere just above them in the bank, the bamboo ticking in the air movement, the miscanthus dry and rustling at the west bank margin. A frog that had been present in the same spot on all three visits was present again, tucked under the overhang of a protruding root. Shin had looked it up: a *kajika* frog, unusual this far from its typical mountain stream habitat, and a second anomaly he had entered in his notebook after the first.

He did not mention the frog being unusual. He wasn't sure it was worth mentioning yet.

---

After the cards Taro declared that he was going to inspect the upper section and set off uphill without waiting. Kenji followed. Shin secured his notebook and shoulder bag on the platform and followed both of them, picking his way along the west bank.

The upper section had a different character from the widening — narrower, the banks steeper, the vegetation pressing in from both sides. The smell changed here. Mineral, and cold, and under the cold something that Shin did not have a word for and had recorded in his notebook as *something else — older* on the first visit and had not improved on since.

The horsetail grew thickest here, at the point where the channel floor changed character — darker soil, damper, the fault margin that Shin had found on the first visit and had since looked up. He crouched at the edge of the stand and picked up one stem.

It came apart in his hands at the joints, clean and precise, each segment separating without resistance. He reassembled it. Came apart again. He did this twice more, not as a game — more like confirming a fact he already knew and wanted to verify physically.

"What's that," Taro said.

"Tokusa." Shin set the stem back at the stand's edge. "It's been here since the Carboniferous."

Taro looked at the stand of horsetail. "*This* has?"

"This species. Essentially unchanged." He considered how to make the number meaningful. "About three hundred million years."

Taro appeared to be working with this. He looked at the horsetail and then at the channel walls and then at his feet. "And we found it," he said. This was Taro's mode of engaging with geological time: personal.

Kenji, who had been listening, said nothing. He was looking at the place where the channel narrowed further above them, the green pressing in from both sides, the path continuing into something that was not yet fully visible.

"It goes further," he said.

"Not today," Shin said. He had the field guide page back in his hand. "Light."

Kenji looked at the sky, calculated, and accepted this.

---

On the way back down, at the widening, Taro crouched beside a stone at the base of the west bank and turned it over.

The beetle was underneath — black, eighteen or nineteen millimeters, motionless for a moment before it oriented and began to move. Taro said: "There's a bug." He was not particularly interested in bugs.

Shin was there in three steps. He crouched with his notebook already open and his pencil in his hand, and he looked at the beetle with the quality of attention he gave to things he couldn't categorize. The pronotal shape was wrong for everything in his guide. The elytra, in the channel's afternoon light, had a faint iridescence he hadn't been able to see in the lower light of the first visit — blue-green, barely present, the kind of color that was gone the moment you were certain you'd seen it.

He drew. The beetle was cooperative for approximately ninety seconds before it found a gap between two stones and was gone. He looked at what he had: better than the first sketch. The iridescence noted in pencil in the margin, with a question mark.

"Did you get it," Taro said, already bored and moving toward the playing stone to retrieve his tin.

"Enough," Shin said. He studied the drawing for a moment, added a line to the pronotal angle, and closed the notebook.

---

On the walk home Taro had theories about the upper section — what was up there, how far the channel went, whether the water source was a spring or something else, and the related question of whether a spring would have fish in it and if so what kind and whether they were catchable. He presented these theories in the running monologue that was his natural conversational mode, not necessarily requiring response, comfortable whether or not one came.

Kenji half-listened. He was thinking about the upper section — the way the channel narrowed, the smell, the sense of the place continuing into something unresolved. He had felt it clearly on the first visit and it had not diminished. If anything it was more present today. He checked his watch out of habit. It was later than he'd thought.

Behind them Shin walked with his notebook under his arm. The second beetle sketch was better. He could see the iridescence now that he'd drawn it. He would look through his father's bookshelves when he got home. Not the field guides — the other books, the older ones, the ones he wasn't technically supposed to take from the study without asking.

He would ask, he decided. He would ask his father whether he had anything on ground beetles.

His father would ask which kind, and Shin would describe it, and his father would say he didn't know much about insects but would look interested anyway, and Shin would feel the small warmth of that without naming it.

The channel would be there tomorrow. The beetle would be under its stone or it wouldn't. Either way, it was in the notebook, drawn from two angles now, with a question mark that was still a question.

He walked home. Above the rooftops of the danchi the mountain was visible in the evening light, the same shape it was on the card in Taro's tin.

He had no particular thought about this. It was just true.
