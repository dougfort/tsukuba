# Nakamura Takashi — 中村隆

## Basic Information

- **Age:** Early-to-mid 40s
- **Role:** Father of Shin
- **Field:** Cognitive science, perception, human mental development
- **Institution:** Tsukuba Science City research community
- **Family:** Nakamura family

## Physical Description

- **Build:** Rounder and softer than Hiroshi, physically expressing his inward orientation
- **Expression:** Receptive stillness — where Shin's stillness is observational, his father's is receptive
- **Distinguishing Features:** Listens with unusual quality, making people feel genuinely heard

## Personality

- Asks questions that make people feel their thoughts matter
- Children tell him things they didn't intend to say
- Shares Shin's quality of stillness but expressed differently — receptive rather than observational
- Professionally interested in what is universally human rather than individually exceptional
- Working at a genuinely exciting and slightly controversial frontier in 1979 Japan

## Relationships

- **Yuriko:** Wife. They share a quality of deep stillness that makes their household notably quiet.
- **Shin:** His most interesting subject without either of them fully knowing it.
- **Mori Hiroshi:** Friend of decades. Each finds the other's field genuinely illuminating. Their complementary fields together cover almost everything Nushi would want to study.

## Background

- Born approximately 1938-1939
- Attended Tokyo University (Todai), graduate studies late 1950s to early 1960s
- Connected with Mori Hiroshi through an informal cross-departmental study group
- Lost touch during early career years, reunited at Tsukuba
- Recruitment to Tsukuba within a year of the Mori family — possibly not coincidental

## Nushi Connection

- His professional skills as a listener will become complicated when the story develops — he may notice changes in Shin before anyone else and be the last to accept what they mean
- His field is precisely what attracted Nushi's attention to humanity — humans beginning to study consciousness itself
- May find the word Nushi troubling when he hears it — not because it's wrong but because it's too right in a way that challenges his scientific framework
- His son reached for an ancient folk concept that fit better than anything cognitive science offers

## Notes

- His field and Mori Hiroshi's together cover almost the complete scope of what Nushi would want to understand
- Whether the two families ending up at Tsukuba together is coincidental is an open story question
