# Part One Scene Map — The Territory
## From the Channel Discovery to the Slope Node Incident

*Part One covers roughly the first year of the boys' relationship with the channel, culminating in the Slope Node incident which ends the Part and is the event the Prologue already described from Nushi's perspective. The reader arrives at the end of Part One having seen the afternoon twice: once from below, once from within.*

*The Prologue (Nushi's perspective, the Slope Node incident) precedes Part One but is not a chapter of it. Part One opens on the boys, before anything has touched them.*

---

## The Structure at a Glance

Part One has three phases:

**Phase 1 — Discovery and Establishment** (First summer): The channel found, claimed, and learned. The boys in their natural state. Nothing strange yet — or almost nothing.

**Phase 2 — The Year-Round Pattern** (First autumn through second spring): The channel as a sustained relationship, not just a summer event. Nushi's attention sharpening without the boys knowing. The first anomalies appearing, too small to name.

**Phase 3 — The Expedition and the Slope Node** (Second summer, the expedition): The boys follow the channel all the way to the mountain. The Slope Node incident. The end of the ordinary world.

---

## Drafted Material

Two scenes already exist and anchor the map:

- **The Prologue** — Nushi's perspective, the Slope Node afternoon. Already drafted and approved. Stands before Part One begins.
- **The Channel Discovery** — Kenji's perspective, the first afternoon in the channel, ending at the widening. Already drafted. This is the opening scene of Part One, Chapter One.

Everything below is what goes between them.

### PROLOGUE (Pre-Part One)
**"The Slope Node" — Nushi's Perspective**
*Already drafted.*

The afternoon of the Slope Node incident, seen from below: Nushi in a period of heightened cognitive activity, three human signals arriving in the concentration zone, the neural resonance. The reader enters the story knowing something is watching. Every subsequent scene is inflected by this knowledge.

*Function: Establishes the dual perspective that is the story's structural asset. Tells the reader what kind of story this is.*

### PART ONE, CHAPTER ONE
**The Channel Discovery**
*Already drafted.*

The gap in the fence. The culvert. The concrete section giving way to the earthen one. The widening with its dry floor, bamboo platform, miscanthus seat. Taro pressing his palms to the earth. Kenji looking at the mountain. Shin opening his notebook.

On the walk home: Taro talking without stopping, Kenji half-listening, Shin noting *taro's hands on earth* and not being sure why.

*Function: Introduces the trio in motion, establishes their dynamic (Kenji goes first, Shin observes and records, Taro provides the chaos and the certainty), and claims the territory. Taro's hands on the earth is the first seed — planted in the notebook before anything has been understood about it.*

### PART ONE, CHAPTER TWO
**The Claiming**
*Suggested new scene.*

The second and third visits, probably within the same week. The boys making the spot theirs through use — the flat playing stone established and leveled by Kenji over two visits; Taro's menko tin arriving on the second or third day; Shin's notebook beginning to accumulate natural history of the channel's specific ecology. The session where Taro wins the flat stone for menko and declares it ideal territory.

In this scene we establish the channel's physical texture properly: the horsetail at the fault margin, the specific dragonflies (the large red ones at the water that Shin notices without comment), the sound of the thin cold current, the way the bamboo light filters and moves. This is also where Shin reads aloud from memory that horsetail (*tokusa*) is essentially unchanged since the Carboniferous — casually, not making a big point of it — and Taro registers this without showing it. He won't forget it.

*From Nushi, felt rather than stated: the channel has been quiet. Three signals in the concentration zone, returning with intention.*

*Function: Establishes the spot's physical and ecological specificity. Plants the horsetail as the oldest living thing any of them has touched — a detail that will land differently in retrospect. Establishes the menko game and the playing stone over the fault substrate.*

---

### PART ONE, CHAPTER THREE
**Beetles**

An evening expedition onto the lower slope above the channel — flashlights, the kunugi oaks, the rhinoceros beetle hunt. This scene takes the boys out of the channel for the first time and establishes the lower slope as known territory. They go further than they meant to. They can see the mountain from here in a way they couldn't before.

Taro captures the rhinoceros beetle that will live on the bedroom windowsill. Kenji captures one too but releases it. Shin, who has no particular desire to keep a beetle in his room, sketches it on the lower slope with his flashlight held between his teeth, in the dark, and gets three good lines before Taro knocks his elbow.

On the way back through the channel: very late, the spot in darkness, just the sound of the water. Kenji pauses at the widening and stands still for a moment before following the others through the culvert.

He doesn't say anything. He just pauses.

*Function: Expands the boys' territory onto the slope — the direction of the eventual expedition. Establishes that the mountain is visible from up there. The pause at the spot in darkness seeds Kenji's relationship to the place as intuitive rather than intellectual.*

---

### CHAPTER FOUR
**Autumn at the Spot**

A single scene anchored in early October — the first time the miscanthus plumes are out, the dragonflies gone, the channel carrying its autumn personality. The after-school pattern is established: they have an hour, they use it. 

Taro has appointed himself the channel's caretaker and is very serious about this. He has made minor improvements — a better arrangement of stones in one section, a bit of clearance on the bamboo bank — that are either genuinely useful or not but which he monitors with proprietorial satisfaction.

The unidentified beetle appears for the first time in this scene — Shin encounters it under a stone near the fault margin and studies it with the particular attention he gives things he cannot name. He draws it twice from different angles. He checks his field guide when he gets home. Not in the guide.

This scene's emotional weight is quiet: these are three boys having an ordinary autumn afternoon in a place they have made theirs, and the ordinariness is worth loving before it begins to change.

*From Nushi: The notebook. The miscanthus in the notebook. The year-round quality of the boys' presence distinguishing them from every prior animal use of this corridor.*

*Function: The turn of the season. Establishes the channel as a year-round relationship. First appearance of the unidentified beetle. Gives the reader something to love before things become strange.*

---

### CHAPTER FIVE
**Winter Visits / Shin's Reading**

This chapter earns a lighter touch — not a single dramatic scene but a sequence of very brief ones or a single winter afternoon, carrying the story through the cold months without lingering.

The spot in December: brief, cold, purposeful. Still dry. Still theirs.

Cut to: Shin in his father's study, which he is permitted to visit when his father is working late. He is not reading his father's research papers. He is reading around them — the books on the shelf that aren't textbooks, older volumes of local history and folklore, a collection of poetry. He finds the Man'yoshu references to Mount Tsukuba almost by accident. He copies two poems into his notebook. He does not know why this feels like the same category as the beetle and the horsetail.

The winter hypnagogic baseline is felt here without being named: both boys sleep well that winter. Their dreams are vivid in a way they have not thought to mention to anyone.

*Function: Advances time efficiently. Plants Shin's Man'yoshu research as something that found him rather than something he sought. Establishes the hypnagogic thread without dramatising it yet — just the fact of unusually vivid dreams, held privately by each boy.*

---

### CHAPTER SIX
**The Mountain Card**

Second spring. The channel comes back: the frogs return, the weasel kits, the first dragonfly. The boys return with the particular energy of people arriving at a place they have been missing.

In this chapter, Taro produces the mountain card. The scene where he takes it from his tin and Shin examines it — identifying it as probably Tsukuba from the south, in an older graphic style — is brief and apparently inconsequential. Taro puts it back. They play menko. It loses.

The scene's hidden content: Taro found this card in a secondhand shop and wanted it immediately and cannot explain why. He has never mentioned this to anyone. He keeps it at the bottom of the tin with the slightly bent corner, under the anime characters and the baseball players. He is aware that it is different from the other cards without knowing what to do with that awareness.

*Nushi registers the menko sessions on the playing stone — impact through substrate, the rhythm of throw/result/throw/result — as a pattern that activates something old in the network. The channel has a memory of exactly this kind of rhythmic call-and-response.*

*Function: Introduces the mountain card quietly, without significance — so that when it begins to behave oddly, the reader remembers it was just a card in a tin.*

---

### CHAPTER SEVEN
**Nushi Makes the Channel Pleasant**

Still second spring. An after-school session where the boys arrive to find the channel unreasonably good — the spot dry after two days of rain that soaked everything else, the path through passable where last year it was overgrown by now, the air at the widening warm in a way that has no obvious source.

Taro notices this first and takes full personal credit. He provides a detailed account of why his terrain-assessment skills are unusually advanced for his age. Kenji says nothing. Shin writes a note in his notebook: *the spot is dryer than it should be after rain.* He reads this back, frowns, and isn't sure why he wrote it as if it were a fact rather than a lucky coincidence.

This scene can be warm and funny — Taro in full self-congratulatory mode is good material. What it plants, invisibly, is the first sense that the spot is responsive to the boys' presence in ways that haven't needed explaining yet.

*Function: Nushi's first deliberate act. Funny on the surface (Taro); quietly strange underneath (Shin's notebook entry). Begins the reader's awareness that the channel is looking after the boys without the boys knowing it.*

---

### CHAPTER EIGHT
**Further**

Second summer, early. The boys begin following the channel higher than they have gone before. This is its own momentum — each summer they have gone a little further; this summer the summit of the channel is the obvious destination.

This chapter covers two or three exploratory sessions, shorter episodes that advance the trajectory toward the Slope Node without arriving yet. The channel narrows and changes character in the upper section. It becomes less a boys' territory and more a geological one — the horsetail denser, the fault-zone smell stronger, the bamboo giving way to a different vegetation. The air is colder even on a hot day.

On one of these sessions, Taro asks out of nowhere: *how old is this place?* Shin says: *the channel was made for farming, but the fault is much older.* Taro: *how much older?* Shin considers. *Millions of years. Before there were people.* Taro looks at the ground under his feet for a long moment. Then: *huh.*

*Nushi registers their approach — the three signals moving further up the corridor with each session, the same incremental curiosity that has characterized all their engagement with the channel.*

*Function: Builds the sense of approach without arriving. Taro's question — how old is this place — plants a thought the reader can hold going into the Slope Node scene. The geological age becomes real, briefly, for all of them.*

---

### CHAPTER NINE
**The Slope Node**
*(Mirror of the Prologue — the same afternoon, now from the boys' side.)*

The expedition up the channel in full. A long chapter, or a scene with real room. Kenji leading, Shin following, Taro refusing to be left. The channel becoming the mountain's terrain. The moment of arrival at the Node — no announcement, just the way the air changes.

What the boys experience: the feeling of somewhere that has not been organized by anyone's expectations. The subsonic quality in the ground. Taro's cap stirring without wind. The simultaneous desire to leave and the inability to. They stay longer than they meant to. The mountain visible above them in a different way than it was visible from the channel floor.

The home walk is very quiet. Even Taro, unusually, does not narrate.

That night: the first vivid dreams. Each boy alone in his separate room. None of them mentions it in the morning.

The chapter ends on that — three separate boys waking at separate tables eating separate breakfasts, each carrying something they have no words for, each deciding without deciding not to speak.

*The reader, who has read the Prologue, knows what just happened. The boys do not. That gap is the story.*

*Function: Part One's climax. The mirror of the Prologue. The end of the ordinary world. Leaves the reader in the same position as the boys: having experienced something real that has no name yet.*

---

## What Part One Establishes

By the end of Chapter Nine the reader has:

- The three boys as fully realized individuals, seen through action rather than description
- The spot as a loved and specific place — its sounds, smells, ecology, seasonal character
- Nushi's presence felt as a texture (the dry floor, the channel's unusually pleasant quality) before any anomaly is named
- The seeds of every significant element — Shin's notebook, Taro's hands on the earth, the mountain card, the horsetail, the unidentified beetle, the Man'yoshu poems, the cap that stirs
- The double perspective: the reader knows what the boys don't

---

## What Part One Does Not Do

It does not explain anything. It does not name Nushi. It does not produce a single clear anomaly that the boys cannot dismiss. It earns the strangeness by making the ordinary world worth living in first.

The mountain card does not behave oddly yet. The hypnagogic contact does not begin yet. The cap stirs at the Slope Node but can be attributed to wind. The notebook holds observations, not questions.

Everything that will happen in Part Two has been planted. None of it has germinated.

---

## Structural Notes

**Chapter count:** Nine chapters (including the Prologue) keeps Part One lean. If the beetle expedition and the first autumn scene feel light, they could be combined — seven chapters works too.

**The Prologue's position:** Stands alone before Chapter One, untitled or titled differently (perhaps just a date — *Summer, Year Two* — to match the epilogue's symmetry). This is an open question in `story_structure.md`.

**Nushi's presence in Part One:** Felt but not narrated. The Prologue is the only direct Nushi perspective in Part One. After that, Nushi is present through effects: the dry floor, the channel's hospitableness, the dream quality, the attention. The reader who remembers the Prologue will feel the watching in every scene. No second Nushi section is needed until Part Two.

**The notebook:** Appears in nearly every chapter but in its natural-history register. By Part One's end it contains beetles, dragonflies, horsetail, the channel's seasonal record, two Man'yoshu poems, and the note about *taro's hands on earth.* The entry that doesn't belong yet. That entry is already there.
