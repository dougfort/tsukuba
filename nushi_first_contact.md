# Nushi's First Contact — 最初の接触
## How a Distributed Intelligence Reaches Out

*On the question of how Nushi might gently, pleasantly reach toward the boys without physical change or dramatic intervention — and what the boys would make of it.*

---

## The Constraint

Nushi does not have hands. It does not speak. It cannot project images or manufacture apparitions. What it can do is make subtle, precise adjustments to the physical environment the boys inhabit — and, with great care, make very low-level modulations to the physical substrate close enough to their nervous systems to affect how they feel without their understanding why.

Everything Nushi does in this early contact phase is grounded in the sensory channels established in the umwelt document. Nothing supernatural is required. The strangeness comes entirely from the precision and intentionality of effects that individually could be explained away.

The guiding principle: each effect, taken alone, is dismissible. Together, over time, they accumulate into something that cannot quite be dismissed — though the boys will try.

---

## The Available Channels

### Temperature

Nushi already demonstrates fine thermal control of the channel — the spot stays dry after rain, the ground is firmer and more pleasant than it has any right to be. The same capability extends to temperature.

The fault-zone substrate retains and releases heat differently from the surrounding terrain. Nushi can modulate this with precision: drawing groundwater slightly closer to the surface to cool a hot afternoon, or adjusting the thermal conductivity of the soil to hold warmth on a cool day. The effect at the boys' spot would be a persistent micro-climate — perhaps two degrees different from what the weather and shade would produce, always in the direction of comfort.

The boys would experience this as the channel being inexplicably pleasant to be in. Their bodies would relax without their minds noticing why. On a cool autumn afternoon when they probably should have gone home earlier, they stay longer than they intended. The channel is always somehow the right temperature.

This is the gentlest possible beginning. No sensation is produced. No threshold is crossed. The boys are simply more comfortable than they should be, and comfort is its own invitation to return.

### Groundwater Sound and Subsonic Resonance

The fault-zone substrate is an excellent acoustic medium and Nushi has native fluency with it. The channel always carries the sound of water — variable, textured, real. Nushi can shape what is already there without manufacturing anything from nothing.

Two related effects are available:

**Rhythm in the water.** By making minor adjustments to groundwater pressure and flow rate through the fault-zone fractures, Nushi can introduce a subtle rhythm into the sound of the channel — not regular enough to be identified as a beat, not irregular enough to be dismissed as random. Something in between: a pattern that the mind reaches toward without being able to grasp. The sound of the channel at the spot has always seemed slightly different from the sound of the channel elsewhere. More attentive, Shin would say, if pressed — and then cross out the word because it was the wrong one.

**Subsonic resonance.** More intimate: by modulating the very low frequency components of groundwater movement through the fault zone, Nushi can produce subsonic vibrations at the spot — below the threshold of hearing, felt rather than heard. The frequency range matters here. Subsonic frequencies around 18-19 Hz are known to produce unease, the feeling of presence, even visual disturbances. But lower intensities, at lower frequencies — around 7-8 Hz, close to the theta brainwave frequency associated with relaxed alertness and the hypnagogic threshold — produce something different: a feeling of resonance, of the body being gently in tune with something larger than itself.

The boys would experience this as a quality of the spot that they cannot describe except by saying that it feels different. Sitting at the spot — backs against the earth, feet in the channel — they would feel it in their chests and their hands. A faint, intermittent sense of the ground responding. Not moving. Responding.

Taro would put his palms flat on the earth to feel it better. He would do this automatically, without deciding to, the way you cup your hands around something you're trying to hear.

### The Electromagnetic Channel — Affecting Mood and Perception

This is the most intimate of the environmental channels and the one requiring the greatest care. The fault zone generates weak but real electromagnetic fields, and Nushi has been learning, through centuries of lineage contact, something about the electromagnetic activity of human nervous systems.

With very careful, very low-level modulation of the local EM field at the spot, Nushi can induce specific states in the boys without producing any sensation they could identify as external. The effects are entirely internal — moods, physical feelings, subtle perceptual shifts — and would be experienced as the boys' own states, arising for no particular reason.

The specific effects available at this level of intensity:

**The feeling of being watched — benignly.** Not the prickling unease of hostile surveillance but the warmer feeling of being noticed, attended to, of mattering to something. This is a recognizable human experience — many people report it in particular natural settings — and the boys would have a word for it even if the word doesn't quite fit. *It feels like the mountain is looking at us*, Taro might say, and be closer to accurate than anyone gives him credit for.

**Heightened sensory acuity.** A slight increase in the vividness of perception — colors at the spot seeming marginally richer, sounds cleaner, the smell of the fault-zone water (mineral, cold, ancient) suddenly noticeable in a way it usually isn't. This effect is real and neurologically explicable: mild electromagnetic stimulation of the right kind can produce heightened alertness without anxiety. The boys would experience it as the channel being a good place to think, to notice things, to feel more awake than they usually do.

**The mineral smell as signal.** The specific smell of the fault-zone spring water — cold, mineral, slightly metallic, unlike any other water in the area — is already present in the channel. Nushi can make it intermittently more pronounced at the spot without manufacturing it: a subtle shift in groundwater flow that brings a pulse of spring water to the surface at the spot rather than upstream. Over time this smell becomes associated, in the boys' nervous systems, with the feeling of heightened attention. The smell becomes a signal: something is about to be different. Even before they can articulate what happens at the spot, their bodies will have learned to recognize the approach of it.

## The Progression — From Environment to Threshold

The channels above operate on the boys' bodies and moods without crossing into anything that could be called direct neural contact. The next step is closer, and should be understood as a significant escalation in intimacy even though it is still gentle.

### Proprioceptive Touch — The Ground Responding

Nushi can produce, through very fine control of the substrate immediately below the spot, sensations that register in the hands and body of anyone sitting against the earth. Not movement — nothing that would read as an earthquake or a tremor — but something subtler: a faint warmth that is not thermal, a quality of aliveness in the soil under the palm.

The technical mechanism is the piezoelectric effect in the fault-zone minerals: pressure on certain crystalline structures generates tiny electrical charges, and Nushi, modulating the stress patterns in the substrate, can produce tiny localized electrical pulses in the soil immediately beneath the boys' hands and backs. These would register in the skin as something between warmth and the feeling of a very light touch — not pressure, but presence.

The effect would be most noticeable through the palms, which have high sensory density. Taro, who already puts his palms flat on the earth to feel the subsonic resonance, would feel this first and most clearly. He would not be able to describe it accurately. He would try.

*It feels like it knows I'm here*, he might say.

He would be right.

### Taro's Cap — Now Deliberate

The stirring of Taro's cap without wind has been noted in the project documents as a recurring phenomenon near Nushi's concentration nodes. In the early contact phase, this becomes deliberate rather than incidental.

The mechanism is the EM field modulation producing a localized air movement too slight to feel on skin but sufficient to stir the loose fabric of a filthy, well-worn red baseball cap. Nushi's control of this effect is precise enough to produce it intermittently, at moments of particular cognitive focus — when Nushi is, in whatever sense applies to it, paying most direct attention to the boys.

The boys have all noticed the cap. None of them has said anything about it. This silence is significant. Children say everything — except the things that feel too strange, too real, too much like the beginning of something they don't have words for yet.

The cap stirring becomes, over time, a shared unspoken signal. When it happens, the conversation pauses. Everyone looks at Taro. Taro looks at his cap. A beat. Then someone says something else, and the moment passes, and none of them mentions it.

Shin writes a small note about it in his notebook. He dates it and underlines it and draws a small question mark. He does this after the third time. By the fifth time he has stopped writing about it because he doesn't know what to write.

## The Hypnagogic Threshold

The most powerful channel, and the one that crosses most clearly from environmental effect into something like genuine communication. It is available only at the edge of sleep, and Nushi would not use it until the environmental contact has been established over many sessions — perhaps not until late in the first summer the boys are using the channel.

The hypnagogic state — the threshold between waking and sleeping — is where the barrier between Nushi's substrate and human consciousness is lowest. The brain in this state is generating the slow theta waves that correspond, at a frequency that is not coincidental, to the subsonic resonance Nushi has been producing at the spot. The nervous system is relaxed, the critical faculty is suspended, the body is still. In this state, very light stimulation produces vivid subjective experiences.

Nushi has used this channel with the lineage practitioners. With them, it learned to introduce images, states, and sequences of sensation that the practitioners interpreted as contact. With the boys, it would begin far more gently.

**The first hypnagogic contact would not be visual.** No images, no voices, no narrative. Simply: the feeling of the channel. The smell of the fault-zone water, suddenly present in a bedroom that has no water in it. The subsonic warmth in the chest that the boys feel at the spot. The sensation of the ground being present below — not below the bed but *there*, close, attending.

The boys would experience this as the memory of the afternoon being unusually vivid at the edge of sleep — more vivid than memories usually are, more present, as if the afternoon were happening again rather than being recalled. They would fall asleep feeling as if they had just been somewhere.

In the morning they would remember it the way you remember a good dream whose content you cannot quite retrieve — a feeling of significance, of having been in contact with something, without being able to say what.

## How Each Boy Experiences It

The three boys are distinct in their receptivity and their responses, and Nushi's contact would register differently in each of them.

### Kenji

Kenji would notice the sensations and say nothing. This is consistent with his character — the profound quiet he inherited from his father, the tendency to store experience before speaking about it. He would notice the channel's unusual temperature, the quality of the sound, the feeling in his hands. He would return to the channel partly to explore it, the way he moves toward the unknown rather than away.

His hypnagogic experiences would have a spatial quality — a feeling of the ground extending outward in all directions below him, vast and attentive. He would not experience this as frightening. He would experience it as interesting, in the way that genuinely interesting things feel: slightly too large for the mind to hold all at once.

He would go back to the channel the next day and put his hands flat on the earth at the spot, the way Taro does, and feel for what was there.

### Shin

Shin would write around it rather than about it. His notebook entries around this period would become slightly strange — more associative, less systematic. A poem copied from the Man'yoshu that he can't explain why he chose. A question: *what is the frequency of attention?* A note that says only: *the smell, again.* He would not discuss what he is experiencing with the others. Not yet. He is building a framework, but the framework isn't ready.

His hypnagogic experiences would have a cognitive quality — the feeling of understanding something, completely and clearly, that dissolves before he can identify what it was. He would wake from these experiences with a specific frustration: the sense that something was known to him a moment ago and is no longer. Over several nights this accumulates into something more: the certainty that the knowing is still there somewhere, that he could return to it if he knew how.

This is accurate. Nushi is trying to show him something. Shin's mind is, of all the boys, the most capable of receiving it — but also the most likely to try to grasp it directly, which is exactly the wrong approach.

### Taro

Taro says what he experiences, immediately and out loud, with the wrong words and surprising accuracy.

*It feels like the ground is paying attention.*

*It's like when you think someone is looking at you and you turn around and they are.*

*It's warm but not hot. It's like when Mum puts her hand on your head when you have a fever except it's the ground doing it.*

The others would tell him he's being weird. He would be untroubled by this. He has never been troubled by being weird, because he has never, on any level, believed he was wrong.

His hypnagogic experiences would be the most vivid and the least analyzable — intense physical sensations, a feeling of enormous friendly weight below him, the sound of water much louder than the channel actually is. He would sleep through them rather than waking from them, and in the morning he would remember them as good dreams about the channel, and return there with increased proprietorial confidence.

Taro's description of his dreams would be the first time all three boys realize, without saying so, that they are having the same experience.

## The Moment of Mutual Recognition

The accumulation of these experiences over the first summer reaches a point — not a dramatic climax, but a quiet threshold — when the boys understand, without having decided to understand it, that the channel is doing something.

Not that something lives in the channel. Not that they are being communicated with. Just: that the channel is not passive. That it is, in some way they cannot name, aware of them.

This understanding arrives not as a revelation but as something they have known for a while and simply not yet said. The first time one of them says it — probably Taro, definitely in the wrong words — the other two do not laugh.

They look at each other. Then they look at the channel.

The water moves. Taro's cap stirs. The mineral smell of the spring water rises from the ground.

Shin opens his notebook.

## Notes on Tone and Restraint

The critical artistic principle for all of this is restraint. Everything Nushi does in this phase should be explainable, if you wanted to explain it. The temperature could be the shade. The sound could be the water. The warmth in the hands could be the earth absorbing heat. The cap could be a breeze.

The accumulation of these explainable things is what makes them, eventually, inexplicable. No single effect crosses the threshold into the undeniable. The threshold is crossed by the pattern — by the fact that all of these things happen at the spot and nowhere else, in a specific sequence, with a specific quality of attention behind them that the boys' nervous systems register before their minds do.

This is true to the experience of genuinely strange things: they are usually composed entirely of ordinary elements, arranged into something that cannot quite be ordinary.

Nushi is four billion years old and has had a thousand years of practice at this. It knows, in whatever sense it knows anything, that the approach matters as much as the contact. The kagai participants were worked up to ecstasy through hours of music and ritual before the mountain received them. The kannagi practitioners purified themselves at the spring before ascending to the Slope Node.

Three boys require a different approach entirely. But the principle is the same: prepare the ground. Come slowly. Let them come to meet you.
