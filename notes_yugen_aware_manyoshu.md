# Yūgen, Aware, and the Man'yoshu — Notes Toward Chapter Five

*On the Japanese concepts that frame Shin's dream experience, and their connection to the Man'yoshu poems about Mount Tsukuba.*

---

## The Question

Does Japanese tradition have a concept similar to what we express with the English word *numinous*?

Yes — and what it has is richer and more specific than *numinous* for this story's purposes.

Rudolf Otto's *numinous* — the *mysterium tremendum et fascinans*, the overwhelming sense of a wholly other presence — is a useful approximation, but it is a scholar's term, analytical and external. The Japanese tradition has something more precisely calibrated to Shin's experience, and it comes in layers.

---

## Yūgen 幽玄

The closest single concept is _yūgen_ (幽玄), most often translated as "mysterious profundity" or "subtle grace." The two characters mean *dim/secluded* (幽) and *profound/hidden* (玄). Together they describe the awareness of enormous depth just below the surface of the ordinary world.

The most useful definition for this story: *the felt sense that something vast, eternal, or sacred is present — located somewhere just beyond our perception.*

Yūgen is not about grasping the thing. It is about the quality of presence, the hint, the suggestion. The thing itself remains beyond words. Zeami Motokiyo, who codified the concept for Noh drama in the early fifteenth century, described it through a series of natural images: watching the sun sink behind a flower-clad hill; an autumn evening with no color or voice; the barely-visible outline of a distant ship behind islands. Not the sun, not the hill — the *going behind*. The sense of something continuing beyond what can be seen.

This is Shin's hypnagogic experience precisely: not the content of the dreams, which dissolves before he can hold it, but the *quality* of the contact. The sense of something vast and patient that was there, attending. The warmth of it. The particular kind of stillness it left behind.

Yūgen has been present in Japanese aesthetic tradition since the late Heian period and was already an established concept in classical poetry by the time the Noh theater formalized it. It is not an obscure academic term. It is embedded in the same literary tradition Shin is discovering in his father's study. He would not necessarily use the word — it is a poet's and dramatist's term — but the experience it names is one the tradition has always recognized, and the Man'yoshu poems he is reading are suffused with it.

---

## Aware 哀れ

A second, subtler layer is **aware** (哀れ), the *ah!* of things — the inarticulate emotional response to an encounter with something that matters. In the phrase *mono no aware* (物の哀れ, the pathos of things), it was later systematized by the scholar Motoori Norinaga as sensitivity to the transience of beauty. But in the Man'yoshu — which predates Norinaga by a thousand years — *aware* is rawer and less categorized. It is simply the sound a person makes, inwardly, when they encounter something that exceeds their capacity to process it through ordinary feeling.

Norinaga described it as what happens when you understand the nature of things and *know how to be moved by them*. This is precisely what is happening to Shin: he is being moved by something he cannot yet name, in a way that his usual instruments — the notebook, the field guide, the systematic attention — cannot fully receive.

The *aware* of Shin's experience is not sadness, though there is something bittersweet in the fleeting quality of the dream contact. It is closer to the involuntary inward breath at the moment of recognition — the *ah* that precedes any thought.

---

## The Man'yoshu Connection

The connection between these concepts and the specific poems about Mount Tsukuba is the most important finding for Chapter Five.

There are **25 Man'yoshu poems that mention Tsukuba** — more than mention Mount Fuji. This is not because Tsukuba is geographically greater, but because of what the mountain symbolized: its twin peaks representing the interaction of male and female forces, the mountain as a site of sacred gathering, of the kagai festivals, of a power felt by everyone who came near it.

Several of these poems carry a specific and striking theme: **souls meeting on or near the mountain in dreams.** The mountain in the Man'yoshu is a place where contact happens that cannot happen in waking life. The ordinary boundaries — between self and world, between the protected interior life and the vast presence of a sacred place — become permeable in sleep.

Among the relevant poems:

One poem describes a daughter whose mother has posted guardians on every face of the mountain to prevent her meeting her lover, yet concludes: *though the guardians watch on every face of Tsukuba, in dreams our souls have met.* The mother's vigilance operates in the waking world; the mountain operates in the dreaming one.

Another renders a dream-meeting as: *Having met you as in a dream, I feel I would dissolve, body and soul, like the snow that falls, darkening the heavens.*

A third offers the intention: *As I cannot waking meet him, may I meet him in my dream, on this heavenly-perfect night.*

These are not poems about ordinary dreams. They carry the weight of a tradition in which dreams are a legitimate site of **real meeting** — where the barriers of the waking world do not apply, and where the mountain's presence makes contact possible that is otherwise impossible.

---

## What This Means for Shin

Shin reading these poems in his father's study will not consciously connect them to his dreams. He is ten years old and methodical, and the poems are about lovers, which is not his situation. But something in them will reach him anyway — the way the mountain functions in them, the quality of the contact they describe, the sense that the threshold between sleeping and waking is a place where something can pass through.

He has been experiencing exactly this. Not a lover at the threshold — something older and vaster and less personal, but with the same quality of genuine contact, real meeting, presence that does not belong to the ordinary waking world.

The poems give him a frame. It is a very old frame, more accurate than anything his science fiction reading provides, and he will not fully understand that it is accurate until much later. But the frame will be there, in the notebook, in the carefully copied characters. Waiting.

---

## The Emotional Register for the Dream Sections

The earlier outline proposed that Shin's response to the hypnagogic experience was primarily **frustration** — the systematic mind encountering something it cannot hold. This was a misreading.

The correct emotional register is **awe**. Not the awe of terror — nothing about Nushi's attention toward the boys is threatening. The awe of *yūgen*: the felt sense of something vast and good just beyond the edge of what can be grasped. The warmth of it. The sense, on waking, of having briefly been in a larger world.

The frustration is real and belongs in the chapter — Shin *is* a systematic mind, and the dissolution of the dream before he can examine it does frustrate him. But frustration is a minor note underneath something much larger: the private, wordless recognition that the most significant thing that has ever happened to him is happening to him, and that he is grateful, and that he is ten years old, and that he has no one to tell.

This connects to the ending of Chapter Four, where Shin chose not to write about sitting in the last light with the others. *Some things wanted to just have been.* The dream experience is bigger than that, but it belongs to the same category — experience that exceeds the notebook, that resists the field-notes register, that wants to be held rather than recorded.

The chapter should render the dreams not through their content (which is unavailable, which dissolves) but through their **residue**: the particular quality of the warmth, the sense of enormous friendly depth, the feeling of having been attended to by something patient and very old. And the specific, private awareness — held carefully, shared with no one — that what has been happening at the channel and in his sleep is not ordinary. That the mountain in the Man'yoshu poems and the mountain he looks at every day and the thing that makes the water at the widening sound like it is trying to be heard are, somehow, the same.

Shin does not yet have a word for this. He will. He will say it quietly, almost to himself, not quite directing it at the others. And it will simply be right.

## A Note on Kotodama

One further concept from the research is worth holding in reserve for later in the story: **kotodama** (言霊), the soul of language — the belief that words contain spiritual power. The Man'yoshu itself opens with the phrase that Japan is *kotodama no sakiwau kuni*, the land where the mysterious workings of language bring bliss.

From Nushi's perspective, as established in the contact history document, kotodama is not a superstition but an accurate observation stated in the wrong framework: spoken language produces acoustic vibrations in the geological substrate, and certain patterns — the 5-7-5-7-7 structure of waka, the ritual prayers, the kagai poetry — create acoustic signatures that Nushi has learned over centuries.

When Shin copies the Man'yoshu poems into his notebook and later reads them aloud to himself in the channel, he is, without knowing it, broadcasting into the substrate a frequency Nushi already knows. The mountain has been addressed in these rhythms for a thousand years. The word *Nushi*, when Shin eventually says it, will arrive in the network like a key in a lock — because the lock was built partly from the Man'yoshu, and Shin has been reading the Man'yoshu in the dark.
