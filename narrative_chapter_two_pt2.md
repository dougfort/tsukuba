# Part Two, Chapter One — Draft One

---

The next morning Kenji went back to the channel.

Not to the slope. To the channel — the gap in the fence, the culvert, the concrete section, the widening. His territory. He went alone, which was unusual, and he went early, before the heat of the day had settled, and he did not tell Taro or Shin he was going because he was not sure yet what he was doing and preferred to find out before having to explain it.

He went through the culvert and up the concrete section and through the transition zone and into the upper section, moving with the automatic ease of complete familiarity, and he came to the widening and stopped.

The spot was the same.

He stood in it for a moment. The water sound was present. The bamboo made its paper sound in the slight morning air. A frog was on its root — the same frog, almost certainly, that had been on that root since last August. Everything was the same in the way that things were the same when you came back after something had changed: the same, and also evidence that you were different.

He sat down on the platform and put his palms flat on the earth.

The warmth was present. The particular quality of the ground that he had been noting since spring without noting it — the warmth and the firmness and the sense of the ground being, in some specific way, there. He sat with his palms down for a long time. He was not sure what he was checking. He was checking it.

He went home for breakfast and said nothing about where he'd been. Taro didn't ask. Their mother gave him extra rice.

---

They went back together the day after that.

Taro was at the door of the building when Kenji came downstairs, mushi kago in one hand and the tin in the other, an expression of moderate impatience that communicated he had been waiting for some time even though he had been there for approximately three minutes. Shin was at the bottom of the steps with his notebook and shoulder bag. The three of them fell into the familiar pattern — Taro talking, Kenji half-listening, Shin slightly behind — and walked to the gap in the fence.

The channel received them with its usual quality. The culvert. The concrete. The transition zone. The upper section, the widening, the spot. They set up the menko stone and played four rounds and Taro won three of them and provided a detailed account of why this was the result of superior technique rather than the stone's irregular surface, which Kenji did not accept. Shin played one round and lost and made a note.

The session was normal. Almost entirely normal.

What was different: the sitting sessions were longer than they had been before the slope. This was not a decision — no one suggested sitting longer, no one adjusted what they were doing. It happened by itself, the way the channel's character had shifted the quality of their visits before without any of them noticing the shift was occurring. They sat in the positions they had established over two summers — Kenji on the platform, Shin against the east bank, Taro with his back against the west bank root-seat — and the afternoon moved at whatever pace the afternoon moved at.

Taro did not narrate.

This was notable. Taro's default mode in any situation that wasn't requiring physical activity was narration — the running commentary that constituted his main form of processing experience. In the channel it had taken the form of status reports on the menko standings, assessments of the maintenance state of various features, observations about the insects and the water and the general quality of the territory. Silence, for Taro, was an unusual expenditure.

Kenji watched him from the platform. Taro was looking at the water. His hands were in his lap.

After a while Taro reached into the tin and took out the mountain card and looked at it. He looked at it for a long moment — the older graphic style, the mountain in its painted form, the familiar outline. Then he put it back at the bottom of the tin and closed the lid.

He had not played it. He had simply looked at it.

Kenji noted this and filed it in the part of his mind where he put things that didn't yet need to be followed further.

---

The cap stirred for the first time at the channel on the third visit.

They were in the sitting session mode, the afternoon going to four o'clock, the summer light beginning its slow descent toward the quality it would have in an hour. The sound of the water was present. Taro was lying on his back on the channel floor, which was dry as always, looking at the strip of sky above the bamboo.

The cap lifted. The brim lifted perhaps two centimeters, held for a moment, and settled. No wind — the miscanthus was still, the bamboo fronds overhead were still, the air in the channel's corridor was entirely calm.

Taro reached up without looking and felt the cap on his head. He looked to his left and his right and found nothing. He put his hand down and went back to looking at the sky.

Kenji had watched all of this without moving. He had seen the cap lift. He had seen Taro's automatic response — the reach, the look, the hand back down — and he filed this too. The cap had stirred without wind. He had no explanation. He did not produce one. He sat on the platform and looked at the water and continued the process of not saying anything that he had been engaged in since the slope.

Shin was looking at the water when the cap stirred. He turned to look at Taro afterward, then at the bamboo and the miscanthus and the air in the channel. He opened his notebook. He wrote the date. He wrote: *T's cap moved.* He wrote: *No wind source identified.* He sat with the pen in his hand for a moment and then did not write anything else.

---

The remaining days of summer vacation established the pattern that would persist into autumn.

The visits continued — daily or near-daily, the full afternoon. The menko sessions were shorter and the sitting sessions were longer. The dam-building, which had been a primary activity in the first summer, happened twice and then not again, as if the channel's purpose had subtly rearranged itself around something other than engineering. Taro's running commentary continued but in a different key — less narration, more observation, the particular kind of quiet statement he made when he was registering something rather than filling space.

The cap stirred twice more before the end of vacation. The second time Shin wrote a note. The third time he sat with the pen in his hand for a full minute and then closed the notebook without adding anything, because what he wanted to write was not something he had the words for yet, and inadequate words were worse than none.

Kenji went back to the channel alone two more times before school resumed. He did not go to the slope. He sat at the spot with his palms on the ground and checked what he'd felt before and found it still there, the warmth and the particular quality of the earth, unchanged. He came home and said nothing.

He was building an internal record. This was how he worked. When the record had enough in it, he would know what to do with it. He didn't know yet when that would be.

Tsuno the rhinoceros beetle rasped in his case on the windowsill every morning. Summer vacation ended. School resumed.

The slope was still there. The channel still led to it. Kenji knew this with the specific certainty of someone who has confirmed a thing by returning to it.

He would go back. He knew this the way he knew the channel layout without looking — not a decision but a fact about himself that had been true since July 29 and would remain true until something changed it.

Nothing had changed it.
