# Part One, Chapter Six — Draft One

---

The frogs came back on a Tuesday in early April, which Taro took as a personal welcome.

This was not something he said aloud. It was something he felt, standing at the spot after the first proper after-school visit of the second spring, listening to the sound that had been absent since October and was now present again — full and immediate, as if the frogs had simply resumed a conversation that had been interrupted rather than ended. The channel had been waiting. The frogs had come back. He had come back. These things were connected.

He crouched and pressed his palms to the ground at the widening. The earth was cold but not unpleasant — the deep cold of something that had been keeping itself, not the dead cold of something abandoned. Under his hands the ground was firm and slightly damp, the mineral smell strong after the winter, the base flow audible somewhere above him in the bank. The channel was exactly as he'd left it.

This was important. The channel being exactly as he'd left it was not something he'd been certain about through the long months of weekend visits and cold air and the general unsatisfactoriness of winter. He had come back on eight Saturdays between December and March, brief and cold, more checks than visits, confirming each time that the spot was still there and still dry and still theirs. But it had not felt fully present in winter the way it felt present now, the frogs resuming their conversation and the weasel sign fresh in the east bank and the bamboo beginning to put out its new growth above the old.

"The seat survived," he reported.

Shin, who was crouched at the upper margin looking at the horsetail emerging from the fault-zone substrate, said: "Of course it survived."

"I wasn't sure about the left section. I made a note of it." He hadn't made a note. He had thought about it several times over the winter, which was the same thing.

"It's miscanthus root," Shin said. "It has survived approximately three hundred million years in this fault zone. It will survive one winter."

Taro looked at the seat. This was a fair point. He sat down on it to confirm it was satisfactory, which it was, and settled in with the proprietary ease of someone whose territory had been returned to them in good order.

---

Kenji arrived six minutes after them, which was faster than his usual after-school pace, and dropped into the channel without breaking stride. He walked the length of the upper section before coming back to the spot — the reconnaissance walk he did at the beginning of any visit after a significant gap, relearning the channel's current state. Taro watched him without appearing to watch him. Kenji's reconnaissance walks had a quality he couldn't describe — not systematic like Shin's note-taking, not casual like Taro's own movement through a place. Something between the two. As if he were checking the channel against an internal record of it, updating the record where the channel had changed.

"Higher than last autumn," Kenji said, about the base flow. He had come back to the spot and was looking at the water.

"April snowmelt," Shin said, without looking up from the horsetail.

Kenji crouched and put his fingers in the flow. Cold — Taro could tell from the expression, which was the expression of someone confirming something rather than discovering it. He wiped his hand on his shorts and stood up.

"Good," he said.

Taro agreed completely, though they were not talking about the same things.

---

He produced the tin on the second visit, a Thursday after school, the light still generous enough at five o'clock to play properly. He had been carrying it in his randoseru since April began on the theory that the channel visits would resume and the tin should be available when they did. He set it on the playing stone — the flat granite that Kenji had levelled last summer with the shims — and opened it.

The cards were as he'd left them. He fanned them on his knee: the tokusatsu heroes, the baseball player, the sumo wrestler, the anime characters from three different series arranged by his own private taxonomy, and at the bottom, kept slightly apart from the others by a habit he had never examined, the mountain card.

He did not take it out first. He never took it out first. He started with the cards he played regularly — the tokusatsu heroes were his best performers on this surface, something about their weight relative to their size — and let the mountain card sit at the bottom of the fan, face against his knee, while he played.

He won four rounds and lost two. The losses were to Kenji, who had no particular interest in menko but was unreasonably competent at it in the way he was unreasonably competent at things he didn't try to be good at. Shin played one round and lost and made a note about the trajectory angle that Taro didn't find useful. Shin's understanding of menko was entirely theoretical. The theory was correct. This did not help.

After the sixth round Taro took the mountain card from the bottom of the tin and placed it on the stone.

The card sat there. It was the same card it had always been — the mountain in its older graphic style, the painted quality that made it different from the printed cards around it, the peak with its slight suggestion of cloud. He had looked at it so many times that he had stopped seeing it freshly and had begun to see it as itself, which was different.

He played it against Kenji's tokusatsu hero.

It lost. It flipped facedown in the way that losing cards flipped, which was clean and definitive, the mountain hidden, the plain cardboard back facing up.

He turned it faceup again with one finger and put it back at the bottom of the tin.

This was how the mountain card always went. He noted this without following it further. There was something about the card that he held in the back of his attention without bringing it forward, the same way he held the warmth of the spot and the rhythm in the water sound — things that were true about this place that he had not yet found the right occasion to think about directly.

He closed the tin. He would think about them when it was time.

---

The visit two weeks later was after rain.

Taro knew it as soon as he dropped into the channel below the culvert — the concrete section was wet and smelled of recent water, the channel floor dark and slick where it was usually dry. Expected. He had expected this. He had thought about it on the walk from school, the question of whether the spot would be passable or whether they'd have to stay in the concrete section, which was nobody's first choice.

He moved uphill through the transition zone, which was wetter than usual and required some care. Shin was behind him, picking his way. Kenji was ahead — he'd gone through first, of course, Kenji always went through first — and was already at the upper section by the sound of it.

The spot was dry.

Not just passable. Not merely less wet than it should have been. Dry — the floor of the widening firm and dark and holding no water, the way it was on an ordinary day after no rain at all, as if the two days of steady rain that had soaked the danchi gardens and the school courtyard and the concrete section eight meters below had arrived at the widening and decided not to stay.

Taro stood in it and looked at the floor and looked at the section immediately above the spot, which was muddy, and the section immediately below, which was wet. He pressed his palm to the ground. The earth was damp a centimeter down, mineral-cold, but the surface was dry.

"Hm," he said.

He stood up. He looked at the spot with the expression he wore when something had confirmed what he already knew without his having known he knew it.

Kenji was sitting on the platform looking at the upper channel.

Shin had his notebook out. He wrote something, looked at it, and wrote something else.

"Good place," Taro said. He said it in the tone of a man surveying his estate.

"It's the drainage," Kenji said, not looking away from the upper channel.

"It's a good place," Taro said again, because this and the drainage were not mutually exclusive, and because the first statement was more important than the second.

Shin wrote something else in his notebook. He had the slight frown he wore when he was writing something he wasn't sure about.

Later, on the walk home, Taro thought about the dry floor and the wet sections above and below it, and about how this had been true since the first summer — the spot always drier than the rest, always more hospitable, always as if the terrain had arranged itself with them specifically in mind. He had thought this before. He had not thought it in quite these words before.

He thought: *this is a place that knows we're here.*

He thought this clearly and completely and then walked on without examining it, because Taro's relationship to significant thoughts was to have them and let them settle at whatever level they settled at. The right time to think about them would come. It always did.

Shin had written: *spot dry after two days of rain. Sections immediately above and below: wet. Differential marked. Not easily explained by drainage gradient.* 

He had looked at this for a moment and then written below it: *third time this has been notable.*

He was not sure why he had written *notable* rather than *unexplained* or *anomalous*, which were the words his natural history register would have produced. *Notable* was a different kind of word. It implied that someone was doing the noting, and that the thing being noted was being noted *for a reason* — that it was worth attention beyond the ordinary attention he gave to channel observations.

He capped his pen and walked on. He did not cross out *notable.*

The mountain was above the rooflines, visible in the late afternoon light, the same shape it was on the card in the tin in his randoseru on Taro's back three meters ahead of him. He had not mentioned this to anyone. He did not mention it now.
